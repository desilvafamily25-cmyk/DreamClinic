## CATEGORY 11 — WORK HEALTH AND SAFETY / WORKSAFE / INCIDENTS

### What Has to Be Done

- **WHS Risk Register maintenance:** Conduct annual comprehensive hazard identification workshop with clinical and administrative staff. Document all hazards (bloodborne pathogen exposure, sharps injuries, chemical exposure, manual handling, psychological stress, environmental hazards), assign risk ratings (likelihood × consequence), identify control measures (engineering, administrative, PPE), document responsible owner and review date. Update register quarterly or when new hazards emerge.

- **Hazard identification and reporting:** Establish open reporting culture where any staff member can flag hazards via form or direct to WHS representative. Ensure no-blame environment for hazard reporting. Conduct monthly walkthrough inspection of clinical areas, staff rooms, storage areas, outdoor emergency zones. Document findings on standardised hazard report form with date, location, description, proposed control, and sign-off.

- **Incident and near-miss register:** Log all work-related injuries, near-misses, and property damage within 24 hours. Register must contain: incident date/time, person(s) involved, location, description of event, injuries sustained, immediate actions taken, witness names, investigating officer, root cause analysis, corrective actions, and closure date. Distinguish between lost-time injury (LTI), medical treatment injury (MTI), and near-miss for WorkCover reporting purposes.

- **WorkSafe Victoria notification (serious injuries within 1 hour):** Under Section 38 OHS Act 2004 (Vic), notify WorkSafe immediately (by phone 1300 136 089) if a worker is killed, suffers a serious injury (likely to require medical treatment involving overnight hospitalisation, or involves loss of consciousness, or permanent loss of vision/hearing, or loss of limb/body function), or suffers a serious illness. Follow up written notification within 48 hours. Retain notification records and WorkSafe reference numbers. Clinic manager or WHS representative must be trained to recognise reportable injuries.

- **Return-to-work (RTW) coordination:** Appoint designated RTW coordinator if clinic has >20 employees (per WorkCover Victoria requirements) or as insurer specifies. Coordinator manages communication between injured worker, treating medical practitioners, insurer, and clinic management. Develop RTW plan within 5 working days of employee notifying injury. Document graduated duties arrangement, modified hours, equipment modifications, and expected duration. Review RTW plan fortnightly until full duties resumed. Maintain confidentiality of medical information.

- **WHS Committee establishment if >20 employees:** If clinic employs >20 staff, establish WHS Committee with worker and management representatives (minimum 1:1 ratio plus management chair). Committee meets monthly (documented minutes), reviews incident trends, endorses risk register updates, approves new safety procedures, monitors regulatory compliance, and conducts tool-box talks. If <20 employees, nominate WHS representative with dedicated time allocation (suggest minimum 0.5 day/week protected time).

- **Emergency evacuation procedures and fire drills:** Document evacuation procedure (assembly point location, headcount procedure, emergency contact tree). Display evacuation maps in every clinical and administrative area showing exits, fire extinguisher locations, first aid kit locations. Conduct unannounced fire drill every 6 months, document participation and any issues identified. Ensure all staff know procedure and assembly point. Maintain fire extinguisher servicing records (annual certificate) and emergency lighting/exit sign testing records.

- **Sharps safety and needlestick injury protocol:** Implement safe sharps handling: use only approved sharps containers (yellow lidded containers, compliant with AS/NZS 4261), locate near point-of-use in all clinical areas, label with biohazard symbol, replace when 2/3 full (never overstuff), arrange disposal via approved clinical waste contractor (Stericycle, Daniels, or Enviro). Train all staff in safe sharps handling: no recap, no bending, immediate placement in container, one-handed scoop technique where necessary. For needlestick injury: immediate first aid (encourage gentle bleeding from wound, wash with soap and water, dry and cover), complete incident report, source patient testing (within 72 hours), baseline testing of injured worker, follow-up testing at 6 weeks and 3 months if source patient positive, post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) consideration if high risk, referral to occupational health service (e.g., Occupational Medicine Victoria), notification to WorkCover.

- **Manual handling assessments:** Identify all manual handling tasks in clinic (lifting patient examination chairs, moving equipment, stocking supplies, moving patient records, moving clinical waste bins, handling steriliser loads). Assess each task using risk matrix (force required, frequency, distance, awkward posture). For high-risk tasks, implement engineering controls (trolleys, lifting devices, height-adjustable furniture), administrative controls (job rotation, break scheduling), and training. Document assessment with responsible person, date, and review trigger. Annual reassessment minimum.

- **Personal protective equipment (PPE) provision:** Stock and issue PPE based on risk assessment: gloves (nitrile latex-free in multiple sizes), clinical gowns, eye protection (safety glasses or face shields per task), surgical masks, N95 respirators for aerosol-generating procedures (AGP). Ensure correct fit-testing for N95 masks (fit-test certificate required annually for users). Educate staff on correct donning/doffing sequence, storage, and disposal. Maintain stock par levels with monthly stocktake. Document PPE training in personnel files.

- **WorkCover insurance premium management:** Ensure clinic holds current WorkCover insurance policy (mandatory for all Victorian employers — premium based on payroll and industry classification code: typically 6211 for medical practitioners). Review premium quote annually to ensure correct payroll reporting and classification. Provide accurate end-of-year payroll declaration to insurer by 30 June (ideally 15 June). Monitor claims history and implement preventive measures to reduce future claims (impacts next year's premium). Maintain certificate of currency for landlord and accreditation bodies.

- **Annual WHS inspection:** Commission independent external WHS professional to conduct annual full-site audit (covers hazard identification, risk assessment, control measures, incident management, staff competency, regulatory compliance, documentation). Document inspection report with recommendations. Develop action plan for any identified deficiencies with responsible owner, target completion date, and progress tracking. Close out actions and document completion.

- **Contractor safety induction:** All contractors (plumber, electrician, cleaning contractor, equipment servicer, builder) must complete site-specific induction before commencing work. Induction covers: clinic location and hazards (bloodborne pathogens, sharps, chemical hazards), emergency procedures and assembly point, WHS representative contact, incident reporting procedure, prohibited areas (clinical areas off-limits unless supervised), PPE requirements, parking and site rules, expected duration of work, client contact person. Document induction on sign-off sheet (name, date, induction provider). Keep records for minimum 3 years.

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Clinic Manager (overall responsibility) | **Backup:** GP Principal/Owner

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- WHS risk register (quarterly, especially new hazard entries and rating changes)
- Serious incident notifications to WorkSafe (within 24 hours of incident, before notifying WorkSafe)
- Annual WHS audit report and response action plan
- WorkCover insurance policy renewal and premium (annually)
- Incident trend analysis (injuries, near-misses, pattern identification — quarterly)
- Return-to-work plans for any injured staff member

### Documents / Systems Involved

- WHS Risk Register (spreadsheet or dedicated WHS software: SafetyMaster, SpeakUp)
- Incident and Near-Miss Register (form-based or integrated with staff management system)
- WorkCover insurance policy and certificate of currency
- Fire evacuation procedures and plan (displayed in clinic areas)
- Fire drill records and maintenance logs
- Sharps container inventory and disposal contractor invoices (Stericycle/Daniels)
- Needlestick injury protocol document and post-exposure management records
- Manual handling assessment spreadsheet and control implementation records
- PPE inventory, fit-test records (N95), training records
- WHS Committee meeting minutes (if >20 employees)
- WorkSafe Victoria notification logs and correspondence
- Return-to-work plan templates and tracking
- Annual WHS inspection report

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Serious injury without immediate notification:** WorkSafe Victoria can issue infringement notices (up to $30,000 for failing to notify serious injury within 1 hour per Section 38 OHS Act 2004). Owner/clinic director personally liable if failure to report is reckless. Worker compensation claim rejected if employer fails notification obligations, creating legal and reputational risk.

- **Needlestick injury mismanagement:** If source patient testing not arranged within 72 hours, critical window for baseline serology lost, complicating future infection tracking. Unmanaged exposure risk: infected worker sues clinic for negligence if not offered PEP. Staff morale and retention impacted. Workers compensation claim costs escalate without timely intervention.

- **No RTW coordinator appointed (>20 employees):** Breach of WorkCover Victoria requirements. Insurer may void coverage or impose claim denial if RTW obligations not met. Injured worker may abandon RTW attempt, leading to long-term disability and workers compensation premium increase. Clinic unable to retain injured staff effectively.

- **WHS Committee not established (>20 employees):** Non-compliance with OHS Act 2004 Section 36. WorkSafe can issue non-compliance notice requiring committee formation within specified timeframe. Failure to comply triggers prosecution risk and $15,000+ infringement notices.

- **Hazardous incident occurs without risk assessment:** If staff injured from identified hazard with no documented control measure, clinic exposed to negligence claim (common law duty of care under negligence law) + workers compensation claim + WorkSafe investigation. Indemnity insurer may deny cover due to poor risk management.

- **Sharps disposal non-compliance:** Improper sharps disposal (overfull containers, unsafe handling) creates infection risk. If staff member injured from sharps in unsecured area, WorkCover claim + negligence claim + reputational damage (healthcare regulator scrutiny). Medical waste contractor invoicing disputes if disposal non-compliance noted.

- **Fire drill not conducted or exits blocked:** If fire occurs and staff cannot evacuate safely, potential loss of life, criminal investigation, closure of clinic, loss of accreditation and provider number. Failure to maintain emergency procedures breaches workplace law and building code.

## CATEGORY 12 — PRIVACY / RECORDS / ACCESS REQUESTS / DOCUMENT CONTROL

### What Has to Be Done

- **Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) and Australian Privacy Principles (APPs) compliance:** Clinic must comply with 13 Australian Privacy Principles. Key obligations: APP 1 (manage personal information transparently), APP 2 (notice and collection), APP 6 (use and disclosure — only with consent or permitted reason), APP 11 (security of personal information — reasonable steps to protect against misuse, loss, unauthorised access), APP 13 (correction and complaints — allow patients to correct records and lodge privacy complaints with clinic or Office of the Australian Information Commissioner). Implement written privacy policy addressing all APPs, training for all staff on privacy obligations, and process for handling privacy complaints.

- **Health Records Act 2001 (Vic) compliance:** Victorian health organisations must comply with 10 Health Principles (HPs) under Health Records Act. Key obligations: HP 1 (collection for lawful purpose), HP 2 (collection of personal health information only with consent), HP 3 (use and disclosure — only for purpose collected or directly related or with consent), HP 4 (data quality and data correction — ensure accuracy, completeness, relevance, timeliness), HP 5 (data security — protect against misuse, loss, unauthorised access, modification), HP 10 (complaints — health records complaints to Commissioner for Health Records Privacy). Clinic must display Health Principles notice, maintain complaint log, and respond to complaint within 30 calendar days.

- **Patient health record retention — 7 years (adult), until age 25 for minors:** Clinic must retain patient medical records for minimum 7 years from last consultation (RACGP guidelines align with this standard). For patients who were minors when record created: retain until patient reaches age 25 years (or 7 years from age of majority, whichever is longer). Documentation must be clear: electronic records backed up, paper records stored securely. At end of retention period, destroy records securely (shred paper, secure data deletion software for electronic). Document destruction with date and method. Exception: retain longer if legal claim or audit in progress.

- **Patient access to records requests — respond within 30 days:** When patient requests copy of their health record, clinic has 30 calendar days to respond (Health Records Act 2001 (Vic) HP 6 and Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) APP 12). Process: (1) identify request in writing, (2) verify patient identity and authority, (3) locate and compile requested information, (4) redact third-party information (e.g., referring doctor's notes) where appropriate, (5) provide in requested format (electronic PDF preferred, paper print if requested), (6) charge reasonable reproduction cost only (not professional interpretation cost). Log request with date received, patient name, date responded, and format provided. Keep copy of records released.

- **Privacy breach identification and response:** Train staff to identify potential privacy breaches (unauthorised access, disclosure, loss of records, hacking, email sent to wrong recipient). Breach = unauthorised access, use, or disclosure of personal health information likely to result in serious harm. Develop breach response protocol: (1) contain breach immediately (change password, quarantine affected data, stop unauthorised access), (2) investigate scope (which patients, which data, how long), (3) document incident (date, time, discovery method, initial response), (4) notify OAIC and affected individuals if serious, (5) implement preventive measures, (6) retain incident record for 7 years. Test privacy controls quarterly (e.g., audit access logs).

- **Notifiable Data Breaches (NDB) scheme compliance:** If clinic holds health information and suffers data breach likely to result in serious harm to individual, clinic must notify: (a) affected individual(s) and (b) OAIC (Office of the Australian Information Commissioner) as soon as practicable. NDB scheme applies if: health information held by covered entity, data breach occurs, serious harm is likely. Notification to individual must be in writing and include: identity of organisation, what information was involved, nature of breach, likely consequences, action clinic taking to mitigate harm, recommendations for individual to protect themselves. Notification to OAIC via online form at oaic.gov.au if breach likely to result in serious harm.

- **Staff confidentiality agreements:** All staff (including GPs, nurses, receptionists, cleaners, contractors) must sign confidentiality agreement before commencing work. Agreement must cover: obligation to maintain confidentiality of patient information and clinic business information, prohibition on disclosure except as authorised by clinic or law, consequences of breach (disciplinary action up to termination, legal action), ongoing obligation after employment ceases. File signed agreement in personnel file. Refresh agreement annually or when role changes.

- **Privacy policy display and patient information forms:** Display written privacy policy in clinic reception and on website (minimum: name of clinic, purpose of collecting personal health information, how patients can access records, complaints process, contact details of Privacy Officer/Manager, link to Health Principles notice). Provide patient information form at first visit addressing: collection purposes, use and disclosure practices, access rights, complaint procedure, referral and third-party communication practices. Retain signed acknowledgement in patient record.

- **Document version control system:** Implement version control for all clinical and operational documents (policies, procedures, induction manuals, protocols). Version control log must track: document name, version number (e.g., v1.0, v2.1), date created/modified, author name, change description, approval authority, effective date, review date. Use consistent naming convention (e.g., WHS_Policy_v2.3_2024). For electronic documents, use document management system (SharePoint, OneDrive, or dedicated practice management software feature) to track versions automatically. Never overwrite previous versions; archive superseded versions for 7-year audit trail.

- **Record destruction schedule for expired records:** Develop destruction schedule (annual or quarterly) specifying: record types subject to destruction (expired patient records, destroyed sharps containers, old financial records), retention period per record type, destruction method (paper shredding on-site or via contractor, electronic data using NIST 800-88 approved deletion or secure destruction software), documentation of destruction (destruction certificate from contractor, sign-off sheet for on-site shredding with date, quantity, method), responsible person. Include in schedule: financial records (7 years minimum), staff records (7 years), incident reports (7 years), contractor documents (7 years), expired medicines (hazardous waste disposal), expired vaccines (clinical waste).

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Practice Manager / Privacy Officer | **Backup:** GP Principal/Owner, Senior Receptionist

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Privacy policy and Health Principles notice (annually, or when clinic services/data practices change)
- Any privacy complaints received (within 24 hours of receipt)
- Notifiable Data Breach response plan (annually, or if breach occurs)
- Patients' access to records request process (quarterly audit: sample 5 requests, verify 30-day response met)
- Confidentiality agreement template (annually, refresh if new staff roles added)
- Record destruction schedule and certificates of destruction (annually)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) — Australian Privacy Principles notice
- Health Records Act 2001 (Vic) — Health Principles notice
- Written Privacy Policy (clinic-specific, addressing APPs and HPs)
- Patient Information Form and confidentiality acknowledgement
- Staff Confidentiality Agreement (signed by all staff)
- Clinical Software privacy settings audit log (access control, audit trail by user ID)
- Patient Access to Records Request Register (dates, names, formats, responses)
- Privacy Breach Log (incident reports, response actions, OAIC notifications)
- Document version control system (SharePoint, OneDrive, practice software)
- Record Destruction Schedule and certificates of destruction
- OAIC correspondence (if breach notifications required)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Privacy policy not displayed / not meeting APP 1 requirements:** OAIC can issue compliance notice requiring policy update within 30 days. Failure to comply triggers investigation and possible enforcement action. Clinic may face negative publicity and patient loss if privacy practices unclear.

- **Patient access to records request not responded to within 30 days:** Breach of Health Records Act 2001 (Vic) HP 6. Patient can lodge complaint with Commissioner for Health Records Privacy. If commissioner investigates, may require clinic to release records + pay compensation to patient (up to $40,000+ in serious cases). Reputational damage and regulatory scrutiny.

- **Serious data breach not notified to OAIC or affected individuals:** Breach of NDB scheme under Privacy Act 1988 (Cth). OAIC can issue civil penalty up to $2.5 million for body corporate, or $500,000 for individual. If breach discovered later, clinic faces compounding reputational damage, potential class action lawsuit by affected patients, media attention.

- **Health records destroyed before 7-year retention period:** If patient requires historical record (legal claim, treatment continuity) and records destroyed prematurely, clinic cannot provide evidence, loses defence, patient may obtain judgment by default. Breach of Health Records Act 2001 (Vic) HP 1 (must collect for lawful purpose and retain appropriately).

- **Staff access to patient records not audited:** If staff member accesses records of family member, celebrity, or for no clinical purpose, breach goes undetected. Affected patient may sue for privacy invasion. Clinic liable if negligent in monitoring access. Reputational damage if media publicises unauthorised access.

- **Confidentiality agreements not signed or outdated:** If staff member discloses patient information after termination (e.g., to media, competitors, for commercial gain), clinic has limited legal recourse if agreement not in place. Patients may claim clinic failed to protect confidentiality, triggering privacy complaints and potential class action.

- **NDB breach response delayed:** OAIC expects notification within 30 days of becoming aware of serious breach. Delayed response may result in regulatory action. If patients learn of breach from media rather than clinic, trust eroded, class action risk increased.

## CATEGORY 13 — BUILDING / LEASE / MAINTENANCE / UTILITIES

### What Has to Be Done

- **Lease payment tracking and rent review schedule:** Establish rent payment calendar with due dates, payment method (EFT preferred), and payee details. Clinic manager to verify rent amount matches lease each month before payment. Document lease start date, term length, review date/increment clause (typically annual CPI adjustment or fixed percentage — e.g., 3% p.a.), break clauses if available, renewal trigger date (usually 6–12 months before expiry). Set calendar reminder 6 months before lease expiry to assess renewal or relocation options. Maintain lease agreement file with all amendments, board minutes approving any variations, and correspondence with landlord. Annual review with accountant to ensure rent expense coded correctly.

- **Outgoings reconciliation (council rates, water, building insurance):** Verify lease specifies which outgoings clinic responsible for: typically clinic pays water, utilities; landlord pays building insurance, council rates (unless lease shifts these). Request landlord's statement of outgoings annually (usually provided in June–July). Reconcile against clinic invoices: check water meter readings match billing, cross-reference electricity/gas bills, verify no double-charging. Request itemised council rates notice from landlord (property code, valuation, rates payable). Challenge any unverified outgoing charges with landlord in writing. Allocate building outgoings to expense account separate from rent (easier to isolate for rate negotiations later).

- **Preventive maintenance schedule (HVAC, plumbing, electrical):** Develop annual preventive maintenance (PM) plan: HVAC (air-conditioning/heating — service twice yearly, filter replacement quarterly), plumbing (drain inspection annually, tap/fixture inspection 6-monthly), electrical (switchboard inspection annually, electrical safety switch testing annually). Engage licensed contractors (gas fitter license for gas components, electrician licence for electrical work in Victoria). Document all PM services: contractor name, date, scope, issues identified, rectification actions, next service date. Cost budgeting: allocate 5–8% of lease value annually for PM and reactive maintenance. Schedule PM outside clinical hours where possible (early morning, weekends).

- **Equipment maintenance (autoclaves, sterilisers, defibrillators — annual biomedical servicing):** Autoclave and steriliser units must be serviced annually by biomedical technician (validates pressurisation, temperature, sterilisation efficacy). Document service with certificate including: unit serial number, test date, temperature/pressure readings, sterilisation efficacy result (Bacillus spore test if applicable), next service date. Defibrillator (automated external defibrillator — AED) must be serviced annually: pad replacement if expired, battery check, electrode pad function test. Maintain service log accessible for staff reference. Non-compliance with equipment servicing invalidates sterilisation — can expose clinic to infection control breach if unsterilised instruments used on patients.

- **Cleaning supervision (daily cleaning schedule, deep clean schedule):** Designate cleaning manager responsible for overseeing clinic cleanliness and hygiene. Daily cleaning schedule: reception/waiting area (floors swept/vacuumed, surfaces wiped with appropriate disinfectant — dilute bleach or TGA-approved disinfectant), clinical areas (consult rooms, nurses' station, procedure areas cleaned after each patient session, surfaces wiped with disinfectant), toilets (cleaned 2–3 times daily, hand soap and paper towels stocked), staff areas (lunch room, kitchen cleaned after use). Deep clean schedule: fortnightly (thorough floor cleaning, window washing, high-surface cleaning — light fittings, door frames, skirting boards), quarterly (carpet steam cleaning, wall cleaning, sanitiser fogging of clinical areas). If outsourcing cleaning to contractor, ensure contract specifies: daily scope, frequency, disinfectant standards, contingency for absence, quality standards with inspection checklist. Document cleaning completion on sign-off sheet daily.

- **Waste management (sharps disposal, clinical waste, general waste):** Establish waste segregation: sharps containers (yellow lidded, compliant AS/NZS 4261) placed in clinical areas, clinical/contaminated waste (items with blood/body fluid — swabs, dressings, gloves, masks — in red bags or yellow bins per contractor), pathology waste (specimen containers, culture plates — usually collected by pathology contractor), general waste (office paper, packaging — standard bin). Arrange sharps container collection fortnightly or monthly (frequency depends on volume — never let containers become 2/3+ full). Contract with approved clinical waste contractor: Stericycle, Daniels, Cleanaway — verify they have EPA/State environment protection licence. Keep disposal invoices, contractor certificates, and manifest copies for 7 years (audit trail). Budget: sharps disposal typically $50–100/month, clinical waste $200–400/month depending on volume.

- **Landlord liaison and defect notification:** Establish clear communication channel with landlord/property manager (email preferred for documentation). Report defects promptly in writing: specify location, description of defect (e.g., roof leak above reception, tap leaking under sink in nurse station), date first noticed, impact on clinic operations (e.g., water damage risk, infection control risk), requested action and timeframe. Keep copies of all defect notifications and landlord responses. Follow up if landlord response delayed beyond reasonable timeframe (typically 48 hours for urgent issues, 2 weeks for routine). Document when landlord rectifies defects and obtain sign-off. If landlord unresponsive, consider formal letter from accountant/legal advisor highlighting landlord responsibilities under lease and relevant building codes (Building Code Australia, Health Building Code).

- **Make-good obligations review:** Review lease carefully for make-good clause (specifies condition clinic must return premises to at lease end). Typical make-good: remove clinic fitout (if temporary), reinstate original floor/wall finishes (patch paint, remove clinic-specific branding), repair any damage beyond normal wear and tear. At commencement, photograph/video record premises condition and obtain landlord's signature on condition report. Cost budget: $5,000–15,000+ for typical medical clinic make-good depending on fitout extent. Negotiate exclusions (e.g., normal wear and tear not make-good, landlord responsible for structural repairs). If lease ending, obtain make-good quote from contractor 6 months before expiry and negotiate with landlord if quote high (some landlords overestimate make-good costs).

- **Building security (alarm, CCTV, access control):** Install alarm system with after-hours monitoring (24/7 monitored alarm response). CCTV cameras in: reception (to record patients, identify theft/damage), car park if clinic operates own, staff areas if justified (theft/violence risk). Access control: reception entrance locked outside business hours, staff entry via card/pin lock, clinical areas accessible only to authorised staff. Maintain alarm code management: change codes quarterly, remove codes for departed staff within 24 hours of departure. CCTV footage retention: minimum 30 days (allows review of incidents). Review and test alarm system annually (ensure all sensors functioning, monitoring service active). Budget: alarm monitoring $50–100/month, CCTV system $200–400/month for professional monitoring and storage.

- **Utilities management (electricity, gas, water — compare providers annually):** Track utility consumption and costs monthly. Electricity: obtain meter readings monthly, cross-check against invoices, monitor for usage spikes (may indicate faulty equipment — investigate HVAC/refrigerator issues). Gas: used for heating/hot water typically — monitor for unusual consumption increases. Water: track consumption; high usage may indicate leaks or excessive cleaning volume (compare to previous years). Annually: solicit quotes from alternative electricity/gas providers via comparepower.com.au (Australian comparison service). If switching providers, compare: unit rates (c/kWh), daily supply charges, contract length, exit fees, discount rates, solar credits (if applicable). Switch providers if savings >10% on annual bill. Document all utility provider changes with accountant to ensure billing to correct entity.

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Clinic Manager (day-to-day), Business Manager / Accountant (oversight) | **Backup:** GP Principal/Owner

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Lease agreement annually (verify rent escalation clause correct, review break clauses, confirm renewal trigger date)
- Outgoings reconciliation statement from landlord (annually, flag any unexpected increases)
- Annual preventive maintenance plan and completed service certificates (verify coverage adequate)
- Building security audit (alarm system, CCTV, access control — annually or after security incident)
- Make-good obligation clause and cost estimate (if lease ending within 12 months)
- Utility costs and provider comparison (annually, approve any provider switches)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Lease agreement (original + all amendments)
- Rent payment schedule and ledger (office accounting system)
- Outgoings reconciliation statements (from landlord)
- Council rates notice (provided by landlord)
- Utility invoices and consumption records (electricity, gas, water)
- PM schedule and service certificates (HVAC, plumbing, electrical)
- Equipment service certificates (autoclave, steriliser, AED)
- Cleaning schedule and completion sign-offs (daily log, supervisor check)
- Sharps/clinical waste disposal invoices and manifests
- Waste management contractor agreement and contact details
- Landlord/property manager contact list and correspondence log
- Building condition report (commencement photos/video)
- Alarm system and CCTV access/monitoring details
- Make-good obligation clause and photographs

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Rent overpayment due to missed review clause:** If lease includes CPI escalation and clinic fails to track/challenge inflated increase, overpayment accumulates over years. Example: $60,000/year lease with 5% wrong increase = $3,000/year overpaid ($21,000 over 7 years). No recourse if not challenged within timeframe specified in lease.

- **Unverified outgoings passed to clinic:** Landlord may incorrectly allocate rates, insurance, or maintenance costs to clinic if outgoings not reconciled annually. Clinic may pay for landlord-funded services (e.g., building insurance). Can result in $5,000–$20,000+ annual overpayment.

- **Defective HVAC or plumbing not maintained:** Preventive maintenance not conducted, equipment fails mid-patient day. Clinic forced to close or operate without proper temperature control/water supply. Patient safety risk (infection control compromised if water supply lost). Emergency repair costs triple (e.g., $500 routine service vs. $1,500+ emergency call-out). If infection control breach occurs during downtime, AHPRA investigation and accreditation risk.

- **Autoclave/steriliser not serviced annually:** Equipment performance deteriorates undetected. Instruments may not achieve sterilisation temperature/pressure, unknown to clinic staff. Risk of instrument-borne infection to patients. If infection occurs and traced to unsterilised instrument, clinic faces negligence claim, patient compensation (potentially $50,000+), regulatory investigation, loss of accreditation.

- **Sharps containers overfilled or disposal delayed:** Overfilled containers risk sharps injury to staff or cleaning contractors. Delayed disposal creates accumulating biohazard. If staff injured and container non-compliant, WorkCover claim + negligence claim. Contractor may refuse collection if containers overstuffed.

- **Security not maintained:** After-hours break-in, drugs stolen, patient records accessed. Loss of medication stock and potential overdose exposure (TGA/AHPRA investigation). Patient data breach triggers privacy breach response obligations. Reputational damage.

- **Make-good not understood at lease start:** At lease end, landlord demands full make-good exceeding expectations. Clinic owner responsible for costs ($10,000–$30,000+ for major fitout removal) with no dispute resolution if terms unclear. Example: clinic spent $50,000 on clinical fitout, failed to negotiate exclusion, forced to remove and restore at additional $15,000+ cost.

## CATEGORY 14 — INSURANCES

### What Has to Be Done

- **Public liability insurance (minimum $10–20M coverage):** Clinic must hold public liability insurance protecting against claims by patients, visitors, or third parties for injury or property damage caused by clinic operations. Minimum coverage $10M recommended (larger clinics $20M+). Policy must cover: bodily injury (injury to person), property damage, medical malpractice (note: this overlaps with professional indemnity — often purchased separately). Obtain quote from several providers (Allianz, QBE, Westpac Insurance, ACE Insurance, Chubb). Annual premium typically $2,000–$5,000 depending on clinic size, claims history, turnover. Review coverage limits every 2 years to ensure adequate growth with patient numbers. Maintain current certificate of currency to provide to landlord and accreditation bodies.

- **Professional indemnity insurance — clinic level (separate from individual GP PI):** In addition to each GP's individual PI insurance (MDA National, MIPS, Avant), clinic must hold clinic-level/organisation-wide PI insurance. This protects clinic business entity against clinical negligence claims. Essential if clinic structured as company/partnership (distinguishes from sole trader). Policy covers: diagnostic errors, treatment complications, informed consent failures, prescribing errors, test result communication failures. Premium varies by: clinic size (number of GPs), specialty mix (general practice lower risk than procedural), claims history, turnover. Annual cost: $5,000–$15,000+ depending on factors. Ensure clinic PI policy is notified of any clinical complaints/concerns (some policies require notification). Maintain certificate of currency.

- **WorkCover / WorkSafe Victoria insurance (mandatory for all employers):** Statutory insurance required for all Victorian employers (private and public). Covers work-related injury to employees: medical treatment, lost wages (weekly payment), lump sum for permanent disability. Premium calculated as: (total payroll × rate per $100 payroll) based on industry code (typically 6211 for medical practice). Clinic notifies insurer of payroll end-of-year (by 30 June) and pays final premium adjustment. Claims must be reported to insurer within statutory timeframe. Certificate of currency required for business operations and landlord tenancy. If clinic fails to maintain WorkCover, employer personally liable for all worker injury costs (bankruptcy risk for significant claims).

- **Business pack (contents, business interruption):** Business pack typically bundles: building contents insurance (clinic equipment, furniture, medical equipment — replacement value $100,000–$300,000+ depending on clinic size), business interruption insurance (covers loss of income if clinic forced to close due to insured event — fire, natural disaster, key person loss), loss of profits (if clinic cannot operate). Obtain quote for combined business pack (cheaper than separate policies). Contents coverage should include: clinical equipment (autoclave, steriliser, examination chairs, computers, software), furniture, fitout, stock (vaccines, consumables). Inventory all assets and obtain replacement cost estimate (work with accountant/office equipment supplier). Annual review: ensure sums insured keep pace with equipment replacement costs (value creep). Obtain proof of value from accountant for large claims.

- **Cyber liability insurance:** Increasingly important for medical practices. Covers: data breach costs (notification, credit monitoring, forensic investigation), ransomware attack (recovery costs, business interruption), cyber extortion (blackmail threats), business email compromise (fraudulent payment instructions), regulatory fines under privacy law. Premium: $1,500–$3,000/year depending on data handled and systems. Policy should include: IT forensics, legal defense, regulatory defense, notification costs, business interruption. Cyber insurance often paired with cyber security requirements (password manager, multi-factor authentication, regular backups, staff training). Obtain quote from cyber-specialist insurer (Chubb, Westpac Insurance, AIG).

- **Management liability / Directors & Officers (D&O) insurance (if company structure):** If clinic incorporated as company or proprietary company, D&O insurance protects directors/shareholders personally from liability claims (employment practices liability, breach of directors' duties, regulatory fines, shareholder/investor claims). Annual premium $2,000–$5,000. Important if clinic has external investors, multiple shareholders, or employee-related issues (wrongful termination, discrimination claims). Review annually to ensure coverage adequate.

- **Certificate of currency requests (landlord, accreditation bodies):** Insurers provide certificates of currency (evidence of current insurance). Clinic must provide to: landlord (required in lease, typically public liability), accreditation bodies (RACGP AGPAL requires PI insurance and public liability evidence), pathology contractor (may request), business partners. Maintain register of certificates of currency issued and to whom. Set calendar reminder 30 days before policy expiry to request updated certificate from insurer. Email certificates to relevant parties within 5 days of receipt. If certificate not provided before policy expires, landlord may deem clinic in breach of lease. Accreditation bodies may suspend accreditation if proof of insurance lapses.

- **Premium review and broker liaison:** Engage insurance broker (role: obtain multiple quotes, negotiate rates, manage renewals). Discuss with broker annually: claims history, coverage gaps, industry changes (new cyber threats, privacy legislation), policy features (excess amounts, exclusions). Provide broker with clinic information: number of staff, payroll, turnover, services offered, premises size (sqm), claims history (accidents, complaints, thefts). Broker submits to multiple insurers and provides comparison quote table. Review quotes: compare cover (what's included/excluded), premiums, excess levels (higher excess = lower premium, but risk if high claims). Negotiate with broker if quotes higher than prior year (benchmark against inflation — if 10%+ increase, challenge).

- **Claims management:** If incident occurs (injury claim, professional negligence claim, property damage), notify insurer within 24 hours (policy requirement). Submit claim form with: incident description, date/time, parties involved, witnesses, photographic evidence (if applicable), medical reports if injury. Insurer assigns claims manager or legal counsel. Cooperate fully: provide requested documents, statements, records. Do NOT admit liability or discuss claim on social media. Allow insurer to manage defence/settlement (do not negotiate settlement independently without insurer approval). Document all communication with insurer. Follow up if claim stalled (insurers may delay on marginal claims).

- **Renewal calendar management:** Maintain insurance renewal calendar (colour-coded by policy type). Track: renewal date, insurer contact, broker contact, policy number, certificate of currency due date. Set reminders: 90 days before renewal (request quotes), 30 days before renewal (chase broker for final quotes and insurer confirmation), 14 days before renewal (final decision/approval), 5 days after renewal (request certificate of currency). Assign responsibility (typically Business Manager or Clinic Manager) to manage renewals. Approve all renewals and premium payments with Clinic Owner (significant expense, must align with budget). Document decision and approved premium in business records.

- **Insurance adequacy review (annual):** At annual planning meeting, review: have clinic services/size changed (patient numbers grown, new services added, staff increased)? Have claims occurred (indicates coverage gaps)? Have risks changed (new regulatory requirements, new building risks)? Consult with broker/accountant: do current coverage limits remain adequate? Are there new risks not covered (e.g., cyber liability if not previously held)? Revisit sums insured for contents (equipment values increase over time). Example: clinic grew from 4 to 8 GPs; PI insurance limits may need increase from $5M to $10M. Update spreadsheet tracking coverage by type and limits.

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Business Manager / Accountant (day-to-day management, broker liaison, processing renewals) | **Backup:** GP Principal/Owner (approves policies, reviews claims)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- All insurance policies (professional indemnity, public liability, WorkCover, business pack, cyber) — review at least annually or when clinic changes
- Certificates of currency prior to distribution (ensure correct coverage limits and policy dates)
- Insurance claims (within 48 hours of insurer notification of claim — understand claim nature and insurer's response strategy)
- Annual insurance adequacy review and broker recommendations for coverage changes
- Budget approval for insurance premiums (renewal costs reviewed before payment)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Professional indemnity insurance policy and certificate of currency
- Public liability insurance policy and certificate of currency
- WorkCover insurance policy, certificate of currency, and annual payroll declaration form
- Business pack (contents, business interruption) policy and certificate
- Cyber liability insurance policy and certificate (if held)
- D&O insurance policy (if company structure)

- Insurance adequacy review summary (annual)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Public liability policy lapsed or inadequate coverage:** Patient injured in clinic sues for $500,000 damages. If public liability insurance expired or cancelled, clinic owner personally liable. Personal assets (home, savings) at risk. Bankruptcy possible in catastrophic injury case. Landlord may terminate lease if tenant loses required insurance.

- **Professional indemnity insurance not maintained:** Negligence claim against clinic (diagnostic error, treatment complication). PI insurance expired or insufficient coverage. Clinic cannot defend claim, forced to pay settlement from operating cash or personal assets. Example: $200,000 negligence claim with $100,000 PI limit means clinic pays $100,000 out-of-pocket. Reputational damage to clinic and individual GPs.

- **WorkCover insurance not current:** Worker injured in workplace. If WorkCover not current, employer personally liable for all compensation costs (medical, wages, permanent disability lump sum). Large claim (e.g., permanent incapacity) could cost $500,000+. Personal bankruptcy risk. Regulatory penalties from WorkSafe Victoria.

- **Certificate of currency not provided to landlord:** Lease typically requires proof of public liability insurance. If certificate expired and not renewed, lease deemed breached. Landlord can: demand proof within 7 days, terminate lease if not provided, pursue damages. Clinic forced to relocate with short notice.

- **Business pack (contents) underinsured:** Clinic equipment stolen or destroyed by fire. Replacement value $250,000 but policy only covers $150,000. Clinic cannot replace autoclave, steriliser, computers — forced to close or limp along with inadequate equipment. Business interruption loss (lost patient revenue during closure) may not be covered if contents underinsured, triggering claims denial.

- **Cyber liability not held:** Ransomware attack encrypts clinic's patient data, computers non-functional. Attacker demands ransom ($50,000+). Without cyber insurance, clinic pays ransom or incurs recovery costs directly ($20,000–$100,000+). Data breach costs (notification, forensics) not covered. Privacy breach fines from OAIC may be uninsured.

- **Claims not notified to insurer in time:** Claim occurs but clinic doesn't notify insurer within policy timeframe (typically 24 hours to 30 days depending on policy). Insurer denies claim citing late notification. Clinic self-insures unexpected claim cost. Example: patient negligence claim of $300,000 becomes clinic liability if not notified to PI insurer in time.

## CATEGORY 15 — PATHOLOGY AND OTHER SUPPLIER AGREEMENTS

### What Has to Be Done

- **Pathology collection room management and provider selection:** Clinic typically partners with one or two major pathology providers (Dorevitch, Melbourne Pathology, Australian Clinical Labs — ACL, Sonic Healthcare) for blood collection, specimen processing, and test reporting. Confirm which provider(s) clinic uses and ensure collection room is set up to their specifications. Pathology provider controls: collection times (typically 8:00 AM–5:00 PM), specimen handling (temperature, transit time), barcode labelling standards, collection tube types (varies by test). Clinic manager responsible for: stocking correct collection tubes (various coloured tubes for different tests: yellow SST for serum, purple EDTA for FBC, etc.), ensuring collection room temperature 15–25°C, sharps disposal in pathology-approved containers, staff training on specimen collection technique (correct patient identification, proper tube filling, mixing), scheduling pathology staff collection times if on-site collections arranged.

- **Pathology service agreement compliance (check for rent/commission arrangements):** Review clinic's pathology service agreement for key terms: (a) collection arrangement (pathology staff collect in-clinic at scheduled times, or clinic sends specimens to lab), (b) specimen report format (electronic results portal, paper forms, automated interface to clinic software), (c) billing arrangement — most common models: (i) rebate/commission to clinic per test (clinic retains percentage of MBS rebate), or (ii) rent of collection room to pathology provider (clinic charges pathology provider fixed monthly fee), or (iii) mixed (commission on some tests, room rent on other services). IMPORTANT: ACCC scrutiny applies to pathology rent/commission arrangements — ensure arrangement does not tie clinic to exclusive provider inappropriately or restrict patient choice. Commission rates typically 5–15% of MBS rebate depending on test volume. Document arrangement in written agreement signed by both parties. Review annually for compliance with competition law and reasonableness of rates.

- **Specimen logistics and collection times:** Confirm collection schedule: if pathology staff collect on-site, schedule is typically 3–5 days/week, specific times (e.g., Monday/Wednesday/Friday 10:00 AM–12:00 PM). If clinic sends specimens to lab, establish transport logistics (courier pickup, refrigerated transport if required, delivery times). For time-sensitive tests (e.g., glucose tolerance test, psychoactive substance testing), comply with pathology's collection window requirements (e.g., fasting at specific time, collection within 30 minutes). Staff must understand: tests requiring fasting (GlucoseTT, lipid profile), tests requiring special handling (blood cultures transported immediately, CSF transported immediately on ice), specimen stability (some tests must be analysed within 24 hours). Train reception staff to advise patients on pre-test requirements (fasting from midnight if ordered).

- **Results integration with clinical software (verified delivery path):** Clinic's practice management software (Best Practice, Medical Director, Argus, zedmed) should integrate with pathology provider's electronic results portal for automated report delivery. Verify integration active: results should appear in patient's electronic health record within 4–24 hours of lab completion. Test integration: perform dummy test order via practice software, confirm result automatically loads into patient record. If integration not functioning, clinic must have manual fallback: pathology provider sends results via secure email or web portal, clinic staff manually enters results into patient record (error-prone, avoid). Establish protocol: who reviews results daily? (typically nurse or administrative staff flags abnormal results to GP same day). Ensure clinic software flags critical/panic values and triggers alert to GP immediately.

- **Pathology consumable supply and inventory management:** Clinic responsible for stocking collection tubes, needles, cotton wool, alcohol wipes, specimen containers for 24/7 pathology availability. Pathology provider typically supplies tubes at no cost if clinic maintains minimum order volumes (confirm with provider). Establish par-level stock (e.g., 50 units of SST tubes, 20 units of EDTA tubes). Monthly stocktake by reception/practice nurse: count current stock, check expiry dates on tubes (typically 2-year shelf-life), order if falling below par level (ETA 5–7 days usually). Never allow stock to run out — if patient requires test and no tubes available, must arrange alternative (postpone test or refer to pathology collection centre). Document consumable supply arrangement in pathology service agreement.

- **Pathology staff management (if separately employed):** If clinic directly employs phlebotomist/pathology collector (less common for small clinics — usually pathology provider manages own staff), establish employment contract specifying: role description (specimen collection, patient identification, infection control compliance, customer service), hours (typically variable — align with patient collection demand), minimum qualifications (phlebotomy certification, first aid certificate recommended). Set expectations: professional appearance, patient privacy (confidentiality), hand hygiene, correct specimen handling. Conduct performance review annually (quality of specimen collection, patient feedback, compliance with procedures). If pathology provider employs collectors, clinic manager liaises with provider's regional manager if issues arise (e.g., collector late, rude to patients).

- **Supplier invoice verification and payment:** Clinic receives invoices from pathology provider (typically monthly or quarterly based on test volume). Invoice verification process: (1) check invoices match signed service agreement (correct billing rate per test, any promotional discounts applied correctly), (2) sample-check invoice line items against test orders in clinic software (5–10% sample audit), (3) identify and chase aged invoices (greater than 30 days outstanding), (4) verify pathology provider is not double-billing tests (cross-check with Medicare reconciliation). Establish payment terms (usually NET 30 days from invoice date). Process payment via EFT within terms. Flag to manager/accountant if invoice higher than budget (may indicate increased test volume or price increase). Retain invoice and payment records for 7 years (audit trail, tax purposes).

- **Contract compliance monitoring and renewal:** Pathology service agreement specifies term (typically 1–3 years). Establish renewal reminder 90 days before contract expiry. Assess: have we satisfied? Any service issues (late collections, incorrect results, billing disputes)? Review renewal terms: are rates increasing? Are terms changing? Negotiate with provider if rates significantly increase (benchmark against alternative providers' quotes). Consider switching providers if: service unsatisfactory (reliability issues, poor customer service), rates uncompetitive, integration with clinic software poor, patient feedback negative. Switching process: provide notice per contract (typically 30–60 days), train staff on new provider's procedures, configure integration with clinic software, notify patients of change (if relevant). Document renewal decision and approved contract terms.

- **Other supplier agreement management (medical waste, linen, equipment service):** Clinic may have service agreements beyond pathology: (a) Medical waste disposal (sharps, clinical waste contractor — Stericycle, Daniels, Cleanaway): contract specifies collection frequency, container provision, disposal compliance, billing. Verify billing matches invoices, challenge if overchararged. (b) Linen supply (if clinic uses clinic coats, medical uniforms): contract specifies delivery frequency, laundering standard, replacement terms. Monitor quality (uniforms clean, on-time delivery). (c) Equipment service (autoclave, steriliser, defibrillator servicing): contract specifies annual service, parts replacement, response time for breakdowns. Ensure services completed as per contract. Review all supplier agreements annually for: rate increases (benchmark against alternatives), service level (performance issues), contract terms (auto-renewal clauses — note if contract auto-renews, set reminder to cancel if not needed). Maintain supplier contact list with service manager details and escalation process.

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Practice Nurse / Clinic Manager (collection room management, consumable stocking, collection scheduling) | **Backup:** Reception Manager, Accountant (invoice verification and supplier liaison)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Pathology service agreement annually (verify compliance with ACCC competition law if rent/commission arrangement, review billing terms)
- Pathology provider integration with practice software (test annually — order dummy test, verify results load)
- Monthly or quarterly invoice samples from pathology provider (verify billing accuracy and charges match agreement)
- Pathology collection times and specimen handling compliance (observe collection on-site quarterly)
- Annual supplier agreement review (pathology, medical waste, linen, equipment) — identify cost savings or service improvements

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Pathology service agreement (signed contract with provider, specifying collection times, billing arrangement, integration details)
- Pathology provider contact list (collection manager, billing contact, IT support contact)
- Practice software integration documentation (how results populated into patient record)
- Pathology consumable inventory list (par levels, reorder points, supplier contact)
- Specimen collection procedure manual (staff training — patient ID, tube types, handling)
- Monthly/quarterly pathology invoices (filed for audit trail, matched against service agreement terms)
- Supplier invoice register (tracking invoices received, payment status, amounts, approval)
- Pathology provider service level agreements (collection times, result delivery timeframe, uptime guarantee)
- Other supplier agreements (medical waste, linen, equipment service — terms and contact details)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Pathology integration not functioning:** Results not automatically loading into practice software. Staff must manually re-enter results, introducing transcription errors (wrong patient, wrong value). Patient receives incorrect result or misses critical value alert. Clinical risk: missed diagnosis, treatment delay, patient harm. Liability claim. AHPRA investigation if patient harm evident.

- **Pathology collection not scheduled or missed:** Patient ordered for blood test, no pathology staff collection on-site, patient must go to external collection centre. Patient experience poor (additional travel, cost). May delay diagnosis. If urgent result needed and collection missed, clinical risk if result unavailable (e.g., troponin for chest pain rule-out).

- **Specimen consumables stock out:** Patient needs urgent blood test, no collection tubes available. Must defer test or refer to collection centre. Clinical risk: diagnostic delay. Patient satisfaction impact.

- **Service agreement terms not understood or outdated:** Clinic paying commission to pathology provider but is incorrectly locked into exclusive arrangement (ACCC concern if anti-competitive). Or rent-based arrangement with no volume discount (clinic overpays if low test volume). Or billing rate changed without clinic awareness (invoice audit required). Result: overpayment to pathology provider, strain on clinic margin, wasted budget.

- **Pathology results delayed or missing:** Patient result not appearing in software after 24 hours (integration failure or lab delay). Patient cannot access result. GP cannot issue prescription or advice based on result. Clinical risk if critical value (abnormal result not communicated to patient/GP). Example: abnormal creatinine in renal patient not flagged to GP, kidney disease not managed. Follow-up testing overlooked.

- **Pathology invoice not verified:** Provider overcharges clinic for tests (billing system error, manual entry error). Clinic pays inflated invoice without catching discrepancy. If not audited, could be thousands of dollars per year in overcharges. Example: 50 extra tests billed incorrectly = $500–$1,000+ overcharge not caught.

- **Supplier agreement auto-renewal not cancelled:** Linen supplier or equipment service contract auto-renews without clinic review. Clinic continues paying for unused service or overpaying due to rate increase. Example: clinic switches pathology provider but equipment service contract auto-renews with old provider for another year, wasted $5,000+ on duplicate services.

## CATEGORY 16 — CONSUMABLES / STOCK / VACCINES / COLD CHAIN / CLINICAL SUPPLIES

### What Has to Be Done

- **Consumable stock par levels (PPE, wound care, syringes, swabs, specimen containers):** Establish par-level stock for all consumables based on patient volume and supply lead time. Categories: (a) PPE — nitrile gloves (various sizes: XS, S, M, L, XL), surgical masks (box of 50–100), N95 respirators (specific size per fit-tested staff), clinical gowns, eye protection (safety glasses, face shields), hand sanitiser; (b) Wound care — sterile dressings (various sizes: small, medium, large), sterile gauze, cotton wool, alcohol wipes, saline sachets, wound closure strips, adhesive tape; (c) Syringes and needles — 1mL, 3mL, 5mL, 10mL, 20mL syringes (26G, 25G, 23G, 21G needles for different uses: subcutaneous, intramuscular, venipuncture); (d) Specimen containers — urine cups (sterile, plain), sputum cups, faeces containers, saliva collection tubes; (e) Other — swabs (oral, nasal, wound, genital — sterile), tongue depressors, thermometer probe covers, ECG gel, lubricating gel (sterile), specimen bags. Establish stock spreadsheet listing each item: current stock on-hand, par level (minimum quantity to maintain), reorder point (quantity at which to order), reorder quantity (amount ordered each time), supplier, cost per unit. Example: nitrile gloves par level = 2,000 units (approx. 1-month supply for 5-doctor clinic), reorder point = 500 units (low-stock trigger), reorder qty = 2,000 units.

- **Reorder point management and procurement:** Assign responsibility (typically practice nurse or clinic manager) to check consumable stock weekly or fortnightly. When stock falls below reorder point, initiate purchase order to supplier (online, phone, or email). Verify: supplier holds correct item, pricing matches prior quotes, delivery timeframe acceptable (typically 5–7 days for non-urgent items, 24–48 hours if urgent). For urgent restocking, maintain backup supplier contact. Document purchase orders and receiving: log date ordered, quantity, supplier, invoice amount, date received. Cross-check received items against order (correct quantity, correct items, no damage). File invoice and match against practice accounting system (coding to consumables expense).

- **NIP vaccine ordering from DHHS Victoria (National Immunisation Program):** National Immunisation Program vaccines are free to eligible patients (children, pregnant women, at-risk adults per ATAGI recommendations). Clinic orders NIP vaccines directly from Victorian Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS) vaccine provider (typically a vaccine distribution service like medical wholesaler). To access NIP vaccines: clinic must be registered provider with DHHS (GP practice with valid provider number). Order NIP vaccines quarterly (align with cold chain capacity and patient demand). Typical NIP vaccines: DPTaP (diphtheria, pertussis, tetanus + acellular pertussis), IPV (inactivated polio), Hib (Haemophilus influenzae type b), PCV (pneumococcal conjugate vaccine), rotavirus, MMR (measles, mumps, rubella), varicella, meningococcal vaccines, HPV (human papillomavirus, 11–45 year olds), zoster (Shingrix, 65+ years or immunocompromised). DHHS supplies at no cost. Clinic stocks in clinic vaccine fridge. Document receipt of vaccines in temperature log and AIR (Australian Immunisation Register) system.

- **Private vaccine purchasing (non-NIP vaccines):** Vaccines outside National Immunisation Program purchased privately: examples include Shingrix (zoster, if patient age 50–64 and not yet eligible for free vaccine via PBS), higher-dose influenza vaccines (Fluzone High Dose for elderly), recombinant zoster vaccine (Shingrix if patient under 65), hepatitis A/B combination (if not covered by NIP pathway), traveller vaccines (yellow fever, typhoid, Japanese encephalitis), and some meningococcal vaccine variants. Order from vaccine distributors: Seqirus (part of CSL Limited), Sanofi Pasteur, GlaxoSmithKline (GSK), Pfizer, Mylan (now Viatris), MSD. Clinic pays for private vaccines (patient charged via MBS item number, typically more expensive than NIP vaccine). Verify supplier: authentic vaccines (check TGA-approved brands), cold chain handling, expiry dates on delivery. Document purchase invoices and vaccine details (lot number, expiry date, quantity received) in vaccine fridge inventory.

- **Cold chain management — storage temperatures:** Vaccines must be stored at specific temperatures: (a) Most vaccines: 2–8°C refrigerator (standard fridge temperature) — includes DPTaP, IPV, Hib, PCV, meningococcal, hepatitis vaccines. (b) Varicella and zoster vaccines: -15°C to -25°C freezer temperature (more temperature-sensitive, more expensive, requires separate freezer unit). Clinic must have: (i) Dedicated vaccine refrigerator (not a food fridge — dedicated unit preferred to avoid contamination, maintain consistent temperature, easier to monitor), (ii) Vaccine freezer if storing varicella/zoster vaccines, (iii) Temperature monitoring device: minimum daily thermometer check (twice daily preferred: morning and evening), ideally data logger (electronic device recording temperature continuously — more reliable, alerts if temperature excursion). Refrigerator/freezer must be: positioned away from direct sunlight, away from external walls (temperature fluctuations), with 10cm clearance on all sides for air circulation, on level surface. Check fridge operation monthly: ensure door seal intact (close door on £5 note, should not pull out easily — indicates good seal).

- **Temperature monitoring (minimum twice daily logging, data logger preferred):** Establish vaccine temperature log (paper or electronic) with: date, time (morning and evening minimum), temperature reading (in °C), staff member recording, action if temperature out of range (document temperature excursion immediately). If temperature out of range: pause vaccine use, assess duration and severity of excursion, contact vaccine manufacturer or DHHS vaccine team for advice (some vaccines may be salvageable, others must be discarded), document advice and action taken, report incident. Data logger preferred over manual thermometer (continuous recording, no human error, alerts if excursion occurs). Calibrate thermometer annually (send to calibration service to verify accuracy within ±0.5°C — cost $30–$50). Maintain temperature log for 3 years (audit trail if temperature excursion occurs).

- **Cold chain breach protocol:** If temperature excursion detected (refrigerator reading <2°C or >8°C for >2 hours, or freezer <-15°C or >-25°C for any duration), implement breach protocol: (1) Pause vaccine use immediately (isolate affected vaccines), (2) Document breach: date/time of discovery, duration of excursion (if known), affected vaccines, cause (fridge malfunction, door left open, power loss), (3) Contact DHHS vaccine team or vaccine manufacturer: provide breach details, obtain advice on vaccine viability, (4) Assess vaccines: if manufacturer advises vaccine may be viable, label "Presumed Potency Reduced" and use only if no alternatives available (document use), if non-viable, discard as clinical waste, (5) Prevent recurrence: repair fridge, replace faulty thermometer, reposition fridge away from heat source, (6) Document incident in WHS incident register. Temperature excursion in NIP vaccines must be reported to DHHS (may trigger vaccine restock if batch affected).

- **AIR (Australian Immunisation Register) recording after each vaccine administered:** After administering any vaccine (NIP or private), clinic must record in Australian Immunisation Register within 10 days. AIR is national registry administered by Department of Health. Recording includes: patient name, date of birth, vaccine name, date administered, batch/lot number, provider details (provider number). GPs access AIR via: HPOS (Health Professional Online Services) — requires PRODA registration, or integration through practice software (Best Practice, Medical Director have AIR integration). Recording must occur after vaccine given (not before — only record actual administration). Batch number crucial for traceability if vaccine batch recall occurs. Verify patient name/DOB accuracy before recording (common error: wrong patient if clinic has patients with similar names). AIR recording essential for: tracking childhood immunisation status (checked by schools, childcare), travel documentation (some countries require proof of vaccination from AIR), adult risk-group eligibility (e.g., NDSS — National Disability Insurance Scheme clients, aged care residents), safety monitoring (if vaccine adverse event reported, TGA may search AIR for affected batch).

- **Stock rotation (FIFO — first in, first out):** When receiving new vaccine/consumable deliveries, arrange stock so older product (sooner expiry) is dispensed before newer product (later expiry). Process: new delivery unpacked and placed BEHIND existing stock in fridge/cabinet (so older product used first). Staff trained: take stock from front of shelf/arrangement, newer stock placed at back. Weekly check: verify FIFO rotation observed, no expired stock on shelf (should be zero — expiry checked before dispensing). Spreadsheet track: vaccine lot numbers, expiry dates, quantity received, quantity used, balance. Example: receive 100 units of flu vaccine expiring 30 June, already have 30 units expiring 28 June on shelf — place new stock behind existing so 28 June stock used first.

- **Expiry date monitoring:** Before administering any vaccine/injectable, check expiry date on vial/ampoule (date printed on label). If expired, do not use — discard as clinical waste. Weekly fridge inspection: systematically check all vaccine vials and consumable packages for expiry dates, remove any expired items. Spreadsheet record: note items removed, date, reason (expired, opened but not used within required timeframe, temperature excursion). For vaccines: expiry date is critical — expired vaccine assumes loss of potency. For consumables: expiry varies (sterile syringes typically 3–5 years, saline sachets 2–3 years, some items no expiry). Maintain zero-tolerance policy on expired stock: never use expired items, never extend expiry with notation (not approved by TGA).

- **Sharps container management and disposal schedule:** Sharps containers (yellow lidded, compliant AS/NZS 4261) placed in: clinical consultation rooms (1 per room minimum if high procedure volume, e.g., injections, wound care), nurses' station (for medication disposal, dressing changes), procedure rooms if applicable. Container location: secure, accessible to staff, not accessible to children/public, near point-of-use (encourage safe sharps disposal). Staff training: place sharps immediately after use (no recap, no bending), do not overstuff (if >2/3 full, replace container), ensure lid closed securely, label with biohazard symbol. Monthly inspection: count containers in use, flag any overstuffed containers for immediate replacement, monitor fill rate (indicates volume, affects disposal frequency). Disposal schedule: arrange contractor collection (typically monthly or every 6–8 weeks depending on volume and clinic arrangement). Contractor: Stericycle, Daniels, Cleanaway (EPA-licensed clinical waste contractor). Document disposals: date container removed, quantity, contractor name, disposal certificate number. Cost: typically $30–$50 per container exchange, or flat fee ($200–$400/month for regular clinic).

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Practice Nurse / Clinic Manager (stock management, vaccine ordering, temperature monitoring, AIR recording) | **Backup:** Reception Manager, Second Nurse

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Vaccine cold chain audit (temperature logs, fridge function, data logger alerts — monthly)
- Cold chain breach incidents (if any) — immediate review and corrective action
- Stock par levels and reorder points (quarterly review — adjust if patient volume changes)
- Consumable costs and supplier pricing (quarterly or annually — benchmark against alternatives)
- AIR recording compliance (monthly audit: sample 10 immunisations, verify recorded in AIR within 10 days, verify batch numbers correct)
- Vaccine expiry tracking (monthly spot-check of fridge inventory)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Vaccine and consumable stock inventory (spreadsheet: item, par level, reorder point, par quantity, supplier, cost)
- Vaccine refrigerator and freezer with temperature monitoring device (thermometer or data logger)
- Temperature log (twice-daily readings, freezer and fridge if applicable)
- Cold chain breach protocol (written procedure and incident response log)
- NIP vaccine order confirmations and delivery receipts (DHHS or supplier)
- Private vaccine purchase invoices (supplier, lot numbers, expiry dates, cost)
- Sharps container inventory and disposal contractor invoices (Stericycle/Daniels/Cleanaway)
- AIR integration in practice software or HPOS access documentation
- Vaccine batch/lot number log (especially important if recall occurs)
- Consumable supplier contact list and delivery schedules
- Stock expiry checking log (weekly or fortnightly spot-checks)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Cold chain breach not detected:** Vaccines stored at wrong temperature for extended period (fridge fails, door left open). Vaccine potency reduced undetected. Patients vaccinated with non-potent vaccine, immunity not achieved. Years later: child not protected when exposed to disease (e.g., measles outbreak, unvaccinated child contracts measles). Clinic liability if child harm results (negligence claim, AHPRA investigation). TGA may initiate vaccine batch recall if breach widespread.

- **Expired vaccine administered:** Clinic dispenses vaccine past expiry date without noticing. Vaccine potency assumed low (may be non-potent). Patient's immunity not achieved. Similar outcome to cold chain breach above. AHPRA investigation likely if discovered. TGA may demand clinic recalls and restocks.

- **AIR recording not done or delayed:** Vaccine administered, not recorded in AIR within 10 days. Clinic fails to meet 10-day deadline. Patient's immunisation status unknown to schools, childcare, travel health services. Vaccine recorded late or not at all. If vaccine adverse event occurs (e.g., anaphylaxis, Guillain-Barré syndrome), TGA cannot link adverse event to vaccine in AIR (safety monitoring compromised). National safety monitoring impaired if multiple clinics fail to record.

- **Sharps container overstuffed or unsecured:** Sharps container 90%+ full, lid not properly closed. Risk of sharps injury: to staff emptying bin, to cleaner, to child if container tipped. Needlestick injury results in: staff testing, post-exposure prophylaxis, risk of bloodborne pathogen infection. WorkCover claim, negligence claim, WHS investigation.

- **Stock consumables expire without use:** Par levels set too high, consumables expire on shelf (sterile dressings, saline, specimen containers). Expired items discarded as waste. Waste of clinic budget (consumables cost money). Also creates disposal burden (hazardous waste if clinical items).

- **Vaccine supplier contract changes without notice:** Private vaccine supplier increases price 30%, clinic receives invoice for higher price without prior notification. Clinic forced to absorb cost or renegotiate. Or supplier discontinues product, clinic must find alternative. Patients may not receive preferred vaccine type if stock unavailable.

- **Consumable stock out due to slow reorder:** Par level calculated incorrectly (too low), clinic runs out of syringes or sterile gloves mid-week. Patient needs injection, no appropriate needle available. Must defer or source elsewhere (emergency order, higher cost). Patient experience poor, clinical risk if urgent injection delayed.

## CATEGORY 17 — COMPLAINTS / FEEDBACK / RISK MANAGEMENT

### What Has to Be Done

- **Complaints register (date received, nature, GP/staff involved, actions, outcome, closure date):** Establish formal complaints register (spreadsheet or dedicated complaints software: Cognito, SafetyMaster, Feedback Capture). For every complaint received (verbally, email, phone, written letter, online review), log: (1) Date and time complaint received, (2) Patient name and contact details, (3) Nature of complaint (e.g., "rude receptionist", "incorrect diagnosis", "long wait time", "prescription error"), (4) GP or staff member involved (name, role), (5) Description of incident (patient account of what happened), (6) Severity rating (low/medium/high based on harm or seriousness), (7) Investigating officer (who assessed complaint), (8) Investigation findings (root cause — why did this happen?), (9) Actions taken (remedial action, staff training, system change, apology), (10) Outcome (how complaint resolved: refund, re-treatment, written apology, no change required), (11) Closure date (when complaint marked resolved). Update register with status changes as complaint progresses.

- **Complaint acknowledgement within 2 business days:** When complaint received, acknowledge receipt in writing to patient within 2 business days. Acknowledgement letter/email should include: (1) Thank patient for bringing matter to clinic's attention, (2) Confirm understanding of complaint (reiterate patient's version), (3) Advise timeframe for investigation and response (typically 14–28 days depending on complexity), (4) Provide contact name of person managing complaint (e.g., Practice Manager), (5) Contact details for patient to ask questions. Acknowledgement reassures patient clinic taking complaint seriously and provides framework for response. Keep copy of acknowledgement in complaints file.

- **Written response within 30 days:** Complete investigation of complaint and provide written response to patient within 30 calendar days of receipt (standard regulatory expectation). Response letter should include: (1) Summary of complaint and investigation, (2) Clinic's findings (if investigation determined complaint valid or invalid), (3) Acknowledgement of patient's experience (empathy statement: "We understand your frustration..."), (4) Actions taken or being taken to address complaint (remedial action, system improvement, staff retraining), (5) Apology if appropriate (admission of error without necessarily accepting full legal liability — note: apology does not constitute admission of negligence in Australian law, so apologies encouraged), (6) Mechanism for patient to escalate if dissatisfied with response (e.g., Health Complaints Commissioner Victoria or AHPRA). Response letter must be professional, factual, and avoid defensive language. Do not blame patient or deny entirely without thorough investigation.

- **AHPRA mandatory notification obligations (practitioners must notify of impairment, misconduct):** Under Health Practitioner Regulation National Law (Vic), health practitioners (GPs, nurses) must notify AHPRA if they have: (a) Physical or mental impairment that significantly impacts ability to practise safely (e.g., untreated depression, addiction, cognitive decline), (b) Serious or repeated misconduct (e.g., repeated rude behaviour to patients, sexual misconduct, falsifying records), (c) Breach of professional standard that creates risk of harm (e.g., practicing outside scope, failure to maintain infection control, unsafe prescribing). Notification trigger: practitioner becomes aware of condition affecting practice safety. Clinic owner/manager does NOT directly notify AHPRA (responsibility lies with individual practitioner). However, if clinic becomes aware of impairment/misconduct in own GP or staff member, clinic should: (1) Raise concern with practitioner directly (documented conversation), (2) Require practitioner to self-notify to AHPRA or engage occupational health assessment, (3) If practitioner refuses/denies, clinic may need to escalate (seek legal advice from healthcare lawyer before considering clinic notification to AHPRA — significant legal issue, can damage career). Documentation critical: keep records of conversation and steps taken.

- **Health Complaints Commissioner Victoria — voluntary resolution service:** Patients dissatisfied with clinic's response to complaint can lodge formal complaint with Health Complaints Commissioner (HCC) Victoria — independent statutory body overseeing health service complaints. HCC offers: (1) Advice to patients on complaint rights, (2) Informal resolution (HCC facilitates discussion between patient and clinic if both agree), (3) Investigation and formal determination if resolution fails. If patient lodges complaint with HCC, clinic will be notified and expected to provide: statement of facts, documentary evidence, response to patient's allegations. HCC investigation can take 3–6 months. Clinic should cooperate fully, provide requested documents within timeframe specified. HCC may: recommend clinic improve practices, require staff retraining, require financial compensation to patient (discretionary). Decision is binding unless patient or clinic appeals to Victorian Civil and Administrative Tribunal (VCAT).

- **Patient safety notification via AHPRA if serious clinical incident:** If clinic becomes aware of serious clinical incident affecting patient safety (e.g., wrong-site procedure, misdiagnosis resulting in delayed treatment, medication error causing harm, diagnostic test missed causing patient detriment), clinic owner must consider AHPRA notification obligation under mandatory notification scheme. Serious incident examples: (a) Practitioner's conduct creates risk of significant harm to public, (b) Practitioner has not met professional standard in way that creates risk of significant harm, (c) Patient's privacy seriously breached. If incident involves GP at clinic, clinic may be required to notify AHPRA depending on severity and harm. Consult with healthcare lawyer before notifying AHPRA (notification can trigger professional investigation, impacting GP's career). Document incident thoroughly: preserve evidence, conduct root cause analysis, implement safeguards to prevent recurrence.

- **Risk register maintenance (identify, rate, control, review):** Risk register is living document identifying potential risks to clinic operations and patient safety. Register structure: (1) Risk ID (e.g., R-001), (2) Risk description (what could go wrong: "staff needlestick injury", "medication error", "patient fall in clinic", "data breach"), (3) Risk category (clinical, operational, compliance, financial, reputational), (4) Likelihood rating (1–5 scale: unlikely to almost certain), (5) Consequence rating (1–5 scale: insignificant to catastrophic), (6) Risk level (likelihood × consequence = low/medium/high), (7) Current controls (safeguards in place: vaccination, training, procedure, technology), (8) Residual risk (risk remaining after current controls — should be lower than inherent risk), (9) Responsible owner (who manages control), (10) Review date (when to reassess). Quarterly risk review: update register with new risks identified, assess effectiveness of controls, adjust risk ratings if new information emerges.

- **Patient feedback collection system (post-visit surveys, Google reviews):** Establish systematic feedback collection: (1) Post-visit surveys (paper form in waiting room or electronic survey via email/SMS after visit: "How satisfied with visit? Rate 1–5. Any comments?"), (2) Feedback box in reception (patients deposit anonymous feedback), (3) Online reviews: monitor Google Business Profile, health directory reviews (HotDoc, HealthEngine), Facebook, for patient comments. Set aside 30 minutes monthly to review feedback. Positive feedback: acknowledge (reply to reviewer, thank patient), use for staff motivation. Negative feedback: investigate, contact patient if possible for more details, address in complaints register if substantive issue identified.

- **Trend analysis of complaints and incidents (quarterly):** Quarterly review of all complaints, incidents, and patient feedback received in prior 3 months. Analyse trends: (1) Are same issues recurring? (e.g., multiple complaints about wait times suggests scheduling problem), (2) Are particular staff members subject of multiple complaints? (suggests training need or fit/capability issue), (3) Are particular GPs named frequently? (may indicate gap in clinical knowledge, communication style, or clinical practice issue), (4) Time of day patterns? (e.g., complaints spike late afternoon — suggests staff fatigue), (5) New risk areas emerging? Prepare trend report for management meeting and GP principal. Use trends to drive quality improvement initiatives.

- **Root cause analysis for serious incidents:** When serious incident occurs (patient harm, near-miss, safety event, serious complaint), conduct root cause analysis. RCA method: (1) Define incident clearly, (2) Gather facts (interviews with staff involved, review records, timeline), (3) Ask "why?" repeatedly to get past symptoms to underlying cause (5-Whys technique: Why did medication error occur? → Prescription label unclear. Why unclear? → Printing template poor. Why poor? → Never updated since 2010. Why no update? → No process for template review.). (4) Identify contributing factors (individual factors: fatigue, lack of training; system factors: inadequate checking process, poor labelling, workload), (5) Develop corrective actions (retrain individual, redesign process, procure better system, improve labelling), (6) Assign owner and due date for each action, (7) Follow-up: verify action completed and effective, close action when resolved. Document RCA in incident register.

- **Quality improvement actions from complaints:** For each significant complaint, identify opportunity for quality improvement. Examples: (1) Complaint: patient waited 45 minutes for appointment that was supposed to take 15 minutes. QI action: audit appointment schedule, identify bottlenecks, redesign schedule to allow buffer time, retrain reception on better time estimation. (2) Complaint: patient given wrong antibiotic prescribed in error. QI action: implement prescribing double-check (GP + nurse verify before dispensing for high-risk medications), educate GPs on prescribing common errors, implement alerts in practice software for drug interactions. (3) Complaint: patient privacy breached when receptionist discussed patient condition loudly in waiting room. QI action: redesign reception layout for privacy (discussion area away from waiting room), retrain all staff on privacy protocol, audit practice monthly for privacy breaches. Document QI actions in register, track completion, monitor impact (complaints on same issue should reduce post-implementation).

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Practice Manager / Clinic Manager (complaints coordination, register maintenance) | **Backup:** GP Principal/Owner (complaint approval, serious incident response, AHPRA obligations)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- All complaints received (within 24 hours of receipt — ensure appropriately triaged)
- Serious incident root cause analysis (before finalising, ensure RCA comprehensive)
- Quarterly trend report (identify patterns, approve QI actions)
- Patient feedback themes (quarterly, identify reputational risks or clinical concerns)
- AHPRA notification obligations (any incident involving impairment/misconduct — consult healthcare lawyer)
- Written response to complaint (before sending, verify tone appropriate, content factually accurate)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Complaints Register (spreadsheet or dedicated software: Cognito, SafetyMaster)
- Complaint acknowledgement template (letter confirming receipt and process)
- Complaint response template (letter addressing findings and actions)
- Root Cause Analysis template (5-Whys method, corrective action tracking)
- Risk Register (spreadsheet: ID, description, likelihood, consequence, current controls, review date)
- Quarterly Trend Report (summary of complaints/incidents, pattern analysis, recommendations)
- Patient feedback forms (post-visit surveys, feedback box cards)
- Quality Improvement Action Log (tracking QI initiatives, completion status, impact)
- Google Business Profile and health directory review monitoring (automated alerts or manual monthly check)
- Incident photographs or evidence (if applicable to serious incident)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Complaint not acknowledged within 2 days:** Patient assumes clinic ignoring complaint, dissatisfaction increases. Patient escalates to Health Complaints Commissioner without giving clinic chance to resolve. HCC investigation initiated, more formal and costly for clinic (requires legal response, investigation time, potential finding against clinic). Better outcome if clinic engaged patient early.

- **Serious incident not investigated or root cause identified:** Incident occurs (medication error, diagnostic miss), clinic documents but does not investigate cause. Same error occurs again months later. Pattern not identified. Patient harmed second time. If discovered later, clinic negligent (knew about first incident but did not implement safeguards). Liability claim for second incident likely larger + reputational damage ("clinic didn't learn from first error"). Regulatory action (AHPRA) more likely if pattern evident.

- **Serious incident not notified to AHPRA (when required):** Clinic became aware of GP impairment or serious misconduct, did not notify AHPRA. Months later, another patient harmed by same GP and lodges complaint with AHPRA. AHPRA investigates and discovers clinic had prior knowledge but failed to report. AHPRA investigation now broader (clinic as potential accessory to non-reporting). Clinic may face regulatory action for failing mandatory notification duty.

- **Trend analysis not conducted:** Pattern of complaints not identified (e.g., multiple patients complaining about wait times, or multiple complaints about particular GP's diagnosis accuracy). Root causes remain unaddressed. Issues fester, worsening over time. Patient satisfaction declines, negative online reviews accumulate, clinic reputation damaged, new patient recruitment suffers. By time owner realises pattern, clinic may have lost 20–30% of patient base.

- **Risk register not maintained or reviewed:** Risks identified in previous register become outdated (no longer relevant or controls changed). New risks emerge and not captured (e.g., new software introduces cyber risk, new staff hiring risk if vetting not tight). Clinic blindsided by incident that should have been foreseen and controlled. Example: clinic not monitoring infectious disease (COVID, flu), staff unprepared for outbreak, multiple staff members absent, clinic cannot operate. Risk should have been identified and controls in place (ventilation, infection prevention training).

- **Cold chain breach reported to HCC without clinic investigation:** Patient lodges complaint with HCC claiming vaccine administered improperly (claims cold chain breach). HCC initiates investigation. Clinic has no temperature logs or incident report documenting breach. Unable to defend. HCC finds in patient's favour and recommends clinic improve vaccine storage. Clinic reputation damaged (media may cover complaint).

- **Complaint resolved without correcting underlying system issue:** Individual complaint addressed (staff member retrains, apology given) but system problem remains. Similar issue recurs with different patient. Clinic spends time and money resolving same problem repeatedly. Over 2 years: 5 similar complaints resolved, each requiring investigation and staff retraining, but systemic issue never fixed. Cost and time wasted compared to single root cause fix upfront.

## CATEGORY 18 — ROSTERING / LEAVE / RECRUITMENT / ONBOARDING / OFFBOARDING

### What Has to Be Done

- **GP roster planning (minimum 4 weeks ahead, ideally 8 weeks):** Clinic manager develops GP work schedule at least 4 weeks in advance (8 weeks preferred for consistency and staff satisfaction). Roster specifies: (1) Each GP's working days and hours, (2) Session types (morning surgery, afternoon surgery, home visits, procedural clinic — if applicable), (3) Annual leave planned, (4) Professional development time (conferences, training), (5) On-call or after-hours roster (if clinic provides OOHC), (6) Locum coverage for absence. Rostering principles: (a) Balance GP preferences with clinic demand (adequate staffing for patient access, quality of life for GPs), (b) Consistency (same days/times each week improves patient continuity), (c) Flexibility (accommodate GPs' requests for time off, study leave), (d) Coverage (ensure clinic fully staffed, no under/overstaffing). Electronic rostering system preferred (RosterBob, Deputy, Bizimply) — allows staff to view roster, request changes, alert manager to gaps. Publish final roster 2 weeks before effective date to allow staff planning.

- **Nurse and reception rostering:** Separate rosters for nursing staff (practice nurses, nurse practitioners) and reception/admin staff (receptionists, practice managers, admin officers). Rostering based on: patient visit demand (number of consultation rooms, volume of planned procedures), opening hours (reception must cover 8:00 AM–5:30 PM or extended hours), staff availability and leave requests. Reception roster should balance: check-in/check-out, phone coverage (never leave phones unattended), filing and back-office work, reception area supervision (customer service, waiting area cleanliness). Nurse roster: sufficient nurses for planned procedures, vaccinations, chronic disease management appointments. Gaps in rostering (short staffing) create: poor patient experience (long wait times, missed calls), staff stress and burnout, clinical risk if insufficient nurses for patient volume.

- **Leave request and approval workflow:** Establish formal leave request process: (1) Staff member requests leave (written form or electronic system), specifying date range, leave type (annual, sick, unpaid, study), reason if relevant, (2) Notify manager/roster coordinator, (3) Manager checks roster impact (is clinic adequately staffed without this person?), (4) Manager approves or proposes alternative date if clinic cannot spare staff that date, (5) Approved leave blocked in roster system, staff member receives confirmation. Annual leave allocation: FTC staff entitled to minimum 4 weeks/year (National Employment Standards under Fair Work Act 2009 (Cth)). Part-time staff entitled pro-rata (e.g., 0.8 FTE = 3.2 weeks). GPs may negotiate higher leave (market competitive). Sick leave: minimum 10 days/year (or 38 weeks for part-time) — employees entitled to use for own illness or care of ill family member. Track leave usage (spreadsheet of accrued and used leave per employee) and ensure balance not excessive unplanned absences.

- **Locum GP coordination (Ochre Health Locums, Cornerstone, Medrecruit, or direct):** When GP unavailable (annual leave, sick leave, professional development, covering absence), clinic engages locum (temporary replacement doctor). Locum sourcing via: (a) Locum agencies: Ochre Health Locums (subsidiary of Ramsay Health Care — large network), Cornerstone Medical Recruitment, Medrecruit, Doctors Australia Locum Service. Agencies source suitable locums, handle payroll, credentialing. Clinic pays agency fee + locum rate. Advantage: quick sourcing, agency manages credentialing verification. (b) Direct engagement: clinic contacts freelance locums directly (via RACGP networks, personal contacts). Clinic pays locum agreed rate (typically $250–$500/session depending on location and experience). Disadvantage: clinic responsible for credentialing checks. Locum co-ordination requirements: (1) Notify agency/locum at least 2 weeks before needed, (2) Confirm locum credentials (AHPRA registration, PI insurance, police check), (3) Provide locum with: clinic contact, office protocols, system access instructions, schedule, patient list, (4) Ensure locum completes confidentiality agreement, (5) Brief locum on any key patients with complex issues, (6) Debrief post-session (check any concerns or patient feedback). Document all locum sessions (date, locum name, hours worked, rate paid, patient feedback).

- **GP recruitment pipeline (Seek, RACGP Jobs, Wavelength, Cornerstone Medical Recruitment):** When recruiting new GP (expansion or replacement), clinic engages recruitment process: (1) Write GP role description (full-time vs. part-time, hours, specific responsibilities, patient population), (2) Advertise on job boards: Seek.com.au (largest Australian job board, $300–$500 per listing), RACGP Jobs (Royal Australian College of General Practitioners job board — targets GPs), Wavelength (boutique recruitment firm for medical professionals), Cornerstone Medical Recruitment (medical-specific recruiter), LinkedIn, local medical networks. (3) Collect applications, shortlist suitable candidates (assess: relevant experience, general practice experience, location fit, gap in CV if any), (4) Conduct interviews (first-round phone interview, second-round in-person with multiple staff), (5) Reference checks (contact previous employer, colleagues), (6) Credentialing checks (AHPRA registration, PI insurance, police check, immunisation record). Recruitment timeline: 2–3 months typical from advertise to start date.

- **Credentialing process for new GPs (AHPRA, provider numbers, PI insurance, police check, immunisation status):** All GPs must complete credentialing before commencing work: (1) AHPRA registration verification: confirm GP holds unrestricted registration with Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency. Check AHPRA online register (free public search) — verify: name, registration number, registration status (unrestricted), any conditions on registration, any investigation/sanction history. (2) Provider number: GP must hold Medicare provider number (issued by Services Australia to allow billing of MBS items). Request GP provide proof of provider number or facilitate application. Without provider number, GP cannot bill Medicare (cannot generate clinic revenue from patient visits). (3) Professional indemnity insurance: GP must hold current PI insurance before commencing practice (non-negotiable). Request certificate of currency from GP's insurer (MDA National, MIPS, Avant, etc.). Clinic should verify certificate is valid (not lapsed). Some clinic PI policies require individual GP PI cover. (4) Police check (National Police Certificate): obtain through Australian Federal Police — confirm no criminal history. Requirement: Working with Children Check if GP provides healthcare to children. (5) Immunisation status: verify GP has adequate immunisation (COVID-19 vaccination per TGA requirements, influenza annual, MMR if history unknown, varicella if history unknown, other vaccines per occupational health guidelines). Request copy of immunisation record (vaccination history). Non-compliance: clinic should not employ unvaccinated GPs in general (regulatory and WHS risk). Document all credentialing checks completed and retained in personnel file.

- **System access provisioning on hire:** Before GP/staff member starts, ensure all systems access configured: (1) Practice software (Best Practice, Medical Director, Argus, etc.): create user account, assign permissions (GPs need full access, receptionists need limited access to patient demographics/scheduling, nurses need clinical note access), set password (suggest password manager for security), configure sign-in credentials. (2) Email account: create clinic email address (firstname@dreamclinic.com.au), configure in Outlook/email client. (3) PRODA (Provider Online Digital Access): register with Services Australia if GP will manage own provider number or billing. PRODA ID used for registering with AHPRA, HPOS, ehealth record access, prescription tracking. (4) HPOS (Health Professional Online Services): set up access for pathology ordering, vaccine AIR recording, eMR (electronic Medical Record) access if using AQHF (Australian Quality Health Framework). (5) Secure email/secure messaging: configure encrypted email if clinic uses secure messaging for patient communication (HealthVault, Agora or secure email gateway). (6) Clinic-specific systems: alarm code, printer setup, fax access, shared drive access, VPN if remote access required. Provide staff with system access checklist and verify all logins successful on first day.

- **System access removal on departure:** When staff member departing (resignation, termination, retirement), immediately disable system access (same-day if possible, within 24 hours maximum): (1) Practice software: disable login, ensure no access after departure. (2) Email: disable account, forward to manager email (if needed for continuity), backup sent to external drive for retention. (3) PRODA: deactivate access. (4) HPOS: deactivate. (5) Secure email: deactivate. (6) Clinic-specific systems: reset alarm codes, revoke electronic key card/fob, change shared drive passwords if staff had edit access. (7) VPN and remote access: deactivate. Rationale: prevent security breaches, information leaks, inappropriate access to patient data post-departure. Maintain list of all system access removed and date deactivated (audit trail). Some systems flag access removals to security team (good practice).

- **Offboarding checklist (keys, access cards, final pay, PRODA removal, clinical software deactivation):** Develop formal offboarding checklist completed on staff member's last day: (1) Return items: clinic keys, office access card/fob, mobile phone (if clinic-provided), laptop/computer (if clinic-provided), security badge, parking pass. (2) Financial finalisation: process final pay including accrued but unused annual leave, long service leave if applicable (after 7 years, staff entitled to long service leave — minimum 1 week per 7 years or pro-rata). Provide final pay slip with breakdown of all payments. (3) Superannuation: contribute final super payment (if staff leaving and entitled). Provide member statement. (4) Documentation: provide letter confirming termination date, final payment, superannuation details, reference letter (if requested). (5) System access: verify all access removed (practice software, email, PRODA, HPOS, clinic systems). (6) Confidentiality: remind of ongoing confidentiality obligations post-employment (confidentiality agreement binds even after departure). (7) Data and records: ensure any personal files or documents returned to clinic or securely destroyed per privacy policy. (8) Exit interview (optional but valuable): brief meeting with departing staff to gather feedback on clinic, workplace culture, management, suggestions for improvement. Document interview summary. (9) Sign-off: obtain staff member's signature on offboarding checklist confirming all items completed and property returned. Retain checklist in personnel file.

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Clinic Manager / HR Manager (rostering, recruitment, credentialing) | **Backup:** GP Principal/Owner (recruitment approvals, credentialing verification, offboarding sign-off)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- GP roster 4-week forecast (fortnightly review — ensure adequate coverage, no excessive overtime for individual GPs)
- All new GP credentialing checks completed before commencement (AHPRA verification, provider number, PI insurance, police check, immunisation)
- New staff system access provisioned correctly (test system access within first week)
- Departing staff system access removed (same-day or within 24 hours)
- Annual leave and long service leave accrual tracking (quarterly review — ensure legal compliance and no excessive liability)
- Locum arrangement (verify credentials confirmed before locum commences)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- GP and staff roster (electronic system: RosterBob, Deputy, Bizimply, or spreadsheet)
- Leave request forms and approval log (accrued leave tracking, annual leave balance per staff member)
- Locum engagement record (locum name, dates, hours, rate paid, credentials verified)
- Recruitment job description and job ad (Seek, RACGP Jobs, etc.)
- GP interview notes and reference checks (documented feedback from previous employers)
- Credentialing checklist: AHPRA registration verification, provider number proof, PI insurance certificate, police check, immunisation record
- System access provisioning checklist (all systems configured and tested on hire date)
- System access removal log (deactivation dates and systems removed on departure)
- Offboarding checklist (keys, access cards, final pay, PRODA, system deactivation — signed by departing staff)
- Employment contract (terms, hours, pay, leave entitlements)
- Confidentiality agreement (signed)
- Personnel file (contact details, qualifications, references, performance records)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **New GP not properly credentialed before commencing:** GP starts work without AHPRA verification, provider number unconfirmed, or PI insurance certificate unvalidated. Months later: discovered GP's registration suspended due to complaint (unknown to clinic at hire). Clinic exposed: any patient harm post-suspension becomes clinic liability (negligent hiring for failing due diligence). Or discovered PI insurance lapsed (undetected by clinic). Clinical negligence claim not covered by lapsed PI insurance — clinic personally liable.

- **System access not provisioned before start date:** New GP arrives on first day, cannot log into practice software, cannot access patient records, cannot write prescriptions. Clinical day disrupted, patient care delayed. Poor first impression for new hire, reduces morale. Or system access provisioned with wrong permissions (receptionist given access to clinical notes, patients' privacy at risk).

- **System access not removed on departure:** Departed staff member retains access to practice software, email, patient records. Risk of data breach: departing staff accesses patient information maliciously (revenge for poor treatment), discloses patient information to competitors, or accesses financial information. Privacy breach triggers OAIC notification, regulatory investigation. Or departed staff member accidentally sends confidential patient info in email (account still active, accessed by mistake).

- **Offboarding not completed or keys not returned:** Departing staff member leaves without surrendering office keys, access cards, or clinic property. Keys not deactivated (alarm code unchanged). Risk: departing staff can re-enter clinic after-hours, steal drugs/medical equipment, access patient records, sabotage systems. Security breach, loss of property, patient data breach.

- **Annual leave liability not tracked:** Practice manager does not monitor accrued annual leave balances. Staff accumulates excessive unused leave (e.g., 8 weeks accrued but entitled to 4 weeks/year). When staff departs, clinic forced to pay out unused leave in lump sum. Example: staff accrued 12 weeks leave at $50/hour = $24,000 liability, clinic cash flow impacted if unprepared. Or at separation, employee claims clinic illegally prevented them taking leave (legal claim for underpaid leave entitlements).

- **Roster not planned in advance:** Rostering done ad-hoc, week-to-week. GPs unaware of schedule (poor work-life balance, family planning affected). Staff stress high (irregular hours, last-minute requests). Clinic risks losing GPs to competitors (poor working conditions). Or gaps in roster (under-staffing on busy days, over-staffing on quiet days) — patient access poor, staff idle time, inefficiency.

- **Locum credentials not verified:** Clinic engages locum through agency without requesting verification of AHPRA registration, PI insurance, or police check. Locum provides clinical care without valid qualifications (AHPRA suspended registration unknown to clinic). Patient harmed by unqualified practitioner. Clinic negligent (failed to verify credentials). Liability claim against clinic.

## CATEGORY 19 — BUSINESS DEVELOPMENT / GROWTH / DOCTOR RETENTION / STRATEGIC PLANNING

### What Has to Be Done

- **Patient growth metrics (new patients per month, total active patients, retention rate):** Track and monitor patient numbers to assess clinic growth and health. Monthly reporting: (1) New patients registered (count of new patient registrations per month, target: 15–30 new patients/month for stable clinic, 50+ if growth target). (2) Total active patients (count of patients seen in last 12 months, benchmark for clinic size: solo GP 2,000–3,000 active patients, 5-GP clinic 10,000–15,000 active patients). (3) Patient retention rate (percentage of patients continuing to attend clinic year-on-year, target >85% — less than 85% suggests satisfaction issues). (4) Lost patients (count and analysis: why did patients leave? — moved house, changed GP, left country, found alternative provider). (5) Geographic origin of new patients (are new patients from local area where clinic located, or traveling long distance? — impacts on patient experience and retention). Establish dashboard in Excel or business intelligence tool (Tableau, Power BI) displaying these metrics monthly. Identify trends: if new patient intake declining, marketing may need boost. If retention declining, service quality issues may exist.

- **GP utilisation rates (sessions per week vs available sessions, billing per session):** Measure how effectively clinic GPs are used: (1) Sessions per week: count total GP sessions per week (e.g., 5 GPs × 10 sessions/week each = 50 total sessions). Compare against scheduled sessions — if 55 sessions scheduled but only 50 filled, 5 sessions empty (9% utilisation gap). Target: >85% session utilisation. (2) Billing per session: calculate average Medicare/private billing revenue per GP session (monthly billing $ ÷ number of sessions = $ per session). Benchmark: $250–$400/session depending on case mix. If billing per session declining, may indicate: patients seeing registrars (lower billing), fewer complex cases, bulk-billing reduces out-of-pocket. (3) Identify underutilised GPs: if particular GP has <80% utilisation, investigate why (patient preference, clinical gaps, scheduling issue). Take action: offer targeted marketing to build patient numbers, adjust schedule to align with patient demand, identify clinical development needs if feedback poor.

- **Appointment availability monitoring (next available appointment — target <3 days for acute):** Monitor how quickly patients can access appointments: (1) Track next available appointment metric weekly: what is the earliest appointment a new patient can book? Target: <3 days for acute health issues (same-day or next day preferred for urgent), <7 days for routine appointments. (2) If next available appointment >7 days, clinic has capacity issue (understaffed or poor scheduling). Options: increase GP hours, hire locum or additional GP, review appointment schedule (some clinics block appointments for ongoing care, reducing new patient access). (3) Patient experience: if wait time long, patient satisfaction drops, may choose alternative provider, negative reviews. (4) Track no-show and cancellation rates: percentage of booked appointments where patient does not attend. High no-show rate (>15%) indicates: patients may not value appointment (cultural issue, poor patient education), or difficulty attending (transport, work conflicts, childcare). Implement: SMS reminders day before, confirm appointments at booking, flexible scheduling for working patients (early morning, late afternoon, weekend availability if possible).

- **Service expansion assessment (psychology, physio, dietitian, skin, women's health):** Periodically assess opportunity to add new clinical services: (1) Identify unmet patient needs: do patients frequently request mental health support? (opportunity to employ psychologist or counsellor), chronic pain management (physiotherapy), diabetes management (dietitian), skin concerns (expanded dermatology/procedural clinic), women's health screening (cervical, breast). (2) Market research: survey patients and GPs — what services are wanted? (3) Financial viability: estimate: startup cost (fit-out, equipment, credentialing), operating cost (staff salary, consumables, malpractice insurance), revenue (expected billings, patient demand). Payback period (how long to break-even?). (4) Regulatory check: ensure new service within clinic scope, practitioners licensed appropriately. (5) Accreditation impact: if expanding significantly, may require accreditation update or additional accreditation (RACGP, Australian Diabetes Society, etc.). (6) Staffing: recruit new allied health staff (psychologist, physiotherapist, dietitian) with appropriate qualifications, credentialing, insurance.

- **Online presence management (website, Google Business Profile, HotDoc/HealthEngine listings):** Maintain clinic's digital visibility and reputation: (1) Website: clinic should have professional website with: clinic info (location, hours, phone, email), GPs' profiles (photo, qualifications, special interests), services offered, patient information (how to book, parking, access for disabled patients), online appointment booking integrated (link to HotDoc or clinic software), blog or resources (health tips, patient education — drives SEO). Website design: mobile-responsive (many patients access on phone), fast-load speed, easy navigation, accessibility compliance (WCAG 2.1 AA). Annual review: update GP profiles, services, address any broken links. (2) Google Business Profile: clinic must claim/verify Google Business Profile (free — improved search visibility). Profile includes: clinic location on map, hours, phone, website link, reviews. Encourage patients to leave Google reviews (positive reviews boost ranking). Respond to negative reviews professionally (address concerns, offer resolution — demonstrates customer service). (3) HotDoc, HealthEngine: list clinic on these health directory platforms (patients search for GPs by location, availability). Verify details correct (address, hours, GPs listed), respond to patient reviews. (4) Social media: optional but valuable (Facebook, Instagram): share health tips, announce new services, engage with community. Clinic manager or receptionist can post weekly. Monitor comments and respond professionally.

- **Patient experience monitoring (NPS, Google reviews):** Measure patient satisfaction to identify service improvements: (1) Net Promoter Score (NPS): surveys patients "How likely are you to recommend this clinic to a friend or family member?" on scale 1–10. Calculate NPS = (% promoters [9–10] − % detractors [0–6]). Target NPS > 50 (good), >70 (excellent). Use NPS survey: paper in waiting room or electronic post-visit (email/SMS). (2) Google reviews: monitor and analyse patient reviews on Google Business Profile monthly. Positive reviews highlight clinic strengths (thorough GP, friendly staff, quick appointment access). Negative reviews highlight issues (long wait, rude receptionist, misdiagnosis). Identify themes and address. (3) Patient complaints: analyse complaint register quarterly to identify service issues. (4) Action on feedback: if feedback highlights issue (e.g., long wait times, receptionist rudeness), take corrective action (adjust schedule, retrain staff, invest in additional reception staff if needed). Communicate improvement to patients (follow-up message: "We heard your feedback about wait times and have adjusted our schedule. Thank you for helping us improve."). Demonstrates clinic listens and acts on feedback.

- **GP retention strategy (fair billing split, flexible hours, supportive culture, CPD support):** GPs are clinic's most valuable resource. Retention strategy to keep experienced GPs and attract new talent: (1) Fair billing: if GPs paid on commission basis (common in mixed-ownership clinics), ensure split is fair and competitive. Market rate: 50–70% of billing to GP (varies by location and clinic size). Transparent: GP should know exactly what percentage they receive and how calculated. Avoid opaque billing arrangements (hidden deductions, arbitrary reductions). (2) Flexible hours: offer flexible scheduling (part-time options, job share, preferred days off). GPs with families, study plans, or approaching retirement value flexibility. Strict 9-to-5 rigidity causes burnout and departure. (3) Supportive culture: foster collaborative, non-hierarchical culture. Regular GP meetings, input on clinic direction, respect for clinical autonomy (GPs not micromanaged). Positive workplace culture reduces turnover. (4) Continuing Professional Development (CPD): support GPs' learning and career development. Allocate CPD budget ($2,000–$5,000/year per GP), allow time for training, encourage conference attendance, support fellowship training (RACGP Fellowship, specialist training). GPs who feel stagnant or undervalued leave for better opportunities. (5) Recognition: acknowledge GPs' contributions (performance bonuses, awards, public acknowledgement). (6) Competitive remuneration: pay competitive rates relative to local market (recruit locums and new GPs at competitive rates, benchmark against other clinics). Underpaying drives departures to higher-paying clinics.

- **Competitive landscape monitoring:** Understand local competitive environment to position clinic effectively: (1) Identify competitor clinics (other GPs, clinics in local area), assess: location, opening hours, services offered, GPs employed, online reputation (Google reviews, patient feedback). (2) Competitive advantages: what makes clinic unique? (e.g., 24/7 access, specific services like women's health, procedural capacity, bulk-billing policy, particular GP expertise). (3) Market trends: any new clinics opening nearby (threat to patient base), any services becoming popular (opportunity to expand), patient preferences changing (e.g., telehealth increasing). (4) Benchmarking: compare clinic performance (patient volume, revenue, GP utilisation) against local and national benchmarks (industry reports from RACGP, medical management surveys). (5) Strategic response: if competitor clinic opening nearby, may need to: improve marketing, expand services, adjust pricing, invest in patient experience to retain patients. Monitor competitor clinics' websites, Google reviews, patient feedback to stay informed.

- **Annual strategic plan:** At minimum annually, conduct strategic planning exercise: (1) Review past year: financial performance (revenue, expenses, profit margin), patient metrics (growth, retention, satisfaction), GP utilisation, clinical performance (outcomes, complaints, incidents), staff satisfaction, accreditation status. (2) Identify opportunities: market gaps (unmet patient needs, new services), growth potential (geographic expansion, additional GPs, allied health). (3) Threats: competitor activity, regulatory changes, economic downturn affecting patient base, loss of key staff. (4) Set objectives for next 12 months: e.g., "grow patient base by 10%, expand psychology services, improve patient satisfaction NPS to 60, recruit 1 additional GP, achieve RACGP accreditation". (5) Assign accountability: which leader owns each objective? (6) Timeline: break annual objective into quarterly milestones with accountable person. (7) Review: quarterly review of progress against plan, adjust if needed. Document plan in written document, distribute to leadership team, discuss at quarterly management meetings.

- **Referral network management:** Cultivate relationships with referral sources (other GPs, specialists, allied health, hospitals) to ensure smooth patient pathways: (1) Identify key referral partners: specialists locally (cardiologists, orthopods, psychiatrists, dermatologists), allied health (physiotherapists, psychologists, dietitians), hospitals (emergency department for acute referrals, specific wards if clinic specialises). (2) Establish referral pathways: ensure specialists accept referrals from clinic, understand referral process (paper form vs electronic e-referral, any specific requirements). (3) Two-way communication: request specialists provide reports back to clinic after patient consultation (closure of referral loop). If reports delayed or missing, follow up with specialist admin. (4) Regular contact: maintain relationships with key referral partners (annual catch-up call, email updates, attendance at local healthcare meetings). (5) Feedback loop: if patients return from specialists with concerns (poor communication, felt rushed, unclear advice), feed back to specialist politely (aim to improve quality of referral pathway). (6) Reciprocal referrals: if allied health or other GPs refer patients to clinic, acknowledge and ensure good patient experience (may generate return referrals). (7) Referral metrics: track referral patterns (which specialists receive most referrals, patient feedback on specialist experience) — use to guide clinic partnerships and service development.

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Clinic Manager / Business Manager (data collection, metrics tracking, strategic planning) with GP Principal/Owner (strategy approval, service expansion decisions)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Patient growth and retention metrics (monthly dashboard review — identify trends, assess growth against targets)
- GP utilisation rates (quarterly — ensure sessions adequately filled, investigate underutilisation)
- Appointment availability monitoring (weekly — ensure <3 days for acute, take action if exceeding target)
- Google Business Profile and online reviews (monthly — monitor reputation, respond to reviews)
- Patient satisfaction metrics (NPS, complaints, feedback — quarterly review, identify QI opportunities)
- Service expansion opportunities (annually — assess viability of new services)
- Annual strategic plan (develop annually, review quarterly, adjust as needed)
- GP retention strategy effectiveness (annually — assess staff satisfaction, identify retention risks, approve compensation changes)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Patient metrics dashboard (Excel or BI tool: new patients, active patients, retention rate, lost patients)
- GP utilisation tracking (sessions scheduled vs filled, billing per session, utilisation rate by GP)
- Appointment availability monitoring (weekly report of next available appointment)
- Service expansion assessment document (financial viability, regulatory check, staffing plan)
- Website (clinic website with appointment booking integration, regularly updated)
- Google Business Profile (clinic listing, review monitoring, response log)
- HotDoc/HealthEngine listing verification
- Patient satisfaction survey results (NPS scores, feedback themes)
- Competitor analysis summary (competitive landscape, market positioning)
- Annual Strategic Plan (objectives, accountability, timelines, quarterly review notes)
- Referral network contact list (key specialists, referral pathways, contact details)
- GP retention strategy documentation (billing split, flexible hours policy, CPD budget, recognition practices)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Patient growth not monitored:** Clinic unaware patient base declining. Months pass before owner realises patient numbers down 15% (revenue impacted). By then, too late to take action. Competitor clinic captured patient base, patients switched during period of inattention. Preventable if monthly metrics reviewed.

- **GP utilisation poor:** GP working part-time due to low patient numbers, utilisation 60%, clinic cannot justify keeping GP. GP may depart or reduce hours. If key GP departs, clinic loses revenue and reputation. If utilisation tracked, clinic would have identified issue early, taken action (marketing, adjusted schedule) to grow patient numbers.

- **Appointment availability excessive (>1 week wait):** Patients frustrated by long wait, choose alternative provider, leave negative reviews, clinic reputation damaged. New patient recruitment suffers (read bad reviews, don't book). Preventable if appointment availability tracked weekly.

- **Service expansion not financially assessed:** Clinic invests $50,000 in psychology service (fit-out, staff hiring) without validating market demand or financial viability. Psychologist underutilised (insufficient referral volume), service loses $20,000 in first year, clinic forced to wind down service. Capital wasted. Would have been avoided by proper assessment (market research, financial modelling) before expansion.

- **Online reputation ignored:** Clinic has poor Google reviews (3.5-star rating with complaints about wait times, rude staff), clinic does not respond or take action. Prospective patients read reviews, don't book. Clinic loses new patient inquiries. Online reputation is critical for modern patients — ignoring it costs business.

- **GP satisfaction not addressed:** Senior GP considering retirement, potential to transition retirement gradually (mentoring junior GP, reducing hours). Owner unaware of GP's plans until suddenly receives resignation notice. Clinic loses experienced GP, patient relationships, revenue. Preventable if GP retention strategy in place and regular conversations about satisfaction.

- **Referral pathways not maintained:** Clinic refers patients to specialist, specialist's office never sends report back to clinic. Clinic unaware of patient's outcome, cannot follow up appropriately, gaps in continuity of care. Patient experience poor if clinic cannot discuss specialist findings. Risk: if adverse outcome (specialist missed diagnosis), clinic liable even though didn't directly provide specialist care (referral without closure of loop is poor practice).

## CATEGORY 20 — END-OF-MONTH AND END-OF-YEAR OBLIGATIONS

### What Has to Be Done

- **Month-end billing reconciliation (Medicare claims vs deposits — reconcile within 5 business days of month end):** Upon month-end, clinic reconciles Medicare billing with actual bank deposits received. Process: (1) Extract Medicare billing report from practice software for month (all MBS items billed, amount per item, patient names, dates of service). (2) Cross-check against bank deposits: did clinic receive deposits matching billed amounts? (3) Identify discrepancies: unpaid claims (billed but not yet deposited), rejected claims (claim denied by Medicare, reversed), over/under payments. (4) Investigate gaps: if amount billed $50,000 but only $45,000 deposited, where is $5,000? (delayed payment, rejected claim, data entry error?). (5) Follow up with Medicare: contact Services Australia if claim missing or denied (may need resubmission with corrected documentation). (6) Reconciliation deadline: complete within 5 business days of month-end. If delayed, errors compound (harder to track issues retrospectively). Document reconciliation: spreadsheet matching billed vs deposited with resolution of gaps. File reconciliation report in accounting records (audit trail for accountant).

- **Month-end financial close (P&L draft, expense coding review):** Clinic prepares draft Profit & Loss (P&L) statement for month showing: (1) Revenue: total patient billings (Medicare, private, other sources), (2) Expenses: staff salaries, rent, utilities, consumables, service contracts, insurance, depreciation, (3) Gross profit: revenue − expenses, (4) Net profit/loss: gross profit − tax provisions. Review P&L line-by-line to ensure expenses properly coded (rent coded to rent, not office expenses; staff salaries not mixed with consulting fees, etc.). Identify unusual variations: is a particular expense significantly higher than prior months? (may indicate error, theft, supplier price increase, legitimate higher usage). Approve P&L draft with accountant before finalising (accountant may have adjustments for accruals, depreciation, etc.). Retain draft P&L in accounting records.

- **BAS (Business Activity Statement) preparation (monthly or quarterly depending on turnover — GST collected vs input tax credits):** All Australian businesses with turnover >$75,000/year required to lodge BAS to ATO (Australian Taxation Office) — typically quarterly (3-month periods) for most businesses, monthly for higher-turnover or GST-sensitive businesses. Clinic's BAS reports: (1) GST collected (10% tax on clinic fees charged to patients), (2) GST paid on purchases (clinic can claim credit for GST paid on supplies, rent, services — called input tax credits), (3) Income tax withholding (if clinic had employees, tax withheld from salaries — reported on BAS), (4) Superannuation guarantee contributions. BAS preparation: accountant or bookkeeper prepares BAS (collects data from clinic accounting records, calculates GST position). Clinic reviews BAS before lodgement with accountant (verify amounts correct). Lodgement deadline: 21 days after quarter-end (e.g., for quarter ending 30 June, BAS due by 21 July). Late lodgement triggers ATO penalties. Online lodgement via ATO portal (Australian Business Register — ABR lookup, or tax agent portal if using tax agent).

- **Superannuation contributions (due 28 days after quarter end — 1 Oct, 28 Jan, 28 Apr, 28 Jul):** If clinic has employees, clinic required to contribute superannuation to employees' accounts. Contribution rate: minimum 11.5% of employee's ordinary time earnings (as at July 2024 — rate increases annually). Contribution quarterly: employer calculates super owed for quarter, contributes to employee's nominated super fund. Due dates: 28 days after quarter-end: (1) Quarter 1 (1 Jan–31 Mar): due 28 April, (2) Quarter 2 (1 Apr–30 Jun): due 28 July, (3) Quarter 3 (1 Jul–30 Sep): due 28 October, (4) Quarter 4 (1 Oct–31 Dec): due 28 January. Late contributions: ATO imposes administrative charge ($20) + interest. Super contribution must go to employee's nominated super fund (employee nominates preferred fund, or employer nominates default fund if employee doesn't nominate). Process: accountant/bookkeeper calculates super each quarter (total wages × 11.5%), clinic processes payment to super fund via EFT, obtains receipt/confirmation. Document contributions and retain for audit trail.

- **Payroll tax monthly lodgement (State Revenue Office Victoria — if liable):** Victorian employers may be liable for payroll tax if annual payroll exceeds threshold (for 2024: $10.4 million annual payroll). Most GP clinics under $10.4 million payroll, so not liable. If clinic grows significantly (many GPs, large staff), may become liable for payroll tax. Payroll tax is charged to employer (not deducted from employee wages) at rate 4.85% of payroll above threshold. Lodgement: monthly to Victorian State Revenue Office (via online portal). If liable, accountant manages lodgement and payment.

- **Year-end payroll (EOFY 30 June — finalise STP, issue income statements via myGov):** Australian Financial Year ends 30 June. Year-end payroll obligations: (1) STP (Single Touch Payroll) finalisation: all employers with >10 employees (or >$2 million payroll) required to use STP. STP is real-time payroll reporting to ATO (each pay day, employer lodges employee's gross pay, tax withheld, super, HELP debt, etc. to ATO via payroll software). At EOFY, clinic must finalise STP (reconcile amounts withheld vs final tax liability, submit final STP lodge for financial year). (2) Income statements: by 30 June, employer must provide each employee with income statement (statement of income, tax withheld, super contributions, deductions). Employees use income statements to complete tax returns. Clinics typically provide income statements via myGov (ATO's online platform — employees access via their MyTax account) or printed statements. (3) Tax agent: if clinic uses tax agent, agent assists with finalising payroll and preparing tax return (see next item).

- **Contractor annual payment summaries (if clinic engages contractors):** If clinic engages independent contractors (e.g., locum GPs who are self-employed, cleaning contractors, IT contractors), clinic may be required to issue payment summary if contractor earned >$450 from clinic in financial year. Payment summary specifies: contractor's ABN (Australian Business Number), amount paid, date paid, tax withheld (if any). Lodgement: to ATO by 14 July following financial year-end. Contractor uses payment summary for tax purposes.

- **Tax return preparation (with accountant — typically October lodgement for tax agents):** Clinic's financial year ends 30 June. Tax return (income tax return) due to ATO: (1) If clinic lodges own return: due 31 October (5 months after EOFY). (2) If tax agent lodges return: due 15 May following year (extended deadline — 10 months after EOFY). Most clinics use tax agent (accountant-prepared return). Accountant prepares return showing: clinic income (Medicare, private billings, other), deductible expenses (staff, rent, utilities, depreciation, insurance, professional fees), depreciation (equipment, fit-out), capital gains/losses if applicable. Return calculates clinic's taxable income and tax liability. Clinic reviews return with accountant before lodgement, approves, and signs return. ATO may audit clinic's return (especially if large deductions or inconsistencies) — retain all supporting documentation (invoices, receipts, contracts) for 5+ years.

- **WorkCover premium renewal (annual):** WorkCover insurance premium calculated and due annually, usually around June-July (varies by policy anniversary date). Premium is based on: clinic's payroll for prior financial year (reported to WorkCover at EOFY) and industry classification code (6211 for medical practitioners). ATO sends WorkCover premium bill, clinic pays premium before due date (usually within 30 days). Premium varies year-to-year based on: payroll size (more staff = higher premium), claims history (claims increase future premiums), industry risk rating. Clinic may receive refund or be owed money if payroll overestimated or underestimated. Review premium bill with accountant, ensure correct payroll reported, challenge if premium seems excessive (compare to prior years, benchmark against similar clinics).

- **PIP annual attestation (via HPOS):** PIP (Practice Incentives Program) is Australian Government program providing incentive payments to general practices meeting quality and compliance standards. Clinic receiving PIP payments required to attest annually to compliance with PIP requirements. Attestation completed via HPOS (Health Professional Online Services): clinic (usually Practice Manager or GP principal) logs in, confirms compliance with PIP rules (patient enrolments, after-hours care, chronic disease management, cervical screening rates, GP immunisation status per guidelines, privacy/confidentiality, etc.). Attestation deadline: typically 31 October for prior financial year. If clinic not attesting by deadline, PIP payments may be suspended. Accountant usually reminds clinic of attestation deadline.

- **RACGP membership renewals:** GPs maintaining RACGP (Royal Australian College of General Practitioners) membership pay annual membership fee (approximately $1,500–$2,000 depending on membership category). Renewal typically due each July. GPs responsible for own renewal, but clinic manager should track GP renewals (ensure GPs maintain membership, required for accreditation). Invoice reminder sent by RACGP. If GP membership lapses, clinic may lose accreditation or eligibility for certain incentive programs.

- **Asset register update:** Clinic maintains asset register (list of capital items: buildings, equipment, vehicles, computers, fit-out). At year-end, update register with: (1) New assets purchased in financial year, (2) Assets disposed/sold (remove from register), (3) Depreciation recorded (accountant calculates annual depreciation for tax and accounting purposes). Asset register used for: tax depreciation (deductible each year), financial reporting (balance sheet shows net assets), insurance (contents insurance premium based on asset value). Review asset register annually with accountant, ensure completeness and accuracy.

- **Budget preparation for new financial year:** As financial year-end approaches (June), clinic begins budgeting for next financial year (1 July–30 June). Budget process: (1) Review prior year actual performance (revenue, expenses, profit), (2) Project next year: expected patient volume growth, revenue increase, expected wage/salary increases (e.g., award increases), expected expense changes (utilities, rent increases), capex plans (equipment purchases, fit-out). (3) Set targets: revenue target (how much billing to achieve?), expense budget (staff, rent, supplies, consumables), profit margin target (desired profit as % of revenue). (4) Identify risks/opportunities: are there planned GP departures? (revenue loss). Expected new GP recruitment? (expense increase then revenue increase). New services to launch? (startup costs, new revenue stream). (5) Approve budget: management team (GP principal, clinic manager, accountant) approves budget for new financial year. (6) Use budget for monitoring: each month, actual results compared to budget (variance analysis — is clinic on track?), identify issues early, adjust operations if needed.

### Who Usually Does It
**Primary:** Accountant / Bookkeeper (BAS, tax return, payroll, super, asset register) with Business Manager / Clinic Manager (month-end reconciliation, PIP attestation, budget preparation) | **Backup:** GP Principal/Owner (approval of financial statements, budget, tax return)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Month-end P&L statement (review within 5 days of month-end — identify unusual variances, approve before finalising)
- Quarterly BAS before lodgement (verify GST position, income withholding accurate)
- Annual tax return with accountant (review all income and deductions, approve and sign before lodgement)
- Annual super contributions (verify all employees received correct contributions by due dates — spot check 3–5 employees)
- WorkCover premium (review annual bill, ensure correct payroll reported, challenge if excessive)
- PIP annual attestation (confirm compliance with PIP requirements, authorise HPOS attestation)
- Year-end asset register (verify major assets captured, depreciation calculation reasonable)
- Annual budget (develop collaboratively with accountant and management, approve before 1 July)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Month-end billing reconciliation spreadsheet (Medicare claims vs bank deposits, discrepancies identified and resolved)
- Draft P&L statement (monthly, reviewed with accountant)
- BAS lodgement (quarterly — copy of BAS submission, ATO receipts)
- Superannuation contribution records (quarterly statements from super funds, contribution confirmation receipts)
- Payroll tax compliance (if liable — monthly lodgement receipts)
- STP (Single Touch Payroll) — integration with payroll software, monthly STP files submitted to ATO, EOFY finalisation
- Employee income statements (issued by 30 June, copies retained)
- Contractor payment summaries (if applicable)
- Tax agent agreement and tax return (prepared by accountant, signed by clinic owner, ATO lodgement confirmation)
- WorkCover premium bill and payment confirmation
- PIP attestation (HPOS record of annual attestation)
- RACGP membership tracking (reminders for GP renewals)
- Asset register (spreadsheet: item, purchase date, cost, depreciation, net book value)
- Annual budget (document prepared at EOFY, approved by management, used for monthly variance analysis)
- Monthly variance reports (actual vs budget, variance analysis and commentary)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Billing reconciliation not completed:** Clinic bills $50,000 but only receives $45,000 deposit (month-end). If reconciliation not done, clinic doesn't identify $5,000 shortfall. Months pass, shortfall compounded (multiple months of unreconciled billings). Accountant cannot complete accurate financial statements (revenue wrong). Tax return filed with incorrect revenue. ATO audit discovers revenue underreported, penalties assessed. Preventable if reconciliation completed monthly.

- **BAS lodged late (>21 days after quarter-end):** ATO assesses penalty ($20–$100+). If pattern of late lodgement, ATO may pursue clinic for penalties and interest. Also affects cashflow: GST refunds (if clinic overpaid GST) delayed if BAS late.

- **Super contributions not made by due date (28 days after quarter-end):** ATO imposes administrative charge ($20) + interest on late contributions. Employees dissatisfied (their super account not credited on time — impacts their retirement savings). If repeated non-payment, ATO may prosecute employer for failing statutory super obligation (criminal penalties possible if egregious).

- **Tax return not filed by deadline:** If clinic lodges own return (due 31 Oct), failure to lodge triggers penalties. If tax agent lodges (due 15 May), extended deadline more forgiving. However, if clinic owes tax and doesn't file, ATO pursues for unpaid tax + penalties + interest (debt accumulates).

- **Asset register not updated:** Clinic purchases $30,000 equipment (autoclave), not recorded in asset register. Tax depreciation not claimed (clinic entitled to deduction, not taken). Tax return filed without depreciation deduction. Clinic pays more tax than necessary (thousands in lost deductions over time). Also, for insurance purposes, if equipment not listed, may not be covered if equipment damaged/stolen (claim denied due to uninsured asset).

- **Year-end payroll not finalised (STP not reconciled, income statements not issued):** Employees cannot complete tax returns (don't have income statement showing tax withheld). Employee tax return delayed. If discrepancy between STP lodged and actual tax withheld, tax return may be incorrect. ATO audit employees' returns, discovers mismatch, penalties assessed (to employee, but clinic liable if employer error).

- **Budget not prepared for new financial year:** Clinic starts new financial year with no targets or projections. During year, expenses not controlled (spending ad-hoc, no approval process). By end of year, clinic discovers loss or reduced profit due to unchecked spending. Preventable with budget (spending authorised against budget, variances investigated).

Quarterly management meetings should systematically review progress across all 20 categories — establish governance rhythm to prevent items slipping through cracks.
