# PART 1 — EXECUTIVE OPERATOR OVERVIEW

## Section 1.1 — How a 4–5 FTE GP Clinic Functions as a Business

### Revenue Model & Financial Scale

A typical 4–5 FTE (full-time equivalent) general practice in Victoria operates as a small services business with highly variable revenue depending on billing model and patient demographics.

**Billing Models:**
- **Bulk-billing clinics:** GPs receive payment directly from Medicare for eligible services; patient pays zero gap. Operator's revenue = Medicare fee schedule × activity + rebates for chronic disease programs. Simpler administration but lower margins and patient volume dependency.
- **Mixed-billing clinics:** GPs charge patient directly; patient receives Medicare rebate and pays the gap (difference). Allows higher per-consultation revenue but requires active collections and impacts some patient demographics. Higher administration overhead.
- **Private clinics:** No Medicare; all revenue from patients. Highest margins but smallest market, requires strong reputation/location.

**Typical Annual Revenue Range (4–5 FTE):**
- Bulk-billing, average demographics: $1.5M–$2.2M annually
- Mixed-billing, average demographics: $2.0M–$3.0M annually
- High-volume bulk + chronic disease programs: $2.5M–$3.5M annually
- *Source: Expert knowledge. External verification needed via AMA, RACGP survey data, and local practice benchmarking.*

**Medicare as Revenue Driver:**
- Medicare rebates comprise 75–85% of gross revenue in bulk-billing clinics, 60–70% in mixed-billing
- Remaining revenue: private services (travel medicine, work cover, insurance medicals), allied health, rent from subtenants, ancillary (pathology, dispensing)
- *Source: Expert knowledge.*

### Cost Structure & Operating Margins

A sustainable 4–5 FTE practice typically operates on gross margins of 35–55%, with EBITDA (earnings before interest, tax, depreciation, amortisation) of 20–35%.

**Typical Cost Breakdown (as % of gross billings):**

| Cost Category | % of Billings | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **GP Payments** | 30–35% | Largest single expense; FTE-dependent. Contractor GPs typically 40–45% of their own billings; employee GPs 50–60% incl. on-costs. |
| **Nurse Wages** (RN/EN/Immunisation) | 8–12% | Scales with staffing. Junior staff cheaper; specialized (aged care, immunisation) command premium. |
| **Reception/Admin Wages** | 5–8% | Includes payroll tax, superannuation. Multi-reception practice higher %. |
| **Rent & Utilities** | 8–12% | Highly variable by location (CBD vs suburb). Include rates, water, electricity, internet. |
| **Software & IT** | 2–3% | Clinical software (EHR), phone, backup, cybersecurity, medical indemnity insurance. |
| **Consumables** | 3–5% | Vaccinations, swabs, needles, gloves, sharps disposal, cleaning. |
| **Insurance (excluding indemnity)** | 1–2% | Public liability, professional indemnity, income protection, cyber liability. |
| **Administration & Professional** | 2–3% | Accountant, payroll processor, HR/legal advice, compliance consultants. |
| **Marketing & Business Development** | 0.5–1.5% | Patient recruitment, signage, website, referral networks. |
| **Other Operating** | 2–4% | Repairs, equipment lease, pathology courier, waste disposal, bank fees. |
| **TOTAL OPERATING COSTS** | 62–75% | Residual = EBITDA (gross profit available for owner return + reinvestment) |

*Source: Expert knowledge. Percentages vary by clinic model, efficiency, and local market.*

**Key Financial Reality:**
- A $2.5M clinic with 35% GP payments, 10% staff, 10% rent, and 5% other operating costs = ~40% EBITDA (~$1M before tax and owner drawings)
- Cash flow timing is critical: Medicare pays on 14–21-day cycle; payroll is weekly/fortnightly; lease is monthly
- *Source: Expert knowledge.*

### Goodwill & Patient List Valuation

The value of a general practice is driven by two factors: the **patient list** (recurring revenue from enrolled patients) and **goodwill** (reputation, systems, GP relationships).

**EBITDA Multiple Approach:**
- Typical Australian GP practice valuation: **3.5–6.0x EBITDA** depending on:
  - Practice stability (years operating, GP tenure, patient growth)
  - Billing model (bulk-billing slightly lower multiple; mixed-billing higher)
  - Specialisation (chronic disease programs, allied health, aged care increase value)
  - Location (metro higher; rural lower unless isolation loading applies)
  - Receivables quality and collection rate
- *Source: Expert knowledge. External verification needed via practice valuation firms (e.g., Pitcher Partners, Grant Thornton healthcare valuations).*

**Patient List as Asset:**
- A "healthy" patient list has 85%+ active consulting rate (seen in last 12 months)
- Breakup of patient list between long-term patients (low churn) vs transient (high churn) affects stability
- Chronic disease programs (diabetes, cardiovascular, respiratory) improve patient stickiness and revenue per patient
- *Source: Expert knowledge.*

**Goodwill Components:**
- GP employment relationships and non-compete agreements
- Clinical accreditation (RACGP, QIP)
- IT systems and workflows
- Staff relationships (especially practice manager and nursing team)
- Referral relationships (specialists, allied health, pathology)
- *Source: Expert knowledge.*

### Bulk-Billing vs Mixed-Billing: Operator Perspective

| Factor | Bulk-Billing | Mixed-Billing |
|---|---|---|
| **Patient Out-of-Pocket** | Zero | Gap charge (varies by GP) |
| **Operator Cash Flow** | Medicare 14–21 days; predictable | Patient payment + Medicare rebate; collections risk |
| **Patient Demographics** | Broader; includes pensioners, low-income | Skews older, higher income, private insurance holders |
| **Consultation Volume** | Higher (lower per-consult margin) | Lower (higher per-consult margin) |
| **Revenue per FTE** | $400K–$550K FTE | $500K–$700K FTE |
| **GP Motivation** | Volume-driven | Quality-driven; fewer patients, longer consults |
| **Collection Risk** | Minimal | Moderate; 5–10% of patients defaulting on gaps |
| **IT Overhead** | Moderate (bulk-billing compliance tracking) | Higher (rebate management, accounts receivable) |
| **Patient Satisfaction** | High (no cost barrier) | Variable (some resent gap charging) |
| **Sustainability** | Vulnerable to Medicare rebate cuts | More resilient to rebate changes |

*Source: Expert knowledge.*

### Key Financial Ratios the Owner Must Track

**1. Billings Ratio (per GP FTE)**
- Formula: Gross monthly billings ÷ FTE count
- Healthy range: $40K–$60K per FTE per month (varies by bulk vs mixed)
- Red flag: <$35K/month/FTE = underutilisation or patient loss

**2. Collections Rate (mixed-billing only)**
- Formula: Patient gap payments received ÷ patient gap charges issued
- Healthy range: 92–98%
- Red flag: <90% = collections problem or bad-debt exposure

**3. Cost-to-Income Ratio**
- Formula: Total operating expenses ÷ gross billings
- Healthy range: 60–70%
- Red flag: >75% = margin compression; operational inefficiency

**4. Staff Cost Ratio**
- Formula: (Nurse + reception + admin wages) ÷ gross billings
- Healthy range: 13–20%
- Red flag: >22% = overstaffing or low utilisation

**5. EBITDA Margin**
- Formula: EBITDA ÷ gross billings
- Healthy range: 25–40%
- Red flag: <20% = below-market performance; review pricing, volume, costs

**6. Days Cash on Hand**
- Formula: (Current bank balance) ÷ (daily operating expenses)
- Healthy range: 20–35 days
- Red flag: <10 days = acute cash flow risk; <5 days = crisis level

**7. Receivables Aging (mixed-billing)**
- % of invoiced gaps >60 days old
- Healthy range: <5%
- Red flag: >15% = collections breakdown

**8. Medicare Compliance Billing Accuracy**
- % of claims initially accepted (not rejected/required amendment)
- Healthy range: >98%
- Red flag: <95% = billing leakage; potential audit risk

**9. Patient Churn Rate (Active to Inactive)**
- Formula: Patients not seen in 12 months ÷ total patient list
- Healthy range: 15–20% annually
- Red flag: >30% = patient loss; investigate cause

**10. GP Retention**
- Months/years in role for each GP
- Healthy range: >3 years median tenure
- Red flag: Average <18 months = high turnover risk

*Source: Expert knowledge. Recommend quarterly financial review with accountant.*

## Section 1.2 — Main Management Domains

An effective operator must oversee eight interconnected domains. Each is critical; weakness in any one undermines the whole business.

### 1. Clinical Governance (Accreditation, Credentialing, Clinical Risk)

**Purpose:** Ensure clinical quality, patient safety, and regulatory compliance.

**What This Covers:**
- RACGP accreditation and Quality Improvement Plan (QIP) compliance
- GP registration and scope of practice (registration with AHPRA)
- Credentialing of clinical staff (nurses, allied health)
- Clinical protocols, guidelines, and evidence-based practice
- Incident reporting and risk management
- Patient safety systems (medication safety, infection control)
- Peer review and GP performance monitoring
- Clinical supervision (especially for junior GPs, nurses)
- Mandatory reporting (child abuse, elder abuse, patient safety concerns)
- Professional development and continuing medical education (CME) compliance

**Why Owner Must Oversee:**
- Accreditation lapse = cannot bill Medicare = instant revenue loss
- Clinical incident = liability, regulatory investigation, reputation damage
- Non-compliance with mandatory reporting = personal criminal liability
- *Source: Expert knowledge.*

---

### 2. Human Resources (GPs, Nurses, Admin, Contractors)

**Purpose:** Recruit, manage, develop, and retain clinical and administrative staff.

**What This Covers:**
- GP contracting and employment agreements (including restraint of trade)
- Nurse and admin recruitment, onboarding, probation, performance management
- Contractor vs employee classification (avoiding sham contracting)
- Industrial relations compliance (National Employment Standards, Awards)
- Performance management and disciplinary processes
- Professional development and training
- Succession planning and knowledge transfer
- Staff turnover management and exit interviews
- Workplace culture and conflict resolution
- Occupational health and safety (bullying, harassment, stress management)

**Why Owner Must Oversee:**
- GP departure = instant 15–25% revenue loss (typically)
- Unfair dismissal claim = legal costs, damages, admin burden
- Award non-compliance = back-pay liability + penalties
- Sham contracting = ATO liability + penalties + reputational damage
- *Source: Expert knowledge.*

### 3. Finance and Cashflow (Billings, Reconciliation, BAS, Payroll)

**Purpose:** Manage revenue, payments, banking, and financial obligations.

**What This Covers:**
- Weekly/monthly revenue billing and reconciliation (Medicare, patient, private)
- Bank account management and cash flow forecasting
- Payroll processing (wages, superannuation, tax withholding)
- Business Activity Statement (BAS) compliance (quarterly GST, PAYG, superannuation)
- Accounts receivable (gap invoicing, collections, write-offs)
- Accounts payable (supplier invoices, rent, utilities)
- GP contractor payments and GST reconciliation
- Financial reporting (P&L, balance sheet, cash position)
- Tax planning and compliance (income tax, GST, superannuation guarantee)
- Audit and review of financial statements

**Why Owner Must Oversee:**
- Cash flow mismanagement = inability to pay staff/suppliers (insolvency risk)
- BAS non-compliance = ATO penalties, interest, potential prosecution
- Payroll errors = staff disputes, ATO audits, reputational damage
- Billing leakage = underreported income = lost revenue + tax audit exposure
- *Source: Expert knowledge.*

## Section 1.3 — Biggest Operational Risks for an Owner-Operator

A 4–5 FTE practice is vulnerable to 12 major operational risks. Each can cause significant financial or reputational harm within weeks.

### 1. GP Departure (Revenue Loss)

**Risk:** One or two GPs generate 50–70% of clinic revenue. Sudden departure = 15–25% revenue loss + emergency workload redistribution + patient complaints.

**Why It Happens:** Competing practice offers better terms, GP relocates, health/burnout issues, conflict with owner/team, maternity leave.

**Consequence:** Immediate cash flow gap (Medicare already billed to departing GP), staff morale impact, patient loss (5–15% follow departing GP), difficulty recruiting replacement at short notice.

**Mitigation:** Documented employment/contracting terms with reasonable notice period; strong peer support; competitive remuneration; non-compete agreement (if enforceable); relationship management.

*Source: Expert knowledge.*

---

### 2. Medicare Non-Compliance / Audit

**Risk:** Services Australia audits billing patterns; finds non-compliant claims (wrong item numbers, ineligible services, invalid provider details, missing documentation). Clinic receives debt notice for repayment + penalties.

**Why It Happens:** Staff error in billing; GP unfamiliar with item restrictions; intentional/reckless billing; poor documentation.

**Consequence:** Repayment of claimed amounts (potentially $50K–$500K+); audit costs (legal, accountancy); reputational damage; potential provider suspension.

**Mitigation:** Monthly billing audits; staff training on item restrictions; PRODA compliance checks; documentation standards; professional indemnity insurance.

*Source: Expert knowledge; external verification needed via Services Australia.*

---

### 3. Billing Leakage (Under-Billing / Missed Items)

**Risk:** GPs bill at lower item number than warranted, or forget to bill ancillary items (e.g., TCAs, aged care reviews, chronic disease programs). Clinic leaves 5–15% of revenue on the table.

**Why It Happens:** Time pressure; unfamiliarity with current item schedule; staff error in claims processing; GP habit or preference.

**Consequence:** Lost revenue (e.g., $50K–$150K annually for a $2M clinic); depressed profitability; cash flow impact.

**Mitigation:** Regular billing audits (monthly); GP education on item numbers; clinical software configuration to prompt correct items; rebate reconciliation.

*Source: Expert knowledge.*

---

### 4. Key Person Dependency (Practice Manager / Single GP)

**Risk:** Practice manager or single high-revenue GP has no backup. Sudden absence (illness, accident, departure) cripples operations.

**Why It Happens:** Lack of succession planning; knowledge not documented; no cross-training of staff.

**Consequence:** Operational chaos; delayed billing; missed compliance deadlines; patient appointments cancelled; staff stress and errors.

**Mitigation:** Document all key processes; cross-train staff; have contingency plan for practice manager absence; identify backup GP or recruitment plan.

*Source: Expert knowledge.*

---

### 5. Cash Flow Gaps (Medicare Lag vs Payroll Obligations)

**Risk:** Medicare pays 14–21 days after claim submission. Payroll is weekly/fortnightly. Large unpaid gap invoices (mixed-billing) mean cash outflows exceed inflows. Clinic runs short of cash.

**Why It Happens:** Low opening cash balance; slow patient collections; unexpected expense; high GP contractor payments reducing net cash retained.

**Consequence:** Unable to meet payroll, rent, or supplier invoices; staff stress; potential insolvency; overdraft fees.

**Mitigation:** Cash flow forecasting (rolling 13-week forecast); maintain minimum 20–30 days of operating expenses in reserve; line of credit facility available; monitor receivables aging; negotiate payment terms with suppliers.

*Source: Expert knowledge.*

---

### 6. Accreditation Failure (Loss of Eligibility to Operate)

**Risk:** Practice loses RACGP accreditation due to non-compliance with QIP standards or failure in accreditation audit. Loss of accreditation = cannot bill Medicare = clinic becomes unviable.

**Why It Happens:** Non-compliance with clinical governance standards; gaps in documentation, infection control, or safety procedures; GP doesn't hold sufficient CPD points; management neglect of accreditation obligations.

**Consequence:** Immediate loss of Medicare billing; 4–8 week accreditation reapplication process; interim revenue loss; reputational damage; staff concern about clinic viability.

**Mitigation:** Appoint accreditation/quality lead (internal or external consultant); conduct annual self-audit against QIP standards; maintain documentation; monitor GP CPD compliance; plan accreditation audit 6 months ahead.

*Source: Expert knowledge; external verification needed via RACGP.*

---

### 7. Privacy Breach / Cyber Incident

**Risk:** Patient data is accessed, stolen, encrypted by ransomware, or shared without consent. Clinic must notify affected patients + OAIC + media. Regulatory investigation follows.

**Why It Happens:** Weak cybersecurity (no MFA, poor antivirus, outdated software); staff phishing (click malicious link, enter credentials); ransomware attack; disgruntled staff member copying data.

**Consequence:** Notification costs, legal fees, regulatory fine, reputational damage (patient loss), potential clinical software downtime, cyber insurance excess/deductible, forensic investigation costs.

**Mitigation:** Strong IT security (MFA, antivirus, regular backups, secure offsite storage); staff training on phishing; cyber liability insurance; incident response plan; regular security audits.

*Source: Expert knowledge.*

---

### 8. Sham Contracting Exposure (ATO / IR Liability)

**Risk:** Contractor GPs are classified as employees by ATO or Fair Work. Clinic liable for unpaid superannuation, PAYG tax, and penalties.

**Why It Happens:** Contractor has tools provided, fixed hours, direct supervision, integration into team (control tests suggest employment relationship); no genuine independent contractor structure.

**Consequence:** Back-payment of super (often 11.5% of earnings for past 6 years) + interest + penalties; ATO audit; reputational damage; potential IR claim for unfair dismissal rights.

**Mitigation:** Proper contractor agreement (genuine independent contractor terms); GP uses own tools/equipment; flexible hours; no direct supervision; indemnity insurance; legal review of contractor status.

*Source: Expert knowledge; external verification needed via ATO, Fair Work Ombudsman.*

---

### 9. Cold Chain Breach (Vaccine Spoilage / Regulatory Consequences)

**Risk:** Vaccine refrigerator failure or improper storage leads to spoilage of stock (e.g., HPV, MMR, COVID vaccines). Clinic unaware vaccines are compromised. Patients unknowingly receive ineffective vaccines.

**Why It Happens:** Refrigerator malfunction; temperature logs not monitored; vaccine handling error (left out of fridge); poor staff training on cold chain.

**Consequence:** Patient safety incident (reputational, regulatory); clinic liable to replace vaccines; potential TGA notification; accreditation impact; staff liability; mandatory reporting of vaccine failure.

**Mitigation:** Quality refrigerator with alarm and temperature monitoring; daily temperature logs (electronic preferred); staff training on cold chain; regular fridge maintenance; regular stock audits; backup fridge on site.

*Source: Expert knowledge; external verification needed via TGA, NACI.*

---

### 10. Lease Risk (Non-Renewal / Make-Good Costs)

**Risk:** Lease term expires. Landlord does not offer renewal. Clinic has 60–90 days to find and fit-out new premises or close. Make-good obligations require expensive reinstatement (painting, carpet, fitouts).

**Why It Happens:** Landlord redevelops building; competing tenant offers higher rent; poor landlord relationship.

**Consequence:** Relocation costs ($50K–$200K+); business disruption (patient appointments cancelled); staff turnover (staff won't relocate); make-good disputes with landlord; temporary revenue loss during transition.

**Mitigation:** Lease terms reviewed 12 months before expiry; maintain good landlord relationship; negotiate renewal option in lease; budget for make-good; have site contingency plan.

*Source: Expert knowledge.*

---

### 11. Staff Conflict / Unfair Dismissal Claim

**Risk:** Manager dismisses staff member (nurse, reception, admin). Employee claims unfair dismissal (no warnings, discriminatory reason, inadequate process). Clinic must defend claim in Fair Work Commission.

**Why It Happens:** Poor performance management process; manager personality conflict; disability or cultural discrimination; inadequate documentation of performance issues.

**Consequence:** Legal costs ($10K–$50K+); reputational damage (staff culture); management distraction; potential compensation order; appeal process (months of uncertainty).

**Mitigation:** Document performance issues; follow fair dismissal procedures (warnings, opportunity to respond); HR advice; ensure legitimate reason for dismissal; maintain good staff relations.

*Source: Expert knowledge.*

---

### 12. Payroll Non-Compliance (Award Underpayment / Super Gaps)

**Risk:** Clinic pays staff below Modern Award minimum rates or misses superannuation contributions. Staff or FWO investigation reveals breach.

**Why It Happens:** Payroll system misconfiguration; casual staff paid flat hourly rate (missing super); incorrect leave calculations; poor payroll processor understanding of Award.

**Consequence:** Back-payment of wages + interest; super contributions + interest + penalties; FWO investigation; reputational damage; staff grievances; potential compensation claim.

**Mitigation:** Payroll processed by qualified payroll service; annual payroll audit against Medical Practitioners Award; super contributions verified quarterly; staff paid slips reviewed; HR advice on leave entitlements.

*Source: Expert knowledge; external verification needed via Fair Work Ombudsman, ATO.*

# PART 2 — CLINIC MANAGEMENT STRUCTURE

## Overview

An effective clinic requires clear roles, decision rights, and reporting lines. The following sections define 18 key roles within the clinic ecosystem. Each role is either internal (employed/engaged directly), external (contracted services), or hybrid (flexible arrangement).

**Organisational Design Principles:**
- Owner retains ultimate decision authority and oversight of financial/compliance/clinical domains
- Practice Manager acts as day-to-day operational lead, reporting to Owner
- Clinical leadership (senior GP or clinical lead) oversees GP roster and clinical governance
- Each functional area has a named primary contact and documented backup/escalation path
- Clear decision rights (autonomous vs escalate) prevent bottlenecks and ensure owner visibility on material items
- *Source: Expert knowledge.*

---

## Section 2.1 — Owner / Director / Chief Operator

**Core Purpose:**
The owner is the ultimate decision-maker, risk-bearer, and strategic lead. The owner holds Medicare provider number(s), is accountable for accreditation compliance, cashflow, staff relations, and clinic viability. The owner may be a GP (operating doctor) or a non-clinical business owner.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Strategic Planning & Vision**: Set clinic direction (billing model, services, growth targets, 3–5 year plan)
- **Financial Oversight**: Weekly review of billings, cash position, monthly P&L and cash flow forecast; quarterly financial review with accountant
- **Budgeting & Forecasting**: Annual budget; rolling 13-week cash flow forecast; approval of major capex (>$10K)
- **GP Recruitment & Management**: Recruit GPs, negotiate terms (contractor or employee), manage performance, plan succession
- **Practice Manager Oversight**: Hire, manage performance, provide strategic direction; escalation point for major operational issues
- **Medicare Compliance & Audit Response**: Maintain provider number, respond to Services Australia audits, ensure PRODA compliance
- **Accreditation Responsibility**: Ultimate accountability for RACGP accreditation, QIP compliance; owner signature on accreditation documents
- **Lease & Facilities**: Negotiate and sign lease, approve major maintenance, plan relocation if needed
- **Insurance & Risk**: Obtain and review professional indemnity, cyber liability, public liability, property; annually review coverage
- **Staff Relations & Industrial Relations**: Approve employment/contracting terms, handle staff conflicts, respond to complaints/claims
- **Clinical Governance**: Set clinical standards, approve protocols, oversee critical incidents, maintain patient safety focus
- **Board/Shareholder Communications**: If practice is part of larger group, report to board, manage corporate compliance
- **Business Development**: Set revenue targets, approve new services, approve partnerships/subtenancies
- **Regulatory & Compliance**: Monitor legislative changes (Privacy Act, Fair Work, Medicare, TGA); ensure clinic remains compliant

**Decisions Owner Can Make Autonomously:**
- Approve expenditure >$10K
- Hire/dismiss practice manager or senior staff (after HR/legal advice)
- Change billing model or fee setting
- Negotiate GP contractor terms and rates
- Approve new services or subtenancies
- Approve major marketing/business development initiatives
- Approve lease renewal or relocation
- Approve audit response to regulator
- Appoint external advisors (accountant, lawyer, HR consultant)

**Decisions That Require Escalation:**
- Owner decisions do not escalate; however, if owner is not a clinician, owner may seek clinical advice from senior GP on clinical governance matters

**Reports/Data Owner Should Receive:**

- Daily: Bank balance, yesterday's billings
- Weekly: Full GP roster (next 4 weeks), cash flow forecast (13-week rolling), payroll status, any urgent customer complaints or incidents
- Monthly: Financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow), billing audit results, receivables aging, staff turnover/recruitment status, complaints summary, accreditation/compliance calendar check
- Quarterly: Full financial review with accountant, strategic review of KPIs (billings per FTE, EBITDA margin, patient churn, staff retention), forward business plan

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Lack of strategic direction; clinic drifts
- Financial mismanagement; cash flow crisis not detected until too late
- GP turnover accelerates; no succession plan
- Compliance lapses (accreditation, Medicare, payroll); regulatory risk
- Staff morale declines; operational chaos
- Clinic valuation deteriorates; saleable business at risk

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house (essential)**: Owner must be present or have clearly delegated operating authority. If owner is absent or non-engaged, designate a proxy with clear authority limits.
- **Outsourced elements**: Finance (bookkeeping, payroll, tax), HR/legal advice (as needed), IT support, facilities/maintenance contractor

## Section 2.2 — Practice Manager

**Core Purpose:**
The practice manager is the owner's operational lieutenant. PM is responsible for day-to-day clinic operations, staff management, compliance tracking, patient-facing service, and administrative systems. PM is the single point of contact for staff, patients, and external stakeholders on operational matters.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Clinic Operations**: Oversee daily scheduling, patient flow, waiting times, appointment adherence; ensure clinic runs on time
- **Staff Scheduling & Rostering**: Create weekly/monthly rosters for nurses and reception; manage leave requests, absences, backfill; ensure adequate coverage
- **Staff Management (Non-Clinical)**: Hire reception/admin/cleaning staff (with owner approval); conduct performance reviews; handle day-to-day performance issues; induct and train new staff
- **Patient Communications**: Field patient complaints, manage escalations, ensure timely resolution; feedback loop to GPs on patient concerns
- **Billing & Revenue Cycle**: Oversee monthly billing reconciliation; chase receivables (mixed-billing); coordinate with bookkeeper on outstanding invoices; report billing trends to owner
- **Accounts Payable**: Approve supplier invoices for payment (within authority limit, e.g., <$5K); manage vendor relationships; negotiate payment terms
- **Compliance Tracking**: Maintain master calendar of expiring registrations, insurance, contracts, lease milestones; flag items to owner; ensure action taken
- **Accreditation & QIP Management**: Coordinate QIP documentation, maintain records; support GP CPD tracking; liaise with accreditation consultants
- **Facilities & Maintenance**: Oversee cleaning contractor, coordinate maintenance requests, manage utilities, ensure WH&S compliance; address patient safety issues (spills, broken equipment)
- **IT User Support**: First-line support for clinical software issues; coordinate with IT vendor for troubleshooting; manage user access/passwords (with IT provider)
- **Inventory Management**: Stock medical consumables, vaccines, supplies; maintain cold chain logs; order replenishment; manage waste disposal contractor
- **Patient Records & Data Management**: Ensure confidentiality of patient records, supervise staff handling of sensitive data, respond to patient requests for records
- **Meeting Coordination**: Schedule and minute staff meetings, complaint review meetings, accreditation prep meetings
- **Reporting to Owner**: Weekly operational summary, monthly compliance checklist, incident logs, staffing status

**Decisions Practice Manager Can Make Autonomously:**

- Approve appointment scheduling and rescheduling (clinical appropriateness deferred to clinician)
- Hire reception/admin/cleaning staff (up to $XXK salary authority set by owner)
- Manage day-to-day supplier orders and invoicing (<$5K limit)
- Resolve patient complaints (non-clinical; if complaint is clinical quality issue, escalate to GP/owner)
- Approve staff leave requests (within coverage availability)
- Address facilities/maintenance issues (<$2K limit); arrange urgent repairs
- Schedule QIP documentation tasks and accreditation prep activities
- Issue routine reminders to GPs on compliance deadlines (CPD, PRODA renewal, etc.)

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Owner:**

- Discipline or dismissal of staff (consultation with owner + HR/legal advice)
- Salary/wage increases or incentives
- Major operational changes (e.g., appointment length, clinic hours, patient flow redesign)
- Response to patient complaints involving clinical quality or legal liability
- Decisions affecting clinic strategy or services (e.g., adding new service line)
- Any expenditure >$5K not pre-approved in budget
- Any compliance issue that could affect accreditation, Medicare, or legal liability

**Reports/Data PM Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Daily**: Clinic attendance (GPs, nurses present/absent), patient no-shows, urgent incidents/complaints
- **Weekly**: Staffing status (rosters, absences, recruitment progress), key operational issues, billing status (compared to forecast)
- **Monthly**: Patient complaints summary (number, themes, resolution status), receivables aging (if mixed-billing), staff turnover (any departures?), compliance calendar checklist (expiring items flagged?)
- **Quarterly**: Staff satisfaction survey (optional), patient satisfaction feedback, operational KPIs (appointment wait times, clinic start/end times, no-show rate)

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Operational chaos: appointments missed, patients angry, staff confused, schedules conflict
- Billing leakage: invoices not sent, receivables pile up, cash flow degrades
- Compliance gaps: expiration dates missed, documentation incomplete, accreditation audit fails
- Staff morale declines: poor rostering, unfair treatment, lack of support
- Patient safety issues not reported or managed
- Owner distracted by operational minutiae instead of strategy

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house (essential)**: Practice manager must be employed in-house. This role cannot be effectively outsourced. PM must have daily clinic presence and staff relationships.
- If clinic cannot afford full-time PM: consider shared PM (part-time) or hybrid model (owner splits PM duties with external admin support).

## Section 2.3 — Reception Lead

**Core Purpose:**
The reception lead manages the front desk and patient-facing services. RL oversees appointment scheduling, patient check-in, phone/email triage, patient education, and team support for reception staff. RL is the clinic's first impression of quality and professionalism.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Appointment Scheduling**: Book appointments according to clinical needs and GP schedules; manage cancellations, walk-ins, urgent cases; coordinate with clinical staff on availability
- **Patient Check-In & Registration**: Verify patient details, update address/contact/insurance, check co-pays or gaps; ensure consent and privacy forms signed
- **Phone Triage & Messages**: Answer phones professionally, triage patient queries (appointment requests, prescription refills, query results, complaints); document calls; escalate clinical calls to nurse/GP
- **Patient Education**: Explain appointment processes, wait times, fees, payment methods; provide appointment reminders (SMS/email/phone)
- **Patient Departure**: Manage patient checkout, collect fees (mixed-billing), arrange follow-up, provide receipts, answer exit questions
- **Telemedicine Coordination**: If clinic offers telehealth, support patient video appointment setup, link distribution, troubleshooting
- **Staff Support for Reception**: Supervise reception staff, ensure standards maintained, provide training, handle difficult patient interactions
- **Electronic Health Record Management**: Input patient information into EHR, verify completeness, manage patient consent documentation
- **Feedback & Communication**: Relay patient feedback to practice manager/GPs; escalate concerns about wait times or patient dissatisfaction
- **Health Promotion Materials**: Maintain educational materials at reception, update signage, distribute flu/vaccination information
- **Compliance with Privacy**: Ensure patient confidentiality, secure handling of contact details, GDPR/Privacy Act compliance
- **Physical Reception Area**: Ensure reception is clean, welcoming, and organized; manage waiting room standard

**Decisions Reception Lead Can Make Autonomously:**

- Schedule appointments within available time slots
- Collect patient fees and provide receipts
- Resolve routine scheduling conflicts (rearrange appointments if capacity allows)
- Provide information about clinic services, hours, payment methods
- Refer patient to appropriate GP or nurse (triage advice)
- Escalate urgent patient issues to nurse/GP on duty

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Practice Manager / GP:**

- Any patient complaint or dissatisfaction
- Requests for special accommodations (disability access, language interpreter, chaperone)
- Questions about clinical services or GP recommendations
- Issues with appointment coverage or GP absence
- Patient requests for records or privacy-related matters

**Reports/Data Reception Lead Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Daily**: Appointment no-show/cancellation rate, appointment utilisation (% of time slots filled), any missed/deferred appointments due to GP absence
- **Weekly**: Patient phone call volume trends, common patient inquiries/complaints, any operational issues (e.g., appointment system downtime)
- **Monthly**: Summary of patient feedback/complaints, appointment wait times (average time to first appointment, average wait in clinic), patient contact data accuracy

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Patients frustrated by long wait times for appointments, rude reception staff, disorganized check-in
- Billing leakage: fees not collected, invoices not generated
- Clinical safety: urgent calls not triaged appropriately, escalation delayed
- Patient data quality poor: missing contact details, outdated insurance info
- Patient satisfaction scores decline; reputation damaged

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house (essential)**: Reception Lead should be employed in-house. Reception is the clinic's public face and requires daily presence, staff supervision, and patient relationship management.
- **Flexible element**: Reception functions (appointments, data entry) could be partially supported by virtual reception service or external call centre during peak hours, but primary lead must be in-house.

## Section 2.4 — Reception / Admin Staff

**Core Purpose:**
Reception and admin staff provide front-desk and back-office support: booking appointments, checking in patients, answering phones, data entry, mail management, insurance verification, and administrative tasks. These are junior roles supporting reception lead and clinic operations.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Phone Reception**: Answer phones, greet callers, direct to appropriate staff, take messages, provide basic clinic information
- **Appointment Booking**: Book appointments under reception lead direction, manage cancellation requests, follow clinic scheduling protocols
- **Patient Check-In**: Check patients in at reception, verify details, collect co-pays, hand out consent forms
- **Data Entry**: Input patient data into EHR (demographics, contact, medical history as dictated by GP), maintain accuracy
- **Mail & Correspondence**: Distribute incoming mail to appropriate staff, organize outgoing mail, manage filing (physical or digital)
- **Insurance Verification**: Check patient insurance eligibility (PHI), advise patient of rebates, enter details into patient record
- **Accounts Receivable Support**: Monitor invoiced gaps, send payment reminders, reconcile patient payments with invoices (under bookkeeper/PM direction)
- **Clerical Support to GPs**: Copy pathology results, file documents, prepare medical files for patient visits, organize loose papers
- **Compliance Documentation**: Ensure consent forms are signed, patient rights acknowledgements completed, privacy notices in place
- **Inventory Support**: Track appointment reminders (SMS/email), reorder appointment cards, reorder reception stationery
- **Team Support**: Backup reception during absences, support reception lead, assist practice manager with administrative tasks

**Decisions Reception/Admin Staff Can Make Autonomously:**

- Provide clinic information and appointment availability to callers
- Take messages and document calls in system
- Check in patients and collect fees
- Input routine patient data into EHR (under reception lead supervision)
- Arrange appointment reminders

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Reception Lead / Practice Manager:**

- Any patient complaint or query requiring problem-solving
- Clinical queries or urgent calls
- Requests for special services (interpreter, disability access)
- Any situation where patient information is requested outside normal process (legal request, subpoena, etc.)

**Reports/Data Reception/Admin Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Daily**: Clinic attendance (did all patients arrive? Any no-shows?), any phone calls not resolved
- **Weekly**: Summary of appointment-related issues or complaints, data entry error log (if tracked)
- **Monthly**: Patient contact data accuracy issues, any compliance forms not completed

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Patients frustrated with slow check-in, poor phone service, repeated data entry errors
- Billing delays due to incomplete insurance verification or missing invoices
- Clinical safety risk: urgent calls delayed, messages not passed
- Patient privacy breach: data mishandled, files left visible, calls taken in public area
- Staff burnout from taking on additional reception duties

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house (essential)**: Reception staff should be employed in-house. These are core clinic operations requiring daily presence and patient interaction.
- **Flexible option**: Reception support could be augmented with virtual receptionist service for overflow calls or after-hours, but primary staff must be in-house.

## Section 2.5 — Registered Nurses (RN/EN/Immunisation Nurses)

**Core Purpose:**
Registered and enrolled nurses provide clinical support to GPs and perform delegated clinical tasks: patient triage, chronic disease management, immunisation, wound care, phlebotomy, health screening, and patient education. Nurses are critical to clinic revenue and quality of care.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Patient Triage**: Phone triage of patient calls, assess urgency, recommend GP visit or self-care; document in EHR
- **Chronic Disease Management**: Conduct chronic disease reviews (diabetes, asthma, cardiovascular, COPD) under GP delegation; perform pathology tests, measurements (BP, weight); educate patient on management
- **Immunisation**: Administer vaccinations (childhood, adult, aged care, travel), maintain cold chain, record vaccine details in EHR and AIHW register, manage vaccine stock
- **Phlebotomy & Specimen Collection**: Collect blood samples, manage specimens, label correctly, arrange courier to pathology, maintain specimen integrity
- **Wound Care**: Manage minor wound dressing, suturing support, post-operative care, infection assessment
- **Health Screening**: Conduct health checks (cardiac risk, diabetes risk, cervical screening, colorectal screening, depression screening), educate on findings
- **Patient Education**: Counsel patients on chronic disease management, medications, lifestyle, preventive health, contraception options (if trained)
- **Medication Administration**: Administer injections, vaccines, IV therapies as per GP order and scope
- **Team Support**: Assist reception with urgent triage, support GPs during procedures, maintain clinical standards
- **Clinical Governance**: Maintain own CPD, scope of practice, professional registration (AHPRA); raise safety concerns; participate in clinical meetings
- **Documentation**: Complete accurate clinical notes, communicate with GPs via EHR; ensure records meet accreditation standards
- **Stock Management**: Manage nursing supply inventory (dressings, swabs, injections), order replenishment, manage waste

**Decisions Nurses Can Make Autonomously (Under GP Protocols):**

- Phone triage and recommend urgent GP appointment or self-care (based on protocol)
- Administer vaccines within scope of practice and standing orders
- Perform routine measurements and health screens
- Collect specimens and arrange pathology collection
- Provide patient education on chronic disease management and health promotion
- Document clinical interventions in EHR

**Decisions That Require Escalation to GP:**

- Any patient presenting with acute symptoms outside protocol
- Clinical assessment suggesting complication or need for medical review
- Patient declining recommended test or treatment
- Scope of practice issue (procedure outside training)
- Any safety concern or adverse event

**Reports/Data Nurses Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Per patient**: Complete and timely clinical notes in EHR after each patient interaction
- **Weekly**: Summary of immunisation clinics conducted, patient numbers, any vaccine supply issues
- **Monthly**: Triage call summary (volume, outcomes), chronic disease patient numbers reviewed, any clinical safety incidents or patient complaints

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- GP productivity declines: GPs spend time on tasks nurses could handle
- Revenue loss: chronic disease programs not run, immunisations not delivered, health screening not done
- Clinical risk: patients not properly triaged, wound care not monitored, vaccine cold chain compromised
- Patient safety: incorrect administration, poor documentation, adverse events not reported
- Accreditation risk: insufficient nursing documentation, scope compliance issues

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house (strongly recommended)**: Clinical nursing requires in-house presence, patient relationships, and real-time clinical response. Cannot be effectively outsourced.
- **Flexible element**: Specialised services (aged care nurses, sexual health nurses) could be engaged on sessional/contractor basis if required.

## Section 2.6 — Contractor GPs

**Core Purpose:**
Contractor GPs are self-employed independent practitioners who provide clinical services under a defined agreement. Contractors generate the bulk of clinical revenue, are accountable for their own clinical decisions, and have flexibility regarding hours, days, and rates.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Clinical Services Delivery**: Provide general practice consultations (face-to-face or telehealth) to booked patients; diagnose, treat, and manage patients according to professional standards
- **Patient Management**: Maintain accurate clinical records, order tests, refer to specialists, review test results, manage prescriptions (including PBS compliance)
- **Clinical Governance Compliance**: Maintain own AHPRA registration, professional indemnity insurance, CPD/CME points; adhere to clinic protocols and clinical standards
- **Revenue Generation**: Generate billings (either bulk-billing or mixed-billing depending on model); ensure correct item numbers, valid documentation for claims
- **Administrative Compliance**: Complete consultation notes and EHR documentation; comply with privacy, recall management, chronic disease documentation standards
- **Team Communication**: Communicate with nurses and reception about patient needs, follow-up, escalation; participate in clinic meetings and clinical governance
- **Performance Monitoring**: Accept performance monitoring (billing accuracy, patient complaints, clinical audit); respond to feedback
- **Scope Declaration**: Disclose areas of expertise or limitation; decline procedures outside scope; declare any health or professional conduct issues to clinic

**Decisions Contractor GPs Can Make Autonomously:**

- All clinical decisions for their own patients (diagnosis, treatment, referral, prescribing)
- Working hours and days (subject to roster agreement)
- Billing model (bulk vs mixed) and gap charges (mixed-billing only)
- Delegation to nurses for specific tasks (within nurse scope)

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Owner / Practice Manager:**

- Any clinical safety incident or adverse event
- Patient complaints or concerns about clinical quality
- Need for leave >1 week (affecting roster coverage)
- Any conflict of interest or professional conduct issue
- Changes to services offered or patient population served
- Concerns about clinic operations or management

**Reports/Data Contractor GPs Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Per patient**: Consultation notes and EHR documentation (within clinic timeframe, e.g., same day or next day)
- **Weekly**: Hours worked, any absences/cancellations
- **Monthly**: Billing summary (items, revenue), patient complaint log, any professional development undertaken
- **Quarterly**: Performance review against KPIs (billing accuracy, wait times, patient complaints)

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Revenue loss: contractor departs, patient list follows
- Clinical risk: poor clinical practice, adverse events, patient complaints
- Billing leakage: contractor bills at incorrect item numbers, inefficient billing processes
- Accreditation risk: contractor doesn't comply with clinic protocols, documentation standards, or CPD requirements
- Legal risk: contractor conduct creates liability (misadventure, sexual misconduct, substance abuse)

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house (essential for clinic viability)**: GPs must work on-site in clinic. This cannot be outsourced. However, GP engagement model can be flexible (contractor vs employee, full-time vs sessional, mix of both).

## Section 2.7 — Employee GPs (If Any)

**Core Purpose:**
If clinic employs GPs as employees (rather than contractors), they provide the same clinical services as contractors but are subject to employment law, supervision, and performance management. Employment model creates more clinic control but higher employment liability.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- Same as contractor GPs (Section 2.6) PLUS:
- **Supervision & Appraisal**: Accept supervision from senior clinician or owner; participate in regular performance appraisals (minimum annually)
- **Compliance with Policies**: Adhere to clinic employment policies, working hours, dress code, confidentiality requirements
- **On-Call & Emergency**: If clinic provides after-hours coverage, participate in on-call roster and emergency response as per employment agreement
- **Training & Development**: Participate in clinic inductions, protocols training, CPD aligned with clinic needs

**Decisions Employee GPs Can Make Autonomously:**
- Same as contractors, but subject to clinic employment policies and any specific employment conditions

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Owner / Practice Manager:**
- Any issue requiring HR intervention (performance concern, disciplinary matter, conflict with team)
- Leave requests (formal approval required, not just notification)
- Changes to employment conditions (hours, salary, duties)

**Reports/Data Employee GPs Should Provide (and Frequency):**
- Same as contractors, PLUS:
- CPD completion status (tracked for performance appraisal)
- Any training needs or professional development requests

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**
- All contractor risks PLUS:
- Employment law breach (Award underpayment, unfair dismissal, unlawful discrimination)
- Difficulty terminating underperforming employee (requires fair dismissal process)
- Higher on-costs (superannuation, leave accrual, payroll tax)
- GP resentment if employment terms feel restrictive compared to contractor peers

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house (essential)**: Employment of GPs is a strategic choice requiring legal documentation and HR management. Not suitable for outsourcing.
- **Note**: Many clinics use mixed model (some employee GPs, some contractors) for flexibility and risk management.

## Section 2.8 — Allied Health / Psychologists / Subtenants

**Core Purpose:**
If clinic hosts allied health practitioners (physiotherapists, psychologists, podiatrists, etc.) as subtenants or contractors, they provide specialised services and generate additional revenue via rent or referral commissions. These roles are optional for clinic growth but require separate governance.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Service Delivery**: Provide clinical services in their discipline according to professional standards and scope
- **Registration & Compliance**: Maintain own professional registration (AHPRA if applicable), indemnity insurance, CPD
- **Clinical Governance**: Adhere to clinic policies on privacy, documentation, patient safety; comply with accreditation standards where applicable
- **Billing & Revenue**: Bill own patients directly or via clinic (depending on agreement); handle own receivables
- **Space & Facilities**: Pay agreed rent; maintain confidentiality of clinic patients; use facilities responsibly
- **Clinical Coordination**: Communicate with GPs about patient care, accept referrals, provide feedback to referring GP

**Decisions Allied Health / Subtenants Can Make Autonomously:**

- All professional decisions within their scope
- Billing and patient fees (if independent billing)
- Hours and days (subject to lease arrangement)

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Owner / Practice Manager:**

- Any issue affecting clinic reputation or patient safety
- Clinical incidents or patient complaints
- Lease or facility concerns
- Concerns about patient confidentiality or data handling

**Reports/Data Allied Health / Subtenants Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Monthly**: Rent/lease payment confirmation, confirmation of professional registration maintenance
- **As needed**: Response to clinic inquiries about shared patient care, clinical outcomes, patient satisfaction

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- If allied health not well-integrated, clinic may lack awareness of subtenants' quality or compliance
- Subtenants not complying with clinic privacy/confidentiality standards (clinic liable)
- Patient safety incident involving subtenancy affects clinic reputation
- Lease disputes (rent non-payment, property damage, use violation)
- Revenue expectation mismatch (clinic assumes referral flow that doesn't materialise)

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **Flexible/Optional**: Allied health is not essential to core GP operations. Clinic can choose to host subtenants for additional revenue, but this requires formal lease agreement and governance separation.
- **Risk management**: Use lease agreement to clearly define responsibilities, compliance requirements, and dispute resolution. Require proof of professional registration and indemnity insurance.

## Section 2.9 — Pathology Collection Staff / Pathology Provider Relationship

**Core Purpose:**
Pathology is an ancillary but critical service: drawing blood samples, managing specimen integrity, coordinating collection, arranging courier, and ensuring result delivery to GPs. Pathology services may be delivered in-house (clinic employs collector) or outsourced (external pathology provider visits/collects on-site or patients visit external lab).

**Main Responsibilities (If In-House Phlebotomy):**

- **Phlebotomy Training & Standards**: Maintain phlebotomist or nurse qualification; perform blood draws safely, minimize patient harm
- **Specimen Management**: Label specimens correctly, maintain integrity, store appropriately (temperature, time)
- **Courier Coordination**: Arrange timely collection by pathology provider, ensure specimens arrive within acceptable time window
- **Specimen Tracking**: Communicate result status to GPs, flag critical results, manage result processing in EHR
- **Infection Control**: Follow bloodborne pathogen protocols, safe disposal of sharps and biohazard
- **Quality Assurance**: Monitor specimen rejection rate, investigate causes, implement improvements

**Responsibilities (If Outsourced to Pathology Provider):**

- Clinic appoints preferred pathology provider (e.g., Sullivan Nicolaides, Dorevitch, ALS, Australian Clinical Labs, etc.)
- Provider provides collection services (courier visits clinic or patients visit external lab)
- Provider manages specimen handling, testing, and result delivery
- Clinic maintains relationship, negotiates billing terms, monitors turnaround times and quality

**Decisions Pathology Staff / Provider Can Make Autonomously:**

- Blood draw technique and safety protocols
- Specimen handling procedures
- Courier scheduling and result tracking

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Practice Manager / GP:**

- Any specimen rejection or quality issue
- Delayed result reporting
- Patient complaints about phlebotomy experience
- Critical result requiring urgent notification
- Need to change pathology provider or billing arrangements

**Reports/Data Pathology Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Daily/Weekly**: Result delivery status (% of results received within target timeframe, e.g., 24–48 hours)
- **Monthly**: Specimen rejection rate, turnaround times, any quality issues, patient complaints
- **Quarterly**: Cost analysis (cost per test, pathology spend trends), relationship review with provider

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Delayed test results = delayed patient diagnosis = clinical risk and patient dissatisfaction
- Specimen rejection/damage = wasted patient visit and repeat test = cost and time
- Infection control breach (improper sharps handling, bloodborne pathogen exposure)
- Poor pathology provider relationship = delays, poor service, billing disputes
- Patient safety: critical results not communicated to GP in timely manner

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **Flexible**: Phlebotomy can be in-house (clinic staff) or outsourced (pathology provider courier). Most clinics choose outsourcing for simplicity.
- **In-house model**: Requires trained phlebotomist (nurse) or contracted phlebotomist; manages own specimens; may increase cost and complexity.
- **Outsourced model** (recommended): Pathology provider sends courier to clinic (patient-facing convenience); provider manages specimens and results; clinic pays per specimen or agreed arrangement.

## Section 2.10 — Bookkeeper

**Core Purpose:**
The bookkeeper is the clinic's financial operations manager: processing invoices, recording transactions, reconciling bank accounts, managing accounts payable and receivable, and providing accurate financial data for the accountant. Bookkeeper is critical to financial accuracy and audit trail.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Daily Banking**: Deposit patient payments and checks daily; reconcile deposit to point-of-sale or cash drawer
- **Invoicing & Accounts Receivable** (mixed-billing clinics): Generate invoices for patient gaps; track unpaid invoices; send payment reminders; follow up on overdue accounts; record payments received; manage bad debt write-offs
- **Accounts Payable**: Process supplier invoices; verify receipt of goods/services; code to correct GL accounts; process payments on due dates; maintain vendor files
- **Bank Reconciliation**: Reconcile clinic bank account monthly to statements; investigate discrepancies; flag unusual transactions
- **General Ledger**: Record all transactions into accounting software (MYOB, Xero, etc.); ensure correct GL coding; maintain audit trail
- **Payroll Processing**: Process weekly/fortnightly payroll (or coordinate with external payroll processor); verify timesheets, calculate wages, deductions, superannuation; process payments
- **BAS Preparation**: Coordinate with accountant on quarterly BAS preparation (GST, PAYG withholding, superannuation reporting); provide transaction data
- **Financial Reporting**: Prepare monthly P&L and balance sheet; variance analysis (actual vs budget); monthly cash position summary
- **Tax Compliance Data**: Maintain records for accountant; provide documentation for tax audit; track deductible expenses
- **Compliance**: Maintain proper records in accordance with tax law and accounting standards; document all transactions

**Decisions Bookkeeper Can Make Autonomously:**

- Process supplier invoices for payment (within authority limit, e.g., <$5K and matching PO)
- Reconcile bank account and investigate minor discrepancies
- Code transactions to GL accounts (following established chart of accounts)
- Follow up on overdue receivables and send payment reminders
- Process routine payroll (with verified timesheets)

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Owner / Accountant:**

- Write-off of bad debt or uncollectible receivable
- Significant GL coding questions (unusual transaction type)
- Any discrepancy not easily resolved in bank reconciliation
- Estimated amount needed for tax provision
- Changes to accounting policies or processes

**Reports/Data Bookkeeper Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Daily**: Cash received summary (patient payments + deposits)
- **Weekly**: Payroll processing confirmation and payment made
- **Monthly**: Financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow), receivables aging (if mixed-billing), accounts payable summary, variance report (actual vs budget), cash position
- **Quarterly**: Data prepared for accountant (trial balance, transaction list, tax records), any compliance issues identified

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Financial records inaccurate or incomplete; tax audit risk
- Invoices not sent (mixed-billing); cash flow gap
- Supplier payments delayed; vendor disputes
- Payroll processed incorrectly; staff complaints, ATO audit
- Bank account drift not detected; fraud risk
- Monthly P&L not available; owner flying blind on profitability

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house OR outsourced**: Bookkeeping can be performed by in-house staff or outsourced to external bookkeeper or accounting firm.
- **In-house model**: Requires qualified bookkeeper (accounting diploma or equivalent); daily presence; access to all financial systems.
- **Outsourced model** (common): External bookkeeper or accounting firm (e.g., BAS agent) processes invoices, payroll, and monthly financials; clinic or accountant validates. Lower overhead; less internal HR.
- **Hybrid model**: Clinic reception/admin staff handle basic transaction entry; external bookkeeper does reconciliation, compliance, and reporting.

## Section 2.11 — Accountant / Tax Agent

**Core Purpose:**
The accountant provides external, independent financial management and tax expertise: preparing financial statements, lodging tax returns, advising on business structure and tax planning, managing BAS compliance, and supporting audit/compliance. Accountant is essential for legal and tax compliance.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Financial Statement Preparation**: Prepare statutory financial statements (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow) and notes; ensure compliance with accounting standards
- **Income Tax Return**: Prepare and lodge clinic's income tax return; coordinate with bookkeeper on records; claim all legitimate deductions
- **BAS Lodgement**: Prepare and lodge quarterly BAS returns (GST, PAYG, superannuation reporting) or coordinate with BAS agent
- **Superannuation Compliance**: Advise on superannuation guarantee compliance; review payroll super contributions; respond to ATO queries
- **Tax Planning & Advice**: Advise on business structure (sole trader, partnership, company, trust); tax-minimization strategies; deductibility of expenses; capital expenditure treatment
- **Audit Support**: Provide records and cooperation to external auditor (if audit required); respond to audit queries
- **Compliance Advice**: Advise on employment law compliance, tax law changes, GST compliance, payroll obligations
- **Banking & Lending**: Support clinic's banking relationship; provide financial statements for bank credit applications
- **Benchmarking & Advising**: Compare clinic's financial performance to industry benchmarks; advise on cost reduction or revenue improvement

**Decisions Accountant Can Make Autonomously:**

- Selection of accounting policies (depreciation method, bad debt provisioning, etc.) — but discuss with owner
- Tax deduction eligibility determination
- Advice on tax minimization strategies

**Decisions That Require Owner Consultation:**

- Major capital expenditure or investment decision
- Changes to business structure or ownership
- Loan application or refinancing
- Significant strategic change affecting financials

**Reports/Data Accountant Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Quarterly**: BAS preparation and lodgement confirmation; tax payment due dates advice
- **Annual**: Audited or reviewed financial statements; tax return prepared and lodged; annual tax bill calculation and payment plan
- **Ongoing**: Written advice on specific tax or compliance matters as requested

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Tax returns lodged incorrectly or late; ATO penalties and interest
- BAS non-compliance; superannuation debt not remitted
- No strategic tax planning; unnecessarily high tax bill
- Financial statements not prepared; owner lacks visibility on profitability
- Compliance issues not identified; regulatory breach
- Loan applications rejected due to poor financial record
- No external validation of bookkeeper's work; fraud risk

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **Outsourced (essential)**: Accountant should be external, independent firm. Cannot be in-house (lack of independence for audit/tax purposes).
- **Recommended selection**: Accounting firm with experience in GP practices (e.g., Pitcher Partners, Grant Thornton, BDO, local accounting firm specializing in healthcare). Budget $5K–$15K annually depending on complexity and service level.

## Section 2.12 — Payroll Support

**Core Purpose:**
Payroll processing ensures staff are paid correctly and on time; superannuation is remitted; tax withholding is accurate; and records are maintained for compliance. Payroll can be processed in-house or outsourced to a payroll service provider.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Timesheet Collection**: Collect and verify timesheets from staff (weekly or fortnightly); address discrepancies
- **Wage Calculation**: Calculate gross wages based on award rates, penalty rates, allowances, leave accrual
- **Deductions**: Calculate tax withholding (PAYG), superannuation, union fees, child support, court orders
- **Superannuation**: Calculate and remit superannuation contributions (employer + employee) to super fund; maintain records
- **Payment Processing**: Arrange payment to staff (direct deposit); provide pay slips
- **Tax Compliance**: Remit PAYG withholding to ATO quarterly (via BAS); maintain tax records
- **Leave Management**: Accrue and track annual leave, long service leave, personal leave; process leave payments on termination
- **Award Compliance**: Ensure staff paid at least Modern Award minimum rates (Medical Practitioners Award for nurses; other Awards for reception/admin); document compliance
- **Records & Documentation**: Maintain payroll records (timesheets, pay calculations, tax, super) for at least 5 years; provide to accountant for BAS/tax purposes
- **Termination**: Process final pay on termination; calculate leave payouts; provide separation documentation

**Decisions Payroll Support Can Make Autonomously:**

- Calculate wages based on timesheets and approved rates
- Process routine payments
- Respond to simple payroll queries

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Practice Manager / Owner:**

- Disputes about timesheet hours or wage calculation
- Requests for salary advance or special payment
- Disciplinary deductions or changes to pay
- Superannuation fund changes
- Award rate updates or compliance questions

**Reports/Data Payroll Support Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Weekly/Fortnightly**: Payroll run summary (number of staff, total wages, deductions, super, net payment)
- **Monthly**: Reconciliation of payroll to P&L and cash; PAYG and super remittance status; leave accrual summary
- **Quarterly**: BAS data (PAYG and super amounts) to accountant; compliance checklist

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Staff paid incorrectly or late; staff morale, potential dispute
- Superannuation not remitted; ATO debt and penalties
- PAYG withholding incorrect; ATO audit
- Award rates not met; Fair Work Ombudsman investigation; back-pay liability
- Leave entitlements miscalculated; termination dispute, redundancy liability
- Poor documentation; audit/compliance failure

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **Outsourced (recommended)**: Payroll is best outsourced to qualified payroll service provider (e.g., Paychex, Payreward, BrightPay, or local payroll bureau). Reduces internal HR burden, ensures compliance, auditable.
- **In-house option**: If clinic is small or owner prefers, payroll can be processed in-house by practice manager or bookkeeper using accounting software (MYOB Payroll, Xero Payroll). Requires training on Modern Award, tax, super compliance.
- **Cost**: Outsourced payroll typically costs $200–$500 per month depending on staff count and complexity.

## Section 2.13 — IT Provider / Managed Services

**Core Purpose:**
The IT provider supplies and supports clinical software infrastructure, cybersecurity, data backup, and systems administration. IT provider is critical to clinic operations continuity and data security.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Clinical Software Support**: Provide support for practice management system (Best Practice, Medical Director, Genie, etc.); troubleshoot user issues; coordinate software updates and patches
- **System Administration**: Manage user accounts and access controls; reset passwords; configure new staff accounts; manage security access
- **Data Backup & Disaster Recovery**: Implement daily backups of clinical data (offsite, secure); test recovery processes; maintain backup schedule documentation
- **Cybersecurity**: Deploy and maintain antivirus, firewalls, malware protection; implement multi-factor authentication; monitor for suspicious activity; respond to security incidents
- **Network & Infrastructure**: Manage internet, phone systems, WiFi, servers (if clinic has on-site server); ensure availability and speed
- **Email & Communication**: Manage clinic email, secure email protocols for sensitive communications (e.g., patient information)
- **Remote Access Security**: If clinic offers remote work, manage VPN, secure device access, password managers
- **Patch Management**: Keep all systems updated with security patches and software updates
- **Compliance & Security Standards**: Ensure IT systems comply with privacy law, cybersecurity frameworks (ACSQHC), encryption standards
- **Incident Response**: Respond to IT incidents (system outage, data breach, ransomware); coordinate recovery and communication to clinic

**Decisions IT Provider Can Make Autonomously:**

- Routine password resets and account provisioning
- Troubleshooting and resolving common technical issues
- Scheduling software updates and patches (after notice to clinic)
- Implementing security updates and patches

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Practice Manager / Owner:**

- Any security incident or suspected breach
- System downtime affecting clinic operations
- Major software change or update affecting workflow
- Significant capex (new servers, software license, hardware)
- Changes to backup or disaster recovery procedures

**Reports/Data IT Provider Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Daily**: System availability and uptime status (service level dashboard)
- **Weekly**: Security alerts or suspicious activities detected; patch updates deployed
- **Monthly**: Support ticket summary (issues resolved, average response time), system performance metrics, backup integrity confirmation, compliance checklist
- **Quarterly**: IT security audit; recommendations for improvements

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- System downtime; clinic cannot access patient records or bill
- Data loss due to backup failure
- Ransomware infection; clinic held for Bitcoin ransom; data locked
- Data breach; patient information exposed; regulatory notification required
- Slow network; patient appointments delayed
- Compliance failure (unpatched systems, weak security); audit failure

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **Outsourced (strongly recommended)**: IT support is best outsourced to managed IT services provider (MSP) with healthcare experience (e.g., Comsec, Nextgen, local IT managed services firm). Ensures security, compliance, backup, 24/7 availability.
- **In-house option**: Larger clinics may employ dedicated IT staff, but requires expertise and is usually more expensive.
- **Cost**: Managed IT services typically cost $2K–$5K per month depending on number of users, complexity, and SLA.
- **Service Level Agreement (SLA)**: Critical to have written SLA defining response time (e.g., 1 hour for critical outage), uptime guarantee (99.5%+), backup frequency, etc.

## Section 2.15 — Accreditation / Quality Lead

**Core Purpose:**
The accreditation/quality lead manages the clinic's RACGP accreditation compliance, QIP (Quality Improvement Plan) maintenance, clinical governance documentation, and accreditation audit preparation. This role is critical to maintaining clinic's ability to bill Medicare.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **QIP Development & Maintenance**: Develop and maintain clinic's QIP (7-year rolling plan); ensure QIP addresses standards and clinic context
- **RACGP Standards Compliance**: Ensure clinic meets RACGP accreditation standards (governance, safety, quality, equity, etc.); conduct self-assessment
- **Documentation Management**: Maintain evidence of compliance (policies, procedures, meeting minutes, incident logs, audit trails); organize for auditor review
- **GP CPD Tracking**: Track each GP's CPD points; ensure compliance with RACGP CPD requirements (50 points per 5-year cycle); document evidence
- **Incident Reporting & Risk Management**: Implement incident reporting system; analyze incidents; implement corrective actions; maintain risk register
- **Patient Safety & Complaints Management**: Implement complaints management system; track complaints; investigate and resolve; identify trends
- **Accreditation Audit Preparation**: Organize evidence collection 3–6 months before audit; prepare clinic for auditor visit; coordinate with auditor; address audit findings
- **Continuous Improvement**: Lead quality improvement initiatives; monitor KPIs; identify opportunities for improvement; engage staff in quality culture
- **Staff Compliance Training**: Ensure staff trained on accreditation standards, privacy, WH&S, infection control, mandatory reporting; maintain training records
- **External Liaison**: Liaise with RACGP, accreditation auditors, consultants; communicate accreditation status to clinic

**Decisions Quality Lead Can Make Autonomously:**

- Develop QIP and accreditation documentation
- Design and implement compliance processes and checklists
- Track CPD and training compliance
- Implement corrective actions for non-compliances found
- Prepare audit evidence

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Owner / GP Leadership:**

- Any significant accreditation compliance breach
- Major clinical incidents or safety concerns
- Changes to clinic policies or procedures affecting accreditation
- Accreditation audit failure or remediation plan
- Resource requests for accreditation work

**Reports/Data Quality Lead Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Monthly**: Accreditation compliance status checklist, GP CPD progress, any new incidents or complaints, QIP update progress
- **Quarterly**: Compliance dashboard (% of standards met, audit trail review), training completion status, quality improvement initiatives and outcomes
- **Before audit** (6 months out): Full audit preparation plan and evidence collection timeline; audit readiness assessment

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Accreditation compliance gaps not identified; clinic fails audit
- GP CPD not tracked; GPs lapse in compliance; accreditation at risk
- Incidents and complaints not reported or managed; patient safety issues escalate
- Clinic unable to demonstrate compliance during audit; suspended accreditation
- QIP not maintained; lack of strategic quality direction
- Audit failure = loss of Medicare provider number = clinic shutdown

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house OR external consultant (hybrid recommended)**: Quality role can be in-house (if clinic has capacity) or outsourced to external accreditation consultant (e.g., RACGP-approved accreditation consultant, healthcare compliance firm).
- **In-house model**: Requires trained quality/compliance professional; dedicated time; knowledge of RACGP standards.
- **Outsourced model** (common): External consultant conducts annual compliance audit; helps with QIP and audit preparation; provides training. Clinic staff (often practice manager) maintains day-to-day compliance tracking and documentation.
- **Hybrid model** (recommended): In-house practice manager or QIP coordinator handles daily compliance; external consultant provides 6-monthly review and audit preparation support.

## Section 2.16 — Privacy / Complaints / Risk Lead

**Core Purpose:**
This role manages patient privacy compliance, complaints handling, and overall risk management: ensuring clinic complies with Privacy Act, Managing risk, handling patient complaints professionally, and responding to regulatory inquiries.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Privacy Act Compliance**: Implement privacy policies and procedures; ensure patient consent obtained for information use/disclosure; manage patient requests for records; maintain privacy documentation
- **Data Security & Breach Management**: Implement data security measures; respond to data breaches (investigation, patient notification, OAIC reporting); maintain breach log
- **Patient Complaints Management**: Establish complaints process; receive and log complaints; investigate and respond; track resolution; identify systemic issues; communicate with complainants
- **Complaints Reporting**: Prepare complaints summary for management; identify trends; escalate serious complaints; report to regulators if required
- **Health Practitioner Notifications**: Report serious adverse events to AHPRA (if applicable); document reportable conduct of GPs or staff
- **Risk Management**: Maintain risk register (clinical, financial, operational, compliance, reputational); implement risk mitigation strategies; monitor risk status
- **Regulatory Liaison**: Respond to inquiries from AHPRA, Health Complaints Commissioner, Services Australia, TGA, other regulators; coordinate legal counsel if needed
- **Insurance Claims**: Support professional indemnity insurance claims process; coordinate with insurer; provide documentation
- **Mandatory Reporting**: Ensure staff trained on mandatory reporting (child abuse, elder abuse, patient safety concerns); implement reporting procedures; report to relevant authorities
- **Incident Response**: Develop and maintain incident response plan; coordinate response to critical incidents; communicate with affected parties and regulators

**Decisions Privacy/Risk Lead Can Make Autonomously:**

- Develop privacy policies and procedures
- Log and investigate patient complaints
- Implement privacy and data security measures
- Track and monitor risk register
- Coordinate routine regulatory correspondence

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Owner / Legal Counsel:**

- Any serious complaint involving patient harm or legal liability
- Data breach or security incident
- Notifiable incident (AHPRA, Health Complaints Commissioner, TGA)
- Regulatory investigation or audit
- Insurance claim or legal threat
- Systemic risk affecting clinic operations

**Reports/Data Privacy/Risk Lead Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Monthly**: Complaints summary (number, themes, resolution status), incident log, data breach log (if any), risk register status
- **Quarterly**: Privacy compliance audit, staff training completion status, regulatory correspondence summary, insurance claims status

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Patient complaint not handled professionally; escalates to regulator
- Privacy breach not reported; regulatory fine and reputational damage
- Data security breach not detected; patient information exposed
- Mandatory reporting not done; clinic and staff liable for failure
- Serious incident not managed; patient safety risk escalates
- Regulatory inquiry not responded to promptly; penalties or clinic closure
- Clinical negligence claim not managed; legal liability exposure

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **In-house OR external consultant**: Privacy/risk role can be in-house (practice manager often handles) or outsourced to external risk management consultant.
- **In-house model**: Practice manager or designated staff member handles complaints and privacy matters; requires training and templates.
- **Outsourced model**: External privacy/risk consultant provides quarterly compliance audit, policy development, and incident response support; clinic handles day-to-day complaints.
- **Hybrid model** (recommended): In-house practice manager manages day-to-day complaints and privacy matters; external consultant provides quarterly audit, policy updates, and regulatory liaison support.

## Section 2.17 — Cleaner / Waste / Maintenance Providers

**Core Purpose:**
External service providers maintain clinic facilities (cleaning, waste disposal, repairs, utilities management). These roles are essential for patient safety, accreditation compliance, and infection control but are typically outsourced.

**Main Responsibilities (Cleaner):**

- **Daily/Weekly Cleaning**: Clean clinic areas (reception, waiting room, consulting rooms, bathrooms, kitchen); dust, sweep, vacuum, sanitize surfaces
- **Infection Control**: Clean and disinfect high-touch areas (door handles, light switches, keyboards); manage biohazard cleaning (if required)
- **Waste Management Support**: Collect clinical waste and general waste; place in appropriate bins; arrange collection
- **Restocking**: Restock soap, paper towels, toilet paper, hand sanitizer, supplies
- **Reporting**: Report maintenance issues (broken equipment, leaks, etc.) to practice manager

**Main Responsibilities (Waste Management Provider):**

- **Biohazard Disposal**: Collect clinical/biological waste (sharps, swabs, gauze, pharmaceutical waste); transport safely; dispose according to regulations
- **General Waste**: Manage general rubbish collection; arrange regular bin collection
- **Compliance Documentation**: Provide waste disposal certificates; maintain audit trail
- **Safety**: Ensure safe handling of hazardous waste; minimize infection/environmental risk

**Main Responsibilities (Maintenance Provider):**

- **Facilities Maintenance**: Maintain building systems (HVAC, plumbing, electrical); respond to repair requests; perform preventive maintenance
- **Equipment Service**: Service refrigerators, autoclaves, other medical equipment; maintain service logs
- **Safety Compliance**: Ensure facilities safe and compliant (emergency lighting, fire extinguishers, non-slip flooring, accessible parking, etc.)

**Decisions Cleaner / Waste / Maintenance Can Make Autonomously:**

- Schedule cleaning/maintenance activities
- Perform routine cleaning and maintenance tasks
- Report issues to practice manager

**Decisions That Require Escalation to Practice Manager / Owner:**

- Any urgent repair needed (safety issue, system failure)
- Quality concerns with cleaning or waste management
- Cost overruns or contract changes
- Compliance issues (waste not disposed correctly, safety violation)

**Reports/Data Providers Should Provide (and Frequency):**

- **Monthly**: Invoice for services; confirmation of waste disposal (certificates); any maintenance issues identified
- **Quarterly**: Review of service quality; compliance documentation

**Key Risks If Role Is Weak or Absent:**

- Clinic unclean or unhygienic; patient perception poor; accreditation impact; infection risk
- Biohazard waste not disposed correctly; regulatory breach; environmental contamination; staff exposure
- Maintenance issues unaddressed; patient safety risk (slips, falls, equipment failure)
- Compliance documentation missing; accreditation audit failure

**Essential In-House / Outsourced / Flexible:**
- **Outsourced (standard)**: Cleaning, waste management, and maintenance are best outsourced to specialized contractors.
- **Cleaner**: Typically employed by clinic (part-time) or contracted to cleaning company.
- **Waste**: Contracted to medical waste disposal company (e.g., Stericycle, Biomedical Waste Management, local contractor).
- **Maintenance**: Contracted to general maintenance company or individual handyperson; building services if in large complex.

## Section 2.18 — Landlord / Building Manager Relationship

**Core Purpose:**
The landlord and building manager are external parties controlling clinic premises. Strong landlord relationship is critical to lease stability, maintenance responsiveness, and lease renewal.

**Main Responsibilities:**

- **Property Management**: Maintain building (structure, exterior, common areas); respond to maintenance requests from tenants
- **Lease Administration**: Issue lease documentation; collect rent; manage lease renewals and end-of-lease procedures
- **Building Maintenance**: Manage building-wide systems (HVAC, water, electrical); coordinate tradework; maintain compliance
- **Tenant Liaison**: Communicate with tenants; address concerns; manage disputes
- **Make-Good Management**: At lease end, manage property return; assess make-good requirements; manage disputes over restoration costs

**Decisions Landlord / Building Manager Can Make Autonomously:**

- Routine maintenance scheduling
- Rent payment collection
- Lease administration

**Decisions Requiring Clinic Input / Negotiation:**

- Major maintenance projects affecting clinic (interruption, cost-sharing)
- Lease renewal terms and rent increases
- Make-good requirements and costs
- Access to premises for maintenance or inspection

**Reports/Data Clinic Should Expect from Landlord:**

- **Annually**: Lease renewal notice (if option period applies), confirmation of insurance (landlord insurance), notification of any building works

- **External advisors are consultants**: Accountant, lawyer, IT provider report progress to owner; owner decides on actions based on advice.
- **Landlord and subtenants are external stakeholders**: Direct relationship with owner; not part of internal reporting hierarchy.

## Section 2.20 — What the Owner Must Personally Oversee Weekly

The following 15+ items require owner attention on a weekly basis (or more frequently if issues arise). Owner must stay operationally engaged despite delegating day-to-day management to practice manager.

**Weekly Owner Oversight Checklist:**

1. **Yesterday's Billings** (review each business day, at minimum weekly): Check daily billing report; confirm billings match expected pattern; investigate significant drops or anomalies.

2. **Current Bank Balance & Cash Position**: Review bank balance daily or 2–3 times weekly; verify sufficient funds for payroll, rent, supplier payments; monitor cash flow trend.

3. **Full GP Roster (Next 4 Weeks)**: Confirm all 4 FTE positions filled; note any absences, leave, or gaps; verify backup coverage for absences.

4. **Payroll Status**: Confirm payroll processed on time; staff paid; super remitted; no delays or errors reported.

5. **Customer Complaints (Open Issues)**: Review any open complaints; confirm investigation status; follow up on resolution; assess severity and impact.

6. **Accreditation & Compliance Calendar**: Check expiring items (registrations, insurance, contracts, audit dates) within 90-day horizon; ensure action plan in place.

7. **Medicare Compliance Status**: Monitor for any Services Australia communications (audits, queries, compliance notices); escalate immediately.

8. **Insurance Expiry Dates**: Confirm all insurance policies current (professional indemnity, cyber, property, public liability); note renewal dates coming in <60 days.

9. **Critical Clinical Incidents**: Review any incidents reported (patient harm, medication error, safety breach); ensure investigation and remediation occurring.

10. **Staff Attendance & Turnover**: Note any staff absences, sick leave patterns, or departure notices; assess morale and stability.

11. **Performance Against Budget**: Compare actual billings, costs, and EBITDA to monthly budget; investigate variances >10%; assess if on track to annual targets.

12. **Receivables Aging** (if mixed-billing): Review invoices >60 days old; ensure collections efforts underway; assess bad-debt risk.

13. **GP Performance & Utilisation**: Monitor each GP's billing output, patient complaints, no-show rate; identify any underperformance or concerns; discuss with GP if issues arise.

14. **Facilities & Safety Issues**: Confirm no urgent maintenance issues unaddressed; cold chain monitoring for vaccines; WH&S hazards.

15. **Team Communication & Conflicts**: Check in with practice manager on staff issues, team morale, any conflicts or HR concerns; address early before escalation.

16. **Business Development Pipeline**: Monitor new services, GP recruitment progress, patient acquisition initiatives; confirm on track with strategic plan.

17. **Communication with Key Advisors**: Schedule monthly calls with accountant (month-end review), lawyer/HR (if issues), IT provider (security/uptime), accreditation consultant (if preparing for audit).

**How to Operationalize Weekly Oversight:**

- **Monday morning**: Review previous week's billings (Friday), check bank balance, scan complaint log, confirm GP roster for week.
- **Thursday afternoon**: Review week-to-date billings, cash position, upcoming payroll, any urgent issues.
- **Friday end-of-day**: Full week summary (billings total, cash position, staff status, compliance checklist, any escalations).
- **Monthly (e.g., first Friday)**: Full monthly financial review with accountant; operational review with practice manager; strategic discussion.

| **Tax Return & BAS Lodgement** | Accountant / Tax Agent | Bookkeeper provides source documents; accountant lodges; owner reviews and signs | Quarterly: BAS review; annual: tax return review and sign-off |
| **Superannuation Remittance** | Payroll Service Provider | Contribution rates verified; fund details confirmed; remittance timing tracked | Monthly: audit super contributions; quarterly: review statement |
| **Staff Scheduling & Rosters** | Practice Manager | Clinic coverage requirements met; leave requests approved; fairness assured | Weekly: review roster for gaps; monthly: assess staff satisfaction |
| **Staff Performance Management** | Practice Manager (with Owner approval if discipline/dismissal) | Performance issues documented; fair process; opportunity to respond; HR advice obtained | Monthly: performance summary; escalate discipline/termination to owner |
| **Clinical Software Configuration** | IT Provider or Software Vendor | Clinic workflows documented; staff trained; changes tested | Monthly: system performance review; escalate outages immediately |
| **IT Backups & Disaster Recovery** | IT Managed Services Provider | Backup frequency and storage confirmed; recovery tested regularly | Monthly: backup integrity audit; escalate any failures immediately |
| **Cybersecurity & Patching** | IT Managed Services Provider | Security protocols implemented; staff training on phishing; incident response plan | Monthly: security status report; escalate any breach immediately |
| **Accreditation Compliance & QIP** | Quality Lead / Consultant (internal or external) | Compliance plan documented; evidence collected; staff trained | Monthly: compliance status; quarterly: full audit; 6 months before audit: intensive prep |
| **Patient Complaints Handling** | Practice Manager or Privacy Lead | Complaints process documented; investigation thorough; response timely; escalation path clear | Monthly: complaints summary; escalate serious complaints to owner immediately |
| **Privacy & Data Security** | Privacy Lead or Practice Manager | Privacy policies documented; staff trained; breach procedures; incident log | Monthly: privacy audit; escalate any breach immediately |
| **Facility Cleaning & Maintenance** | Contracted Cleaner / Maintenance | Service standards documented; quality inspected; payment verified | Weekly: visual inspection; monthly: service review; escalate urgent repairs immediately |
| **Waste Management** | Contracted Waste Disposal Provider | Compliance verified; disposal certificates obtained; safety assured | Monthly: waste management audit; escalate non-compliance immediately |
| **Vaccine Cold Chain Management** | Nursing Staff (with PM oversight) | Temperature logs maintained daily; fridge monitored; staff trained; backup fridge available | Daily: check temperature logs; monthly: cold chain audit; escalate immediately if breach |
| **Medical Records & Patient Data Access** | Reception / Admin Staff (with strict protocols) | Privacy training; access controls; audit trail; patient consent | Monthly: privacy audit; quarterly: data access review |
| **Patient Education & Health Promotion** | Nurses / Reception (with GP approval) | Materials reviewed for accuracy; culturally appropriate; patient-focused | Monthly: material quality review; patient feedback |
| **Pharmacy Ordering & Dispensing** (if offered) | Trained Staff / Pharmacist | Pharmacy regulations compliance; record-keeping; quality assurance | Weekly: stock review; monthly: compliance audit |

**Key Principles for Safe Delegation:**

1. **Documentation**: Process must be documented; staff trained; no assumptions.
2. **Verification**: Owner must spot-check work; cannot delegate oversight completely.
3. **Escalation Path**: Clear escalation for problems; owner informed of issues.
4. **Frequency**: Higher-risk items (compliance, patient safety, cash) reviewed more frequently (daily/weekly). Lower-risk items (cleaning, supplies) reviewed monthly/quarterly.
5. **Authority Limits**: Specify spend authority (e.g., PM can approve <$5K invoices; >$5K requires owner).

## Section 2.22 — What Absolutely Cannot Be Neglected

The following 10 areas will cause serious harm (financial, clinical, regulatory, or reputational) if neglected. Owner must ensure these are monitored and actioned, even if delegated operationally.

| Item | Why Neglect Is Dangerous | Consequence of Failure | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Cash Flow & Payroll** | Without cash, clinic cannot pay staff or suppliers; instant staff morale crisis and potential insolvency. | Unable to meet payroll → staff walk out → clinic closes → patients abandoned → regulatory investigation | Review bank balance daily; maintain 20–30 days operating expenses reserve; 13-week cash forecast; line of credit facility |
| **2. Medicare Compliance & Billing Accuracy** | Services Australia audits billing; non-compliance can trigger debt notices, provider suspension, audit costs. | Repayment demand ($50K–$500K+), provider suspension, audit costs ($10K–$50K), loss of clinic viability | Monthly billing audit; staff training on item numbers; PRODA compliance checks; professional indemnity insurance |
| **3. Accreditation Status & QIP Maintenance** | RACGP accreditation is prerequisite for Medicare billing. Lapse = clinic cannot bill = instant business failure. | Loss of accreditation → cannot bill Medicare → clinic shutdown → patients abandoned → staff redundancy → business failure | Quarterly accreditation compliance review; 6-month pre-audit preparation; external consultant support |
| **4. GP Retention & Recruitment** | 1–2 GPs generate 50–70% of clinic revenue. Sudden departure = 15–25% revenue loss; difficulty recruiting replacement. | GP departure → revenue loss → cash crisis → staff morale collapse → patient loss → downward spiral | Competitive remuneration; positive workplace culture; succession planning; non-compete agreements; relationship management |
| **5. Staff Superannuation & Payroll Compliance** | ATO can audit payroll; missing super contributions and wages below Modern Award trigger back-pay liability, penalties. | Back-pay liability (6+ years × 11.5% super), penalties, ATO investigation, staff grievances, reputational damage | Payroll processed by qualified provider; annual payroll audit against Award; super verified quarterly |
| **6. Patient Privacy & Data Security** | Privacy Act breach requires notification to OAIC and affected patients; regulatory investigation; reputational damage. | Data breach → notification costs + regulatory fines + reputational damage + patient loss + cyber incident response costs | Strong IT security (MFA, antivirus, encryption); staff training; cyber insurance; incident response plan; regular security audits |
| **7. Professional Indemnity Insurance** | Clinical negligence claim without insurance = uninsured liability; potential personal bankruptcy. | Patient negligence claim → no insurance coverage → personal liability → legal costs → potential bankruptcy | Obtain professional indemnity insurance; verify coverage annually; understand exclusions; report incidents to insurer |
| **8. Vaccine Cold Chain** | Cold chain breach = vaccine spoilage = patients receive ineffective vaccines = patient safety incident; regulatory consequence. | Spoiled vaccines → unaware patients vaccinated with ineffective vaccines → outbreak risk → mandatory reporting → accreditation impact → clinical liability | Quality refrigerator with alarm; daily temperature logs; staff training; regular fridge maintenance; backup fridge |
| **9. Mandatory Reporting (Child/Elder Abuse, Patient Safety)** | Failure to report suspicion of abuse/safety concern = criminal liability for staff and owner; patient safety deteriorates. | Child abuse not reported → victim continues to suffer → criminal investigation → prosecution → personal jail risk → clinic closure | Staff training on mandatory reporting; clear reporting procedures; documented reports; legal support |
| **10. Lease & Premises Stability** | Lease expiry without renewal = loss of clinic premises; 60–90 days to find/fit-out new space = disruption, relocation costs, patient loss. | Lease not renewed → clinic forced to relocate → business disruption → patient appointments cancelled → patient loss → staff turnover → revenue decline | Lease renewal option negotiated in advance; 12-month notice of renewal requirement; relocation contingency plan; make-good budget |

**Operational Framework for Non-Negotiables:**

Each of the 10 items above must have:
1. **Assigned Owner**: Named person responsible (owner or delegated manager with escalation path).
2. **Monitoring Frequency**: How often reviewed (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly).
3. **Red Flag Threshold**: What triggers escalation/action.
4. **Contingency Plan**: What happens if primary person unavailable.
5. **Documentation**: Process documented; staff trained; audit trail.

**Recommended:** Owner creates a "Non-Negotiables Dashboard" (one-page visual summary) and reviews weekly.
