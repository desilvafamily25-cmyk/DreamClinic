# DREAMCLINIC OPERATOR'S MANUAL
## PART 3: CORE OPERATIONAL TASK CATEGORIES 1–10

---

## CATEGORY 1 — GOVERNANCE AND OWNERSHIP

### What Has to Be Done

- **ABN Maintenance and ASIC Registry Liaison:** Verify ABN is current and active with ATO; confirm all ASIC filings (annual updates) are submitted before 28 April each financial year; ensure company number matches all banking, insurance and regulatory documents; lodge any changes to registered office or directors' details within 28 days to ASIC.
- **ACN / Company Register Updates:** Maintain a master copy of the ASIC Company Extract (updated annually); verify all directors' and secretaries' names, addresses and identification are accurate and match signatures in the company register; flag and correct any discrepancies immediately.
- **Director and Shareholder Obligations Tracking:** Document all directors' duties under the Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) and Health Practitioner Regulation National Law; create a director checklist covering insolvent trading prevention, proper record-keeping, financial reporting obligations, and related-party transaction disclosures; schedule annual director training on GP clinic-specific compliance.
- **Business Name Renewal (if sole trader or partnership):** If operating as sole trader or partnership, renew business name with ASIC every 3 years (due date on renewal notice); maintain proof of registration for insurance and Medicare credentialing; alert accountant 60 days before expiry to avoid lapsed registration.
- **Trust Deed Compliance (if applicable):** If clinic operates as a trust (common for GP partnerships), obtain and review trust deed annually; confirm trustee details, beneficiary appointments, and distribution rules are documented; ensure all trustees have signed the deed and copies are held in secure custody; cross-reference with accountant regarding trust return lodgements.
- **Banking Authorisations and Signatory Management:** Maintain an up-to-date list of all bank account signatories (practice must have minimum 2 authorised signers per account for financial controls); notify bank immediately if signatory leaves (provide signed form); confirm no "dormant" signatories remain active in banking system; review signing authorities annually (dual signing for payments over $50,000 recommended).
- **Accountant Liaison and Structured Entity Advice:** Schedule quarterly meetings with GP clinic accountant to review corporate structure suitability (sole trader vs company vs trust implications for tax, superannuation, personal liability); confirm entity structure aligns with ownership intentions and minimises tax exposure; document any recommendations in writing and file.
- **Ownership Transition and Succession Planning:** Document buy/sell arrangements if multiple owners; maintain a succession plan identifying who steps into each clinical and management role if key owner/director becomes unable to work; establish exit valuation methodology (asset-based vs revenue multiple) and hold annually; engage lawyer for binding succession documents.
- **Corporate Register Maintenance:** Keep a bound corporate register (physical or digital) containing: minutes of all board/management meetings, share certificates (if company with share capital), director appointment/resignation records, mortgage and security documents, and all shareholder resolutions; ensure register is housed in clinic premises or solicitor's office with access controlled.
- **Share Certificate Custody and Transfer Registry:** If company has issued share certificates, store originals in clinic safe or lawyer's trust account; maintain a share register listing all certificate numbers, holder names, and issue dates; notify accountant immediately of any share transfers; require all share transactions to be documented and filed with ASIC (update statement of shareholding).

### Who Usually Does It

**Primary:** Clinic Owner (GP principal/director) with external accountant and lawyer support | **Backup:** Business Manager or Finance Manager (for administrative record-keeping and renewal reminder tracking)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Annual ASIC Company Extract and any director/shareholder changes
- Trust deed and any amendments (if trust structure)
- Banking signatory authorities and changes
- Buy/sell agreements and succession plan
- Any communications from ASIC, ATO or corporate regulator regarding compliance breaches

### Documents / Systems Involved

- ASIC online portal (asic.gov.au) for company search and filing
- ATO ABN Lookup (abr.business.gov.au)
- Bank mandate and signatory forms
- Company register or trust deed documentation
- Accountant working papers and annual advice
- Lawyer's file (succession documents, trust deed, shareholder agreements)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **ASIC non-compliance:** Failure to lodge annual updates or correct director details can result in ASIC striking the company off the register, making clinic unable to hold provider numbers, claim Medicare, or operate legally. Financial penalty up to AUD $3,300 per breach; director personally liable for company debts incurred while company was not registered.
- **Director personal liability:** Failure to prevent insolvent trading exposes directors to personal liability for company debts exceeding company assets; ASIC can pursue directors for breaching their duties, resulting in director disqualification and personal financial recovery orders.
- **Banking dysfunction:** If signatory list is incorrect or outdated, clinic may be unable to pay staff, suppliers or liabilities; incorrect signatories can result in fraudulent payments.
- **Tax exposure:** If structure is not optimised, clinic may overpay income tax or superannuation; trust deed non-compliance can result in ATO reassessment and back-tax liability plus interest (10% p.a.) and penalties.
- **Succession failure:** Absence of documented succession plan can lead to family disputes, unplanned owner exit, forced sale of practice at unfavourable valuation, or temporary closure affecting patient care and staff employment.
- **Share transfer disputes:** Lack of share register or transfer documentation creates ownership ambiguity, leading to disputes among partners and potential costly legal proceedings (costs AUD $20,000+).

## CATEGORY 2 — FINANCE AND CASHFLOW

### What Has to Be Done

- **Daily Banking Check and Medicare EFT Reconciliation:** Log into clinic bank account each business day (or nominate manager to do so); verify that Medicare Electronic Fund Transfer (EFT) deposits match expected amounts based on daily MBS claim submissions; cross-reference deposits against PRODA/HI Service claim history or Medicare Online receipt list; flag any deposits that are lower than expected or delayed (Medicare standard is 2–5 business days); document reconciliation in clinic finance log.
- **EFTPOS Merchant Settlement Reconciliation:** At close of business daily, reconcile EFTPOS terminal summary (Amex, Visa, Mastercard) against till and patient billing records; verify total settlement amount matches expected private billing collected; flag any unmatched transactions or chargebacks; confirm merchant deposit arrives in bank account within 1–2 business days; resolve any terminal errors or declined transactions immediately.
- **Accounts Payable Processing and Supplier Invoice Management:** Establish a centralised accounts payable log (spreadsheet or accounting system); receive all supplier invoices (pharmaceutical wholesalers, stationery, utilities, rent, medical equipment suppliers, IT vendors, insurance brokers); verify each invoice against purchase order and delivery docket; code invoice to correct GL account (consumables, fixed assets, overheads); obtain approval from practice manager or owner before payment; pay by bank transfer on supplier terms (typically 7–30 days); maintain digital file of invoices and payment proof.
- **Expense Approval Workflow and Authority Limits:** Define tiered approval limits: practice manager approves expenses under AUD $2,000; owner approves AUD $2,000–$10,000; owner + accountant joint approval for above AUD $10,000; document all approvals in email or approval form; publish approval limits to staff and suppliers; ensure no payment is made without documented approval; review approval log monthly for unusual patterns or policy breaches.
- **Petty Cash Management and Accountability:** If clinic maintains petty cash float (recommended maximum AUD $500), assign one staff member as custodian; require receipts for all withdrawals; reconcile physical cash to register daily (cash in hand must equal float amount plus any pending receipts); conduct surprise cash count monthly by second staff member; document cash count date and signature; reimburse custodian from clinic bank account monthly to top up float; maintain petty cash register log with dates, amounts and expense categories.
- **BAS Preparation Data Compilation:** From monthly financial records, extract: (1) total GST collected on private billing (15% of private fees); (2) GST paid on supplier invoices (input credits, claimed back against GST collected); (3) PAYG withholding amounts paid to ATO for staff and contractors; prepare summary spreadsheet quarterly (end of each tax quarter: 30 September, 31 December, 31 March, 30 June); hand to accountant 1 week before BAS due date (21st of following month for activity statement) to ensure timely lodge and avoid penalties.
- **Cash Flow Forecasting (4-Week Rolling Forecast):** Create a simple 4-week cash flow projection spreadsheet; project all known cash inflows (Medicare EFT deposits, private billing, other income) and outflows (payroll, supplier payments, rent, loan repayments); update weekly as actual transactions occur; identify any weeks where cash position drops below minimum operating balance (recommend minimum AUD $15,000–$25,000 depending on clinic size); flag 2 weeks in advance if additional borrowing or owner capital injection will be needed; use forecast to plan discretionary spending and capital purchases.
- **Bank Reconciliation (Monthly):** At month-end, print or download bank statement from clinic bank account; tick off all deposits and payments against clinic cash book (accounting system); identify any bank fees, interest or unusual items; investigate any uncleared cheques or outstanding deposits; ensure reconciliation balances exactly (bank balance = accounting system balance); document reconciliation by printing bank statement and filing with month-end accounting records; flag any errors or fraud indicators immediately.
- **Overdue Creditor Management and Vendor Follow-Up:** Generate aged creditor report monthly from accounting system; identify any invoices outstanding over 30 days past due date; categorise: (1) invoices in dispute (query with supplier), (2) invoices missed in processing (pay immediately), (3) invoices requiring approval (escalate for approval); contact suppliers of accounts overdue 45+ days to confirm invoice status and negotiate payment plan if necessary; avoid paying one supplier early to disadvantage another (maintain fair creditor relations); escalate unpaid accounts over 60 days to owner for strategic decision (is vendor relationship worth saving?).
- **Capital Expenditure Approvals and Lease vs Buy Decisions:** Any purchase of clinic assets expected to last 3+ years (e.g., new clinical equipment, IT server, furniture, renovations) requires owner approval and should be assessed: (1) outright purchase vs finance lease; (2) hire/rental vs buy; (3) depreciation impact on tax and cash; request quotes from minimum 2 suppliers; document business case (e.g., "upgrade existing patient monitor to comply with RACGP Standards"); submit approval form to owner with supporting quotes; once approved, process purchase order and supplier invoice per standard accounts payable workflow.

### Who Usually Does It

**Primary:** Finance Manager or Business Manager (day-to-day banking checks, EFTPOS reconciliation, accounts payable, petty cash, bank reconciliation) | **Backup:** Clinic Owner/Director (approval of high-value expenses, cash flow review, creditor escalation)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Bank reconciliation (monthly)
- Cash flow forecast (weekly or bi-weekly)
- BAS preparation summary before lodgement
- Approval of expenses over authority limits
- Aged creditor report and overdue follow-up decisions
- Any discrepancies in Medicare EFT deposits vs claims submitted

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Clinic bank account and online banking portal
- EFTPOS terminal and merchant statement
- Accounting software (MYOB, Xero, QuickBooks, Reckon) or spreadsheet
- Medicare Online portal and claim submission history
- Supplier invoices and purchase orders
- ATO BAS form and due date calendar
- Monthly bank statement

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Cash flow crisis:** Failure to forecast 4-week cash flow can result in clinic being unable to meet payroll, resulting in staff non-payment, potential employee claims to Fair Work Commission, and reputational damage (cost AUD $20,000–$50,000+ in penalties and remediation).
- **Unpaid superannuation and PAYG:** If clinic runs out of cash and delays paying superannuation or PAYG withholding, ATO will issue penalty interest at 10% p.a. plus administrative penalties of AUD $510+ per month late; director may be held personally liable under Director Penalty Notice regime.
- **Supplier relationship breakdown:** Unchecked overdue accounts lead to suppliers cutting off credit, refusing to supply essential items (vaccines, consumables), and potentially suing clinic for unpaid invoices; could result in trading stoppage and patient safety incidents.
- **Bank reconciliation errors:** Failure to reconcile monthly allows undetected fraud (staff theft, forged cheques, erroneous bank charges) to accumulate; could result in AUD $5,000–$50,000+ loss before detection.
- **GST compliance failure:** Incorrect BAS preparation (wrong GST collected vs paid) triggers ATO audit; clinic may owe back GST, penalties, and interest; failure to lodge BAS on time incurs penalties of AUD $210+ per month overdue.
- **CapEx mismanagement:** Failing to obtain owner approval for large purchases can result in unauthorised expenditure, disputes with owner partners, and poor purchasing decisions (overpaid for inferior equipment).

## CATEGORY 3 — PAYROLL AND HR

### What Has to Be Done

- **Timesheet Collection and Approval:** Establish a standard timesheet form (paper or digital via software like Deputy, Time & Attendance) requiring staff to record start time, end time, breaks taken, and signature weekly or fortnightly; collect completed timesheets by COB on Thursday (if weekly) or by cycle end date; review each timesheet for: (1) total hours worked vs employment contract hours, (2) any overtime or weekend/public holiday flags, (3) leave dates tallied against leave approval records; obtain approver signature (manager or owner) before payroll run; file timesheet with payroll records for 7-year retention.
- **Payroll Processing (Weekly or Fortnightly):** Based on approved timesheets, calculate gross pay per employee (salary + hourly overtime at correct rates per Health Professionals Award or Nurses Award 2020); calculate PAYG tax withholding per ATO Tax Tables and any tax-free threshold election; deduct superannuation contribution (11.5% minimum Superannuation Guarantee for employees earning over AUD $280/week); apply any salary sacrifice or voluntary deductions (health insurance, union fees, etc.); run payroll via payroll software (STP-enabled: ADP, Paycheckplus, Employment Hero, MYOB Payroll) or accountant's payroll service; generate payslips and email to staff 1 day before payment; process payment via bank transfer (recommended) or cheque; retain payroll register.
- **PAYG Withholding and Remittance to ATO:** For each pay run, calculate total PAYG withheld from all staff (sum of individual tax withholdings); total for financial quarter should match your quarterly BAS; by 25th of the month following quarter-end, lodge PAYG payment to ATO via bank transfer with correct tax reference number; confirm payment receipt from ATO (check transaction reference); reconcile quarterly PAYG paid to TPAR (Tax Payment Account Register) or ATO MyTax account; failure to remit by due date incurs penalty interest at 10% p.a. from original due date.
- **Superannuation Processing and Guarantee Compliance (11.5% SG):** For each employee earning average weekly ordinary time earnings over AUD $280, calculate 11.5% Superannuation Guarantee (SG) contribution based on ordinary time earnings (exclude commission, bonus, allowances in most cases); contribute to each employee's nominated super fund (typically employee nominates fund on employment; if no nomination, contribute to MySuper default); contributions must be made by 28th day of month following quarter-end (e.g., quarter ended 30 June, payment due 28 July); for fortnightly payroll, accumulate and pay lump sum quarterly, OR pay within 3 business days of each pay (if monthly/weekly, less common); confirm contributions posted to employee accounts (super fund statement or member portal); maintain super contributions register listing employee names, fund details, contribution amounts and dates; reconcile quarterly contributions to payroll records; failure to pay SG on time incurs penalties of 200% of unpaid amount (AUD 23 for every AUD 10 owed if late).
- **Award Compliance Checks (Health Professionals Award 2010 and Nurses Award 2020):** Confirm all nursing and health professional staff are remunerated at or above Award minimum rates; for nurses, refer to Nurses Award 2020 base rates (varies by classification: RN Level 1–5, EN Level 1–3); for other health professionals (AHAs, Allied Health), refer to Health Professionals and Support Services Award 2010 (Level 1–5 rates); cross-check employment contracts with Award base rates; identify any staff below Award rates and bring into compliance immediately (back-pay any shortfall); if pay is below Award, document exceptional circumstances (e.g., agreed "learning wage" for trainee, written consent to work part-time hours); verify Award rates annually (typically updated 1 July each year) and adjust staff pay; maintain Award rate comparison spreadsheet.
- **Single Touch Payroll (STP) Reporting and ATO Compliance:** From 1 July 2018, all payroll software must support STP (real-time payroll reporting to ATO); confirm your payroll software is ATO-certified STP provider; at each pay run, payroll system automatically submits employment income data to ATO (PAYG withheld, super contributions, employment status); within 28 days of each quarter-end (financial quarter: 30 Sep, 31 Dec, 31 Mar, 30 Jun), verify STP submissions have been received by ATO (check ATO Online Services Portal or payroll software "STP submission status"); if submission fails, resend immediately; no need to lodge separate PAYG Variation report or Annual PAYG Payment Summary if STP data is correct; conduct annual reconciliation (by 31 July following financial year-end) between STP submitted data and TPAR.
- **Leave Accrual Maintenance and Compliance:** Track accrual of annual leave (4 weeks per year, or 4.33 weeks if 5-day week), personal leave/sick leave (10 days per year, often accrues concurrently with annual leave in some awards), long service leave (1 week per year after 5 years, scaling up), and parental leave (Fair Work Act, 12 months unpaid if eligible); maintain a leave register per employee showing: (1) opening balance, (2) accrual each pay period, (3) leave taken (dates, type, hours/days), (4) closing balance; issue leave balance statement to each employee at least annually (preferred: quarterly); monitor leave-in-lieu arrangements (if applicable for salaried staff) and ensure not used to negate statutory leave entitlements; on termination, pay out unused annual leave in full; calculate long service leave payout on termination (minimum 1 week after 5 years continuous service); ensure all leave calculations comply with the National Employment Standards and relevant Award.
- **Payroll Tax Monthly/Annual Lodgement (Victoria ~4.85%):** In Victoria, if annual payroll exceeds threshold (AUD $10 million in 2024–25, indexed annually), clinic must register for payroll tax and lodge monthly/quarterly returns; calculate: payroll tax = (annual salary bill minus AUD $10 million threshold) × 4.85%; lodge returns with State Revenue Office (SRO) by 25th of following month; pay by same date to avoid penalties; if below threshold, no payroll tax due but must still register with SRO if requested; maintain payroll tax compliance file with SRO correspondence and payment receipts; if payroll grows above threshold mid-year, notify SRO immediately and commence lodgements.
- **Fair Work Record-Keeping Obligations and Staff Files:** Maintain individual staff file for each employee (minimum 7-year retention from termination date) containing: (1) original signed employment contract with position title, salary, award classification, hours, and start date; (2) recruitment documents (CV, interview notes, offer letter); (3) all performance reviews, emails and correspondence; (4) training records (see Category 10); (5) leave records and approval documentation; (6) payslips (all years of employment); (7) superannuation contribution confirmation; (8) disciplinary action records (if any, with fair process documentation); (9) termination letter and final payslip on exit; store files in locked cabinet or secure digital folder (password-protected); ensure staff access only authorised by manager; on staff termination, close file and retain for 7 years; make files available to Fair Work Ombudsman if inspected.
- **Staff File Maintenance and Document Control:** Designate one person (HR Manager or Business Manager) as custodian of all staff files; establish a staff file log listing all active employees and location of their files; update files within 1 week of significant events (hire, promotion, leave, performance issue, termination); conduct annual file audit to identify missing documents (e.g., missed performance review); ensure all files have consistent structure and required documents; redact sensitive information (medical details, tax file number) from shared copies; provide staff member with copy of their file upon request (within 10 business days per Privacy Act); securely destroy files 7 years after staff termination (shred or certified data destruction).

### Who Usually Does It

**Primary:** Business Manager or HR Manager (timesheet collection, payroll processing, leave tracking, staff file maintenance) | **Backup:** Finance Manager (superannuation processing, payroll tax lodgement, Award compliance checks)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Payroll budget vs actual spend (monthly)
- Any staff pay disputes or Award compliance queries
- Superannuation contributions (quarterly verification)
- Leave accrual accuracy (annually)
- Staff file audit results
- Payroll tax liability and lodgement (if applicable)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Payroll software (ADP, Paycheckplus, Employment Hero, MYOB Payroll) with STP certification
- Timesheet forms or Deputy app
- Employment contracts and Award rate reference documents
- ATO Tax Tables and PAYG calculator
- Fair Work Commission Modern Awards website (Nurses Award 2020, Health Professionals Award 2010)
- Superannuation fund member portal
- ATO Online Services Portal (STP submission status)
- Victorian State Revenue Office (SRO) portal (if payroll tax liable)
- Staff files and leave register spreadsheet

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Award underpayment:** Staff remunerated below Award minimum rates can lodge claim with Fair Work Commission; clinic liable to back-pay shortfall plus costs; potential award of additional penalty compensation (up to AUD $20,700 per affected employee); reputational damage and staff morale loss.
- **Superannuation non-compliance:** Failure to contribute full 11.5% SG by due date triggers ATO penalty at 200% of unpaid amount (e.g., AUD 1,000 unpaid SG = AUD 2,000 penalty plus 10% interest p.a.); employees may lodge complaint with ATO or Fair Work Ombudsman; director personally liable under Director Penalty Notice if pattern of non-compliance.
- **PAYG and STP failures:** Incorrect PAYG withholding or failure to submit STP data triggers ATO audit; clinic required to remit shortfall plus 10% interest p.a. from original due date; penalty up to AUD 1,254 for non-lodgement; director liability if negligent.
- **Payroll tax penalties (Victoria):** Late payment of payroll tax incurs penalty interest at 10% p.a. plus administrative penalty of AUD 500+ per month late; if threshold crossed mid-year and not declared, penalty and interest on full liability.
- **Fair Work breaches:** Lack of proper staff files or employment contracts enables Fair Work Inspector to issue compliance notices; failure to remedy incurs penalties starting at AUD 1,254+ per breach; unfair dismissal claims can cost AUD 20,000–$50,000+ in compensation and legal fees.
- **Staff disputes and claims:** Inadequate timesheet or leave records lead to disputes over unpaid entitlements; staff claims to Fair Work Commission cost minimum AUD 5,000–$15,000+ to defend; loss of staff goodwill affects recruitment and retention.

## CATEGORY 4 — DOCTOR PAYMENTS AND CONTRACTOR RECONCILIATIONS

### What Has to Be Done

- **Monthly Billing Extraction per GP from Clinical Software:** Log into clinical software (Best Practice, Medical Director, Genie, HotDoc) using admin or reporting credentials; run monthly billing report by GP name/ID for each provider (filter by date range: e.g., 1–30 June 2026); export report to spreadsheet showing: (1) each MBS item claimed, (2) patient name, (3) fee (bulk bill amount or private fee), (4) date of service, (5) claim status (submitted/paid/rejected); sum all fees per GP for the month; cross-check total against Medicare deposits received and any private billing collected directly; investigate any gaps or inconsistencies with clinical software vendor or GP directly.
- **Percentage Calculation and Payment Methodology (Typically 65–70% to GP):** Establish a written GP payment agreement with each GP (employee or contractor) specifying: (1) percentage of collected fees payable to GP (e.g., 65% of bulk bill, 70% of private), (2) any deductions (e.g., clinic overheads, equipment, software licences), (3) payment frequency (monthly, fortnightly); calculate GP payment by month: (Bulk Bill Revenue × 65%) + (Private Revenue × 70%) − (Agreed Deductions); example: if GP billed AUD 100,000 bulk, AUD 50,000 private, payment = (AUD 100k × 65%) + (AUD 50k × 70%) = AUD 65,000 + AUD 35,000 = AUD 100,000 (note: deductions vary by agreement); prepare payment calculation spreadsheet and provide GP with copy showing itemised breakdown; obtain owner approval before payment.
- **Payment by EFT or Cheque:** Once GP payment calculated and approved, process payment by bank transfer (EFT) to GP's nominated bank account (preferred method) or cheque if GP requests; require GP to provide bank account details and BSB on hire form or update annually; for contractors/locums, obtain completed Tax File Number Declaration (TFN Declaration) before first payment; send payment notification email to GP with payment amount, date, and breakdown of calculation; retain proof of payment (bank transfer reference, cheque copy) in GP payment file.
- **ABN Collection and Verification (Contractors/Locums):** If GP is engaged as contractor rather than employee, obtain Australian Business Number (ABN) verification before engagement; request copy of GP's ABN Certificate from ABN Lookup (free online at abr.business.gov.au); confirm ABN is active and entity name matches GP's name or trading name; retain ABN Certificate in contractor file; require contractor to provide TFN Declaration form (IR-2 or updated TFN declaration under STP); do NOT claim GST input credit on contractor payments if contractor is not registered for GST (most GPs are not GST-registered for health services as they're GST-exempt). Confirm GST treatment with accountant before first payment.
- **GST Obligations and Health Services Exemption:** Most GPs are GST-exempt on health services (Division 2 Supplies under A New Tax System (Goods and Services Tax) Act 1999); clinic should NOT charge GST on bulk bill or private GP consulting fees; if clinic is GST-registered (e.g., for non-health services or clinic management services), clarify with accountant whether clinic can claim input credits on practice expenses but NOT charge GST on patient fees (typical arrangement); do NOT pay GST to contractor GPs unless they explicitly request it in writing (very rare); clarify GST treatment in written GP agreement.
- **Income Statement / PAYG Summary for Employee GPs or Annual Payment Summaries for Contractors:** For employee GPs: include GP salary and any bonuses in clinic payroll; generate annual PAYG Payment Summary (via STP) by 31 July following financial year-end; issue to GP showing: gross salary, PAYG withheld, superannuation contributed, and any salary sacrifices; for contractor GPs: prepare annual payment summary (non-statutory document) by 30 June showing: (1) total fees paid to contractor over year, (2) total claimed by contractor per MBS extraction, (3) any deductions, (4) net payment; provide to contractor by 30 June (or as contractually required) for their tax return preparation; inform contractor to report payment on their tax return as personal income.
- **Reconciliation of GP Payments to Medicare Deposits:** Monthly, cross-check: (1) total GP payments made for the month, (2) Medicare EFT deposits received for the month, (3) private billing collected; ensure Medicare deposits match expected amount based on bulk bill claims submitted; if deposits are lower than expected, investigate with Medicare Online or HI Service (delays in processing, claims rejections, DVA/WorkCover claims); reconcile payments to cashflow (if GPs are paid higher percentage than Medicare deposits received, shortfall must be covered by clinic reserve or private billing); document reconciliation in monthly finance review; flag to owner if Medicare funding is insufficient to cover agreed GP payments (indicates unsustainable payment model or billing problem).
- **Red Flag Monitoring for Billing Anomalies and Fraud Prevention:** Establish automated alerts in clinical software or create monthly review checklist to identify: (1) unusually high billing per GP (compared to peer GPs or historical average), (2) suspicious item number claims (e.g., excessive health assessment items 701–707, mental health items 2700/2701 claims by non-mental health GP), (3) claims for patients with no notes or short consultations, (4) bulk bill claims for patients normally paying private, (5) multiple claims same patient same day, (6) bulk bill % significantly different from clinic average; if anomaly detected, discuss with GP and request clinical notes for review; if pattern suggests deliberate over-claiming or fraud, escalate to owner and consider notification to Medicare Integrity (13 20 11) or Australian Medical Association ethics hotline; maintain fraud prevention log with actions taken.

### Who Usually Does It

**Primary:** Finance Manager or Business Manager (monthly billing extraction, payment calculation, payment processing) | **Backup:** Clinic Owner/GP Principal (approval of payment amounts, fraud investigation, GP payment agreement negotiation)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- GP payment calculation and approval (monthly)
- Reconciliation of GP payments to Medicare deposits (monthly)
- Any billing anomalies or red flags identified
- Annual income statements/payment summaries before issue to GPs
- New GP payment agreements and contractor ABN/TFN documentation

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Clinical software (Best Practice, Medical Director, Genie, HotDoc) — billing report function
- Spreadsheet for payment calculation and reconciliation
- GP employment contract or contractor agreement specifying payment percentage
- Medicare Online portal and claim submission history
- Bank account details form for EFT payment
- ABN Lookup (abr.business.gov.au)
- TFN Declaration form (IR-2)
- ATO and Medicare fraud reporting contacts

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Underpayment disputes:** GPs underpaid due to incorrect billing extraction or calculation can lodge wage claim with Fair Work Commission or civil claim for breach of contract; clinic liable for unpaid amount plus interest and legal costs (AUD 10,000–$30,000+).
- **Billing integrity failure:** Undetected fraudulent claims by GP (overclaiming, false items, patient upcoding) result in Medicare recovery action; Medicare may audit clinic and demand repayment of fraud amount plus interest (20% p.a.); clinic and GPs jointly liable; referral to police and medical board (AHPRA).
- **Contractor tax compliance:** If contractor engaged without valid ABN or TFN, clinic may be liable to remit PAYG on contractor income to ATO; failure to do so incurs ATO penalties; contractor may face ATO action for unrecorded income.
- **GST errors:** Incorrect GST treatment on contractor payments can trigger ATO audit of clinic GST returns; clinic may owe back GST and penalties if input credits incorrectly claimed.
- **Medicare recovery:** Discrepancies between GP payments and Medicare deposits can indicate claims that were rejected or not yet processed; if clinic covers shortfall from reserves long-term, indicates billing or patient mix problem requiring urgent strategic review.

## CATEGORY 5 — NURSE STAFFING AND NURSING OPERATIONS

### What Has to Be Done

- **Immunisation Program Delivery and Schedule Compliance:** Maintain current vaccination schedules (National Immunisation Program — NIP Schedule, published by Australian Department of Health and Aged Care); deliver NIP vaccines at scheduled ages (e.g., 6 weeks: hexavalent + rotavirus + pneumococcal 13v; 12 months: MMR; 18 months: varicella; school-age: HPV, diphtheria/tetanus); for adult/travel vaccines, refer to ATAGI (Australian Technical Advisory Group on Immunisation) recommendations; train nursing staff on correct vaccine administration (intramuscular vs oral), timing of doses, spacing of live vaccines (28 days apart), and contraindications; for NIP vaccines, obtain supply via state immunisation program (Victoria: Vicinal) at no cost; for private vaccines (travel, private HPV if out of funded age, shingles Shingrix, COVID boosters), arrange supply via pharmaceutical wholesaler with clinic account; document each vaccination administered in patient clinical record and in Australian Immunisation Register (AIR) within 24 hours (see below); educate parents/patients on vaccine side effects and fever management (paracetamol safe, not aspirin for children).
- **Wound Care and Dressing Management Protocols:** Establish written protocols for common wound care presentations (minor lacerations, chronic leg ulcers, diabetic ulcers, surgical wound reviews); train nurses on asepsis (hand hygiene, sterile field, non-touch technique), wound assessment (signs of infection, healing progression), and dressing selection (occlusive, antimicrobial, compression); stock wound care consumables: saline, swabs, gauze, dressings (Comfeel, Opsite, etc.), tape, gloves, hand sanitiser; for chronic wound management, involve GP in wound assessment at least quarterly; refer to diabetes educator if diabetic ulcer; escalate infected wounds to GP immediately (may require oral/IV antibiotics); document each wound assessment, dressing applied, and plan in patient record; comply with RACGP infection control standards (see Category 10).
- **Chronic Disease Management (CDM) and Care Plan Coordination:** Identify patients with chronic conditions eligible for CDM under Medicare (diabetes, cardiovascular disease, asthma, COPD, chronic kidney disease, mental illness); for each eligible patient, GP prepares: (1) GP Management Plan (item 721) — comprehensive care plan created by GP at patient first consultation for CDM (fees: GP AUD 132–192 bulk, patient cost if private); (2) Diabetes Management Plan (item 723) — similar for diabetes patients. Nurse role: (a) assist GP in patient assessment and history-taking for plan development, (b) coordinate allied health reviews (physiotherapy, dietetics, podiatry) and ensure reports returned to clinic, (c) track review dates and remind patient to attend allied health appointments, (d) maintain CDM register listing all enrolled patients and plan review dates, (e) ensure GP reviews plan annually (or every 12 months) and updates if condition changes; CDM is also coordinated with health assessment items (701–707) for at-risk populations; compliance tracking: audit percentage of eligible patients enrolled in CDM monthly.
- **AHPRA Registration Monitoring and Renewal Reminders:** For all registered nurses (RN), enrolled nurses (EN), and allied health professionals, monitor AHPRA registration status; before employing any health professional, verify registration on AHPRA website (ahpra.gov.au); record registration number and expiry date in staff file; set calendar reminder 3 months before expiry date; communicate to staff member via email with AHPRA renewal link and deadline (typically annual renewal 30 November for most professions, though dates vary); request updated registration certificate from staff member upon renewal; maintain register of all clinic staff AHPRA registrations (spreadsheet with name, profession, registration number, expiry date, renewal date completed); if staff member fails to renew, immediately remove from clinical duties and notify owner; do NOT permit lapsed registrations to continue working (potential patient safety risk, professional misconduct, clinic liability).
- **Vaccine Ordering (NIP State Supply vs Private Purchase):** Establish supply chain: (1) NIP vaccines (free, state-supplied via Victorian state program, currently via Vicinal); order quarterly by submitting forecast to Vicinal (based on immunisation clinic schedule and patient numbers); confirm delivery dates and check shipment for correct items and expiry dates; (2) Private vaccines (travel vaccines, private HPV, shingles, COVID boosters if not covered by funded program); establish account with 2–3 pharmaceutical wholesalers (e.g., Symbios, API, Pharmaec) and order as needed; budget approximately AUD 3,000–5,000 per quarter for private vaccine supply depending on travel clinic volume; maintain stock list and reorder when stock falls below 2-week supply; check expiry dates monthly and remove expired stock; coordinate with reception staff if patients need to pre-book travel vaccine appointments and ensure vaccines are in stock before confirmation.
- **Cold Chain Temperature Monitoring and Documentation:** Vaccines must be stored at 2–8°C continuously to maintain potency; install qualified refrigerator with internal thermometer (not freezer — freezing damages most vaccines); place min/max thermometer in fridge and check daily: record temperature on cold chain log at start of business each day; temperature must stay between 2–8°C at all times; if temperature falls below 2°C or rises above 8°C, remove affected vaccines, do NOT use, and report to Vicinal/supplier (vaccine viability assessment required); conduct monthly maintenance of fridge (defrost if manual, check door seal, clean interior); arrange annual professional servicing of fridge; if fridge fails, have backup fridge available or store vaccines in temporary ice pack container (4-hour maximum); maintain cold chain log for 7 years (regulatory requirement for immunisation).
- **Equipment Checks and Maintenance Schedule:** Maintain list of clinical equipment requiring regular checking: (1) resuscitation trolley — check weekly: ensure all drugs in stock and in-date (adrenaline, amiodarone, etc.), check defibrillator pads in-date, check airway equipment present (Ambu bag, oropharyngeal airways); (2) automated external defibrillator (AED) — check monthly: battery status, pad expiry dates; (3) blood pressure cuffs (manual and automatic) — check monthly for accuracy and functioning; (4) otoscope/ophthalmoscope — check quarterly for functioning; (5) autoclave (if sterilisation on-site) — run validation test monthly using biological indicators; (6) pulse oximeter — check calibration quarterly; document all checks on maintenance log with date, name of staff member, any issues found, and actions taken; address any defects immediately (repair or replace); ensure critical equipment (resus trolley, AED, BP cuff) never left non-functional.
- **Nursing Policy Compliance and Staff Education:** Document nursing policies in writing covering: hand hygiene (before/after patient contact, before aseptic procedure), sharps safety (never recap needles, use sharps bin, disposal procedure), infection control (PPE use, patient isolation if needed), immunisation protocols (site, technique, observation period), wound care (asepsis, dressing, handover), medication handling (if nurses administer), and privacy (patient confidentiality, data security). Distribute policies to all nursing staff in orientation. Conduct quarterly in-service training on compliance with policies; audit compliance monthly (e.g., observe hand hygiene practice, inspect sharps bins, review immunisation records for completeness); provide feedback to non-compliant staff and retrain if necessary; maintain training attendance records per staff member.
- **Immunisation Register Updates and AIR Reporting:** Every vaccination administered must be recorded in: (1) patient clinical record (date, vaccine name, batch number, site, nurse initials); (2) Australian Immunisation Register (AIR) — national registry managed by Services Australia; AIR reporting: use AIR interface in clinical software or upload batch file to AIR portal (digital.health.gov.au); report within 24 hours of administration (preferred), maximum 14 days; confirmation of successful upload received from AIR (check regularly); verify by running monthly reconciliation of clinic vaccinations vs AIR records (use AIR batch report feature); if discrepancies (missed uploads), resend immediately; monitor for duplicate entries and correct. AIR is used by parents for checking vaccine status (MyGov app), schools for entry requirements, travel to overseas (proof of vaccination), and by ATO for No Jab No Pay policy (childcare subsidies). Compliance tracking: monthly audit showing % vaccines reported to AIR within 24 hours (target 100%).

### Who Usually Does It

**Primary:** Registered Nurse (RN) or Enrolled Nurse (EN) — vaccine administration, wound care, CDM coordination, immunisation register | **Backup:** Practice Nurse or Health Worker with appropriate training; nursing manager/owner supervises register compliance and staff training.

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- AHPRA registration status of all nursing staff (quarterly)
- Monthly cold chain temperature log review (check for breaches)
- Immunisation register compliance report (% reported to AIR on time)
- Resuscitation trolley checklist (monthly spot-check)
- Any clinical incident reports related to immunisation or wound care
- CDM register and % of eligible patients enrolled (quarterly)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- ATAGI immunisation schedule and guidelines (atagi.health.gov.au)
- National Immunisation Program vaccine supply and ordering (Vicinal or state equivalent)
- Pharmaceutical wholesaler accounts for private vaccine supply
- Clinical software with vaccine module and AIR upload capability
- AIR portal (digital.health.gov.au) for batch reporting
- Cold chain log (temperature record)
- Equipment maintenance checklist (resus trolley, AED, BP cuff, etc.)
- Nursing policies and procedures manual
- Staff training records and AHPRA registration tracker
- CDM register (spreadsheet or software)
- AHPRA website (ahpra.gov.au) for registration verification

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Immunisation compliance failure:** Vaccines not reported to AIR within timeframe means parents/schools cannot verify vaccination status; could delay school entry (affects patient family); missed reports trigger Services Australia audit of clinic (potentially costly); if pattern of non-reporting, clinic may lose immunisation funding/accreditation.
- **Cold chain breach:** Vaccines exposed to temperatures outside 2–8°C range lose potency; if administered to patients, vaccines may not provide immunity, leaving patients at risk of preventable disease; if breach not documented, potential patient harm and liability claim (AUD 20,000+); regulator (DHHS Victoria) audit if reported.
- **AHPRA registration lapsed:** Unregistered nurse continuing to work in clinical capacity is working without legal authority; patient safety risk; clinic liable for negligence if unregistered nurse causes harm (vicarious liability); regulatory investigation and potential clinic sanctions; staff member faces professional misconduct charges.
- **Resus trolley non-compliance:** Out-of-date drugs or missing equipment means trolley non-functional in cardiac emergency; patient may not receive timely resuscitation; could result in patient death or severe harm; clinic liable for negligence (AUD 50,000+); coroner may investigate; accreditation loss.
- **CDM coordination failure:** Patients with chronic disease not enrolled in CDM miss out on subsidised allied health services; patient health deteriorates due to lack of coordinated care (e.g., diabetic patient misses diabetes educator); GP loses Medicare revenue (items 721/723); potential patient adverse outcome.
- **Wound care non-compliance:** Unsanitary technique leads to wound infection; infected wound can progress to cellulitis, abscess, or sepsis (AUD 10,000+ in hospital costs); patient claim for negligence; if diabetic patient infection leads to amputation, significant liability (AUD 50,000+).

## CATEGORY 6 — RECEPTION AND FRONT-DESK OPERATIONS

### What Has to Be Done

- **Appointment Scheduling System Management:** Clinic uses clinical software appointment book (Best Practice, Medical Director, HotDoc, HealthEngine, or equivalent); each morning, review day's schedule and confirm patient appointments are accurate (correct time, correct GP, correct reason for visit, correct room); manage waitlist and cancellations daily — if patient cancels, offer to other waiting patients on waitlist or reschedule cancellation slot with next patient; monitor appointment types and durations (e.g., standard consultation 15 mins, chronic disease management 20 mins, minor procedures 30 mins) and enforce to prevent schedule overruns and GP delays; at 3 pm daily, send appointment reminders to next-day patients via SMS or phone (reduces no-shows); manage late arrivals: patients arriving >15 mins late may be rescheduled to next available slot (at reception discretion) to avoid clinic delays; monitor DNA (did not attend) rate monthly and identify frequent DNAs (follow up with letters or calls before next booking); configure booking system to close slots 1 week in advance if practice is at capacity (prevents overbooking).
- **Patient Registration and Demographic Data Collection:** On new patient, collect and record: full name (correct spelling for Medicare), date of birth (verify against ID), Medicare card number and expiry date (scan or manually enter), home and work phone numbers, email address, residential address and postcode, emergency contact details, occupation (for WorkCover coding), Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander status (important for health assessment and funding), and relevant medical history (allergies, current medications, past surgeries). For existing patients, update demographics annually and particularly if patient reports address/phone change (important for hospital discharge communications and recalls). Store data securely in clinical software (password-protected, encrypted); do NOT store Medicare details longer than needed for immediate claim (delete scan after claim submitted); comply with Privacy Act 1988 (Cth) — obtain written consent for health information use and disclosure; maintain patient registration log for audit.
- **Medicare Card Scanning and HI Service Eligibility Verification:** Scan patient Medicare card (front and back) at each appointment or annually; immediately verify Medicare card is current (not expired) and patient details match record; use HI Service (Healthcare Identifiers Service) to verify patient eligibility for Medicare before claiming (free lookup via Best Practice or clinical software integration); HI Service returns: (1) current Medicare number, (2) eligible/not eligible status, (3) any restrictions (e.g., overseas visitor, temporary resident not eligible). If patient not eligible, clarify status and inform patient of private billing. Record verification in patient record (timestamp when card scanned and HI verified); if card expired or patient ineligible, do NOT bulk bill — collect private payment. For WorkCover or DVA patients, verify card is current and eligibility confirmed before billing.
- **Bulk Billing Consent Forms and Documentation:** Bulk billing consent can be given verbally or in writing; best practice is written consent: provide patient with form stating clinic bulk bills Medicare on patient's behalf, clinic does not bill patient the gap, and patient understands clinic receives Medicare rebate only (less than full fee); keep signed form in patient file or digital signature in software; some patients may prefer to be bulk billed for some items but not others (e.g., bulk bill standard consultation but pay private for cosmetic services) — document these exceptions; consent is standing unless patient revokes in writing; at reception, confirm bulk billing consent status before each appointment and document in patient record or software appointment notes.
- **Private Billing and EFTPOS Processing at Front Desk:** For private billings (non-bulk bill items), patient pays at reception either before or immediately after consultation: (1) provide itemised invoice showing item number, description, Medicare rebate (if applicable), private gap, and total patient amount; (2) at payment, ask patient preferred payment method (cash, card, cheque); (3) process via EFTPOS (Amex, Visa, Mastercard, debit card) — ensure EFTPOS terminal is secured and password-protected; (4) if payment via card, do NOT write card details on invoice (PCI-DSS compliance); (5) provide patient with receipt showing date, amount, items billed, and payment method; (6) if patient unable to pay, offer payment plan (e.g., pay 50% today, 50% next visit) and document agreement. At end of day, reconcile EFTPOS terminal total against cash/card reconciliation (see Category 2).
- **End-of-Day Till Balancing and Banking Reconciliation:** Daily at close of business (usually 5–5:30 pm), reception staff conduct till reconciliation: (1) count cash drawer (notes and coins); (2) note beginning cash float (should be consistent, e.g., AUD 100); (3) calculate: opening float + cash received today − closing float = expected daily takings; (4) compare to EFTPOS settlement total + cheques received; (5) if cash matches, document on till reconciliation form and store cash in safe; (6) if cash short or over by more than AUD 10, investigate discrepancy with staff member (data entry error, unrecorded cash out) and correct; (7) next morning, verify bank deposit recorded to clinic account corresponds to prior day's takings (deposit should include cash + EFTPOS settlement); maintain till reconciliation log for monthly audit.
- **Phone Management and After-Hours Message System:** Clinic phone lines staffed during business hours (e.g., 8 am–5:30 pm) with protocol: answer within 3 rings, greet with clinic name, offer to schedule appointment or transfer to GP/nurse if urgent enquiry; maintain updated voicemail greeting including clinic name, hours, after-hours contact (e.g., nurse hotline, emergency number), and call-back timeframe; set voicemail to record message (for audit if dispute arises); after hours (e.g., 5:30 pm–8 am, weekends), route all calls to after-hours answering service or dedicated after-hours phone line (list service number on clinic voicemail); check voicemail twice daily (morning and mid-day) and return patient calls within 24 hours (or next business day); maintain call log for triage calls (patient name, date/time, reason, action taken, staff member name) for at least 2 weeks; monitor for patterns (e.g., frequent calls from same patient, complaints about wait times) and escalate to GP/manager if needed.
- **Online Booking Platform Management and Third-Party Integrations:** Many patients now book via clinic website or third-party platforms (HotDoc, HealthEngine, Practo); ensure booking platform is integrated with clinical software (appointment details sync automatically); daily check that online booking is available and clinic availability matches appointment schedule (avoid double-booking); update clinic details on booking platform (address, phone, hours, GPs available) quarterly or immediately if changes; monitor booking platform messages and flag to reception staff (patient booking enquiry, cancellation, reschedule request); ensure confirmation email/SMS is sent to patient upon booking; set booking rules (e.g., close online booking if clinic at capacity, block out GP leave dates, prevent bookings >30 days in advance); monthly, review booking platform analytics (bookings by GP, time of day, cancellations) and identify trends or issues.
- **Patient Recall and Reminder Management:** Identify patients due for routine recall (e.g., annual review for chronic disease patients, immunisation follow-up, cervical screening, blood pressure check); use clinical software recall function or external recall system (recalls are often automated, triggered by patient age, condition, or time since last visit); send reminder letter, SMS or email 2 weeks before due date (include appointment booking link if online booking enabled); track response rate and follow up non-responders with phone call 1 week before due date; maintain recall log showing dates recalled, response rate, and any declined recalls; focus on highest-risk recalls first (immunisation status, uncontrolled chronic disease, overdue cancer screening).
- **Waiting Room Management and Patient Flow:** Reception staff manage waiting room: greet patients on arrival, offer seat, provide privacy statement and registration form if new patient, monitor wait time (target ≤15 mins in normal circumstances); if wait exceeds 20 mins, inform patient of delay and estimated wait time; for anxious patients, offer refreshment (water, coffee) or provide reading material; maintain waiting room cleanliness and temperature; ensure signage is current (clinic hours, payment methods accepted, health and safety policies); for vulnerable patients (elderly, vision-impaired, language barrier), offer assistance (reading documents, interpretation service, seating near door for easy access); monitor for safety issues (trip hazards, aggressive patients) and address immediately.
- **Fax and Correspondence Management:** Establish centralised fax management: clinic receives faxes from hospitals (discharge letters, referral responses), specialists (reports), and third parties (WorkCover authorisations); designate one staff member as fax custodian; when fax received, stamp with date/time, identify sender and intended recipient GP, and route to GP's inbox immediately; for outgoing faxes (e.g., referral to specialist), prepare cover sheet including clinic details, date, intended recipient, and confirmation of fax number; document outgoing fax with date, recipient and subject line in fax log; follow up if fax fails (error message indicates failed transmission — resend immediately); review faxes confidentiality (do NOT leave faxes in open areas — file in patient record or designated GP inbox); some faxes are urgent (e.g., WorkCover certificate for worker unable to work) — prioritise same-day delivery to GP.

### Who Usually Does It

**Primary:** Receptionist(s) and Front-Desk Manager (appointment scheduling, patient registration, bulk billing, EFTPOS, till balancing, phone management, patient recall, waiting room) | **Backup:** Business Manager or Clinic Manager (override issues with appointments/EFTPOS, online platform technical support, fax management escalation)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Patient no-show rate and appointment compliance (monthly)
- Till reconciliation discrepancies over AUD 10 (daily or weekly review)
- Waiting room management feedback (patient complaints or delays over 20 mins)
- Online booking platform analytics (monthly)
- Phone service complaints or access issues
- EFTPOS security compliance (PCI-DSS)

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Clinical software appointment book (Best Practice, Medical Director, HotDoc)
- Online booking platform (HotDoc, HealthEngine, Practo)
- Medicare card scanner or imaging device
- HI Service eligibility checker (integrated in software or web portal)
- EFTPOS terminal and merchant account
- Till reconciliation forms and cash drawer
- Patient registration form and bulk billing consent form
- Clinic phone system (landline, PABX, or VoIP)
- After-hours answering service contract
- Recall system (software-based or external vendor)
- Fax machine (or fax-to-email service)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Appointment no-shows and double-booking:** High DNA rate reduces clinic revenue and wastes GP time; double-booking causes long waits, patient complaints, and rushed consultations (patient safety risk); missed recall appointments mean patients miss preventive care (e.g., immunisation, cancer screening), leading to preventable disease or late diagnosis.
- **Bulk billing fraud or consent issues:** Bulk billing without valid patient consent exposes clinic to Medicare fraud investigation; Medicare can recover payments (typically 3-year lookback) plus interest (10% p.a.) and penalties; clinic may be excluded from Medicare if pattern of non-compliance; patients can lodge complaints with Medicare and request refunds.
- **EFTPOS security and PCI-DSS non-compliance:** Storing card details insecurely (e.g., writing on invoices, unencrypted digital files) breaches PCI-DSS standards; if clinic is compromised and patient card data stolen, clinic liable for fraud losses and notification costs; EFTPOS merchant can suspend account; potential civil claims from patients for identity theft.
- **Till discrepancies and cash losses:** Ongoing till discrepancies over AUD 10 (e.g., AUD 50 short per week) compound over time; could indicate staff theft, data entry errors, or cash mismanagement; annual loss could be AUD 2,000–5,000+ depending on frequency; loss impacts clinic profitability; if staff suspected of theft, requires investigation and potential termination.
- **Failed HI Service verification:** Bulk billing patients not eligible for Medicare (overseas visitors, temporary residents) triggers Medicare rejection of claim and recovery demand; clinic must refund patient; if pattern of non-verification, Medicare suspends bulk billing arrangement.
- **Phone access and safety issues:** After-hours calls not answered or not returned promptly can leave patients without urgent support (e.g., patient with chest pain, severe asthma unable to reach clinic); potential patient harm and liability; complaint to regulator if patient outcome is adverse.
- **Fax and correspondence mismanagement:** Missed fax (e.g., hospital discharge letter) means patient's transition to community care is not coordinated; GP unaware of patient hospitalisation and current medications; potential adverse drug interaction or readmission; loss of important information (pathology results, specialist reports) causes delayed diagnosis or treatment.

## CATEGORY 7 — MEDICARE BILLING AND CLAIMING

### What Has to Be Done

- **MBS Item Number Selection and Billing Accuracy:** GP must select correct MBS item for service provided; common items in general practice include: (1) Item 23 (100 points, AUD 37.85 rebate) — standard consultation up to 20 mins; (2) Item 36 (160 points, AUD 60.60) — consultation 20–40 mins; (3) Item 44 (220 points, AUD 83.20) — consultation >40 mins; (4) Item 3 (131 points, AUD 48.65) — brief consultation <10 mins (used sparingly); (5) Item 5 (164 points, AUD 61.85) — follow-up item. For chronic disease management: (6) Item 721 (150 points, AUD 50.80) — GP Management Plan (initial, up to 3 yearly); (7) Item 723 (180 points, AUD 68.15) — Diabetes Management Plan. For health assessments and risk stratification: (8) Items 701–707 (varying points) — 45-minute comprehensive health assessments for at-risk populations (Aboriginal/Torres Strait Islander, aged 75+, aged 45–49 Aboriginal, dementia screening, etc.). For mental health: (9) Items 2700–2715 (60–155 points) — GP-led mental health plan, psychological therapy items, psychiatric consultation items. For immunisation: (10) Item 10988 (50 points, AUD 20) — vaccination administration (typically bulk billed). Clinical software should provide item selector with descriptions; GP must justify item selected in clinical notes; audit Gp's item selection monthly to ensure appropriateness (e.g., check if Item 36 is used only for genuine 20–40 min consultations, not as default for all visits). Flag inappropriate item selection to GP for retraining.
- **Bulk Bill vs Private Billing Decision and Patient Communication:** For each patient, GP decides whether to bulk bill (clinic claims Medicare rebate, patient pays zero) or ask patient to pay privately (patient pays full fee, clinic or patient claims Medicare rebate separately). Factors: (1) patient eligibility (concession card holder eligible for free bulk bill consultations), (2) item type (mental health items, health assessments, CDM typically bulk billed), (3) clinic policy (some clinics bulk bill all items, others offer patient choice), (4) patient circumstances (pensioner, low income — may prefer bulk bill). If bulk billing: GP bills Medicare directly via clinical software; clinic receives rebate within 2–5 days. If private: patient pays full fee (e.g., AUD 75 for standard consultation), clinic issues invoice, patient may claim own Medicare rebate via MyGov (patient receives rebate after 2 weeks, keeps difference as gap). Reception staff should communicate billing method to patient at booking: "Your appointment is bulk billed — no cost to you" or "Your appointment is private billing — the fee is AUD 75, and you can claim your Medicare rebate separately." Document in appointment notes and confirm at consultation start.
- **Medicare Online Claiming via Best Practice/Medical Director (ECLIPSE):** Clinical software integrates with ECLIPSE (Medicare's online claiming system); at end of consultation, GP enters: (1) patient Medicare number and date of birth, (2) MBS item number(s) claimed, (3) date of service, (4) clinical notes justifying item (e.g., "Chronic asthma, uncontrolled, medication review and spirometry"), (5) whether bulk bill or private. Software submits claim electronically to Medicare; claim status shown as "submitted." Within 24 hours, claim returns status: (1) "paid" — Medicare has processed and clinic receives rebate (within 2 business days), (2) "claimed but not yet paid" — Medicare processing, (3) "rejected" — Medicare has identified issue (see rejection management below), (4) "pending" — awaiting patient verification if first bulk bill. Clinic staff should run daily claim report in software to verify all consultations from previous day are showing as "submitted" or "paid" (not missed). If any consultations show as "not submitted," immediately resubmit. Medicare recommends submitting within 48 hours of service to avoid delays.
- **Daily Claim Submissions and Verification:** Establish workflow: at end of each clinic session (e.g., after morning surgery 12:30 pm, end of day 5:30 pm), staff run "submit pending claims" function in clinical software; software batches all unsumbitted claims and sends to Medicare via ECLIPSE; verify submission successful (software confirms batch submission, receipt number provided); log submission date and batch number. Next morning, run "claim status report" and check all previous day's claims show "submitted" or "paid"; if any show "rejected" or "error," immediately address (see rejection management). Maintain daily claim submission log (date, number of claims, batch reference, status by evening); reconcile daily claims to cashflow (monitor when rebates actually deposit to bank account vs when claims submitted — typical lag 2–5 business days).
- **Medicare Rejection Management and Resubmission Workflow:** Medicare may reject claims for various reasons: (1) patient not eligible (overseas visitor, not Medicare-entitled), (2) item number mismatch (e.g., Item 23 claimed but clinical notes don't justify standard consultation duration), (3) duplicate claim (claim submitted twice for same patient/date), (4) missing clinical notes, (5) patient Medicare number incorrect or card expired, (6) condition code missing (e.g., mental health item without depression screening completed). When rejection received: (1) identify reason from Medicare rejection message (shown in claim status in software), (2) assign to GP to address (e.g., obtain patient consent, add clinical notes, confirm Medicare number), (3) resubmit within 7 days (Medicare allows resubmission), (4) if no resolution, escalate to practice manager and owner to decide: contact Medicare (13 20 11), contact patient, or write off claim. Maintain rejection log (date, patient name, item, reason, resolution) for audit; track rejection trends (e.g., if many mental health item rejections, may indicate missing screening codes — retrain GP).
- **Patient Billing Disputes and Gap Complaints:** Patients may dispute: (1) gaps (difference between Medicare rebate and clinic fee charged privately), (2) bulk bill status (patient claims they should have been bulk billed), (3) unexpected bills (patient thought consultation was bulk billed but received invoice). When patient disputes: (1) obtain full details (patient name, date, consultation with which GP), (2) check clinical record and claim status, (3) review communication to patient at booking (what was promised?), (4) if clinic error (e.g., promised bulk bill but didn't deliver), offer credit or refund, (5) if patient confusion (patient asked for private but later disputes), send patient written explanation of gap and rebate information, (6) escalate to owner if patient insists on refund. Document resolution in patient record; identify systemic issues (e.g., reception staff not communicating billing method) and retrain.
- **DVA Gold / White Card Processing and Eligibility Verification:** Patients with Department of Veterans' Affairs (DVA) Gold or White card are entitled to free GP services; clinic bulk bills Medicare AND DVA; at patient registration, scan DVA card and verify eligibility via DVA online service (deewr.gov.au); record card number and expiry date in patient record. When consulting, ensure GP bills using DVA-specific claiming process: (1) in software, select DVA as funding source (not standard Medicare), (2) software submits claim to both Medicare and DVA, (3) DVA typically processes within 5 business days. If DVA card expired, patient must renew with DVA before next appointment (notify patient). Monitor DVA rebates separately from Medicare (reconcile DVA deposits to clinic bank account); DVA rebates may be lower than Medicare if patient receives concurrent treatment (e.g., private psychology) — clarify with DVA if unsure.
- **WorkCover Billing and Claim Management:** Patients injured at work may have WorkCover claim (state-based workers' compensation scheme); for Victorian WorkCover claims, clinic must: (1) verify patient has WorkCover authority from WorkCover Victoria, (2) obtain patient's worker's compensation claim number, (3) on first consultation, contact WorkCover insurer to confirm coverage for GP services (some WorkCover insurers accept GP billing, others require patient to pay and claim back), (4) if insurer accepts GP claims, bill WorkCover (using WorkCover item numbers, typically same as Medicare items but claimed to insurer), (5) claim to WorkCover via PRODA/HPI-O (WorkCover insurer EDI or paper claim), (6) reconcile WorkCover deposits to bank account. Clinic must provide WorkCover with treatment plan if requested (insurer may want to ensure treatment aligns with workers' compensation guidelines). For legal matters (e.g., patient pursuing common law claim against employer), clinic can charge private fee and patient may recover from damages; ensure separate documentation and charge appropriately.
- **TAC Billing (Victoria) and Motor Accident Claims:** Patients injured in motor vehicle accident (Victoria) may be eligible for Transport Accident Commission (TAC) coverage; clinic must verify TAC eligibility (patient provides TAC claim reference number or hospital discharge letter mentioning TAC claim); if eligible, clinic bulk bills TAC (similarly to WorkCover); obtain TAC claim number and contact TAC (13 11 26) to confirm GP service coverage; claim to TAC via paper form or digital system (some GP software integrates with TAC). TAC claims typically cover up to 26 weeks of GP treatment after accident; monitor claim expiry and communicate to patient if services become private after TAC coverage ends.
- **Medicare Reconciliation (Deposits vs Claimed Amounts):** Monthly, reconcile Medicare deposits received to bank account vs claims submitted to Medicare: (1) run Medicare claim report in software (filter by date range, e.g., 1–30 June), (2) sum all bulk bill claims submitted, (3) extract total Medicare deposits from bank statement for same month, (4) compare: deposits should be 80–85% of claims submitted (5–10% is typically rejected or awaiting resubmission), (5) if deposits are significantly lower than expected (e.g., only 50% of claims paid), investigate: contact Medicare Online Services to confirm claim status, check for large rejections, verify bank account is receiving payments (not another clinic account by mistake). Maintain reconciliation log (month, claims submitted, rebates expected, deposits received, variance). Large variances indicate billing problem or missed claims — escalate to owner.
- **MBS Compliance Monitoring and Red Flag Alerts:** Establish monthly audit of billing against MBS guidelines to prevent fraud or over-claiming: (1) sample 10–20 claims per GP per month, (2) verify item selected matches clinical notes (e.g., Item 23 claimed, notes document <20 min consultation?), (3) check CDM items (721/723) are only claimed once per 12 months per patient (not double-claiming), (4) verify health assessments (701–707) meet eligibility criteria (patient age group, risk factor documented), (5) flag any mental health items (2700–2715) claimed by non-mental-health-trained GP (should be referred), (6) identify high-billing GPs vs peers (e.g., one GP claims 50 Item 36 (long consult) per week vs others 10 per week — is workload justified or over-claiming?). Document findings in compliance log; discuss with GPs; maintain evidence of monitoring for regulatory audit.

### Who Usually Does It

**Primary:** General Practitioner (item selection, billing decision, clinical notes justification) | **Backup:** Clinic Manager or Practice Nurse (claim submission, rejection management, rejection log maintenance, compliance audit)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Monthly Medicare reconciliation (deposits vs claims)
- Compliance audit results and any red flag items identified
- Rejection trends and resubmission success rate
- Patient billing disputes and resolution
- Any communications from Medicare regarding billing integrity or audit

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Clinical software with ECLIPSE integration (Best Practice, Medical Director, Genie, HotDoc)
- MBS Online (mbsonline.gov.au) for item descriptions and rebate rates
- Medicare Online Services portal (for claim status, bulk bill authority)
- PRODA and HPI-O (for WorkCover, TAC claiming)
- DVA online verification service
- WorkCover Victoria and TAC claim reference systems
- Bank statement and Medicare deposit reconciliation spreadsheet
- Compliance audit log and billing sample documentation

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Billing fraud and compliance breach:** Systematic over-claiming (e.g., Item 36 claimed for all patients regardless of consultation length) triggers Medicare audit; Medicare reviews 5-year history, recovers overpaid amounts (potentially AUD 50,000+), and applies penalties (up to 3× recovered amount); clinic may lose bulk bill authority and provider number (cannot operate); GPs and clinic director personally liable; referral to AHPRA and police.
- **Undetected rejected claims:** Claims rejected due to ineligibility, duplicate, or technical error, if not resubmitted, result in lost revenue (patient not billed, clinic not paid) and operational confusion (accounting system balance issues); accumulated rejected claims could total AUD 5,000–10,000+ monthly if unresolved.
- **Patient billing disputes:** Lack of clear communication on bulk bill vs private billing status leads to unexpected patient bills and complaints; patient may dispute charge and refuse payment; clinic may write off revenue or spend time in collection efforts; negative word-of-mouth damages clinic reputation and patient acquisition.
- **Compliance audit failure:** Large variance between claims submitted and rebates received without explanation triggers Medicare inquiry or regulatory audit; clinic required to remediate (potentially expensive), and if deliberate non-compliance found, penalties and loss of Medicare accreditation.
- **DVA/WorkCover processing delays:** Incorrect claim submission to DVA or WorkCover (wrong source code, missing patient details) delays payment; clinic must follow up and resubmit, tying up management time; patient may lodge complaint if not told why payment delayed.

## CATEGORY 8 — PRODA / HPOS / PROVIDER NUMBER / DIGITAL HEALTH ADMINISTRATION

### What Has to Be Done

- **PRODA Organisation Account Maintenance:** PRODA (Provider Digital Access) is the online portal used to manage provider organisation details and staff access for Australian healthcare providers. Clinic (organisation) establishes PRODA account with responsible officer (usually clinic owner/manager); PRODA allows: (1) staff access delegation (add/remove staff, assign roles — GP, nurse, receptionist), (2) organisation details updates (contact details, bank account, addresses), (3) digital certificate management, (4) HPI-O and provider number verification, (5) access to HI Service queries. Maintain PRODA organisation account: (1) assign one staff member as PRODA administrator (with strong password and 2-factor authentication), (2) review staff access delegations quarterly (remove staff who have left, add new staff), (3) update organisation bank account details if account changes, (4) maintain contact details (clinic phone, email, manager name), (5) keep digital certificate current (see below), (6) ensure all GPs registered with PRODA have their own GP provider numbers linked to clinic HPI-O.
- **Staff Access Delegations and On-Boarding / Off-Boarding:** Use PRODA to manage staff access to healthcare systems: when new staff hired, within 1 week of start date, add staff member to PRODA with appropriate role (GP, nurse, receptionist); assign permissions based on role (GPs can access full patient records and claim; nurses can access clinical records and immunisation register; receptionists can access appointment scheduling and patient demographic data only). In PRODA, specify staff member's work area (e.g., all clinical software access) and systems they can access (Medicare Online, My Health Record, AIR); staff member receives access credentials (username, initial password) and is required to set own password and establish 2-factor authentication. When staff leaves employment: (1) retrieve all clinic IT equipment (computer, USB keys, access cards), (2) immediately remove from PRODA (disable account within 1 day of departure), (3) notify all system vendors of staff departure (clinical software vendor, Medicare Online, HI Service, My Health Record team), (4) archive staff email account and transfer any patient-related emails to clinic email system, (5) reset passwords for shared accounts (clinic email, clinical software admin access) that departing staff may have known. Maintain staff access log (names, start date, access removal date, systems accessed, reason for termination if applicable) for audit.
- **HPI-O (Healthcare Provider Identifier – Organisation) Maintenance:** HPI-O is clinic's unique identifier in Australian healthcare system (16-digit code, e.g., HPI-O 8003623513579); used on Medicare claims, pathology orders, hospital referrals, and consumer-facing systems (My Health Record, HealthEngine). Verify HPI-O is current and linked to clinic name, address, and contact details: (1) search HPI-O on Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency (AHPRA) register (reg.ahpra.gov.au), (2) confirm details shown match clinic reality (address, ABN, phone, email), (3) if address changes (clinic relocation), notify AHPRA within 28 days (AHPRA updates HPI-O record); (4) if clinic name changes, notify AHPRA and request HPI-O update. Maintain HPI-O record for all external parties who need to claim, refer, or communicate with clinic (list HPI-O on clinic letterhead and referral forms); ensure clinical software is linked to correct HPI-O (not another clinic's identifier — common error in multi-location practices).
- **NASH Digital Certificate Maintenance and Renewal:** NASH (National Authentication Service for Health) digital certificate is a security credential used to sign digital transactions (claims, prescriptions, My Health Record uploads); clinic obtains certificate via approved vendor (e.g., Netrust, Actalis); certificate has expiry date (typically 2–3 years); before expiry, NASH sends renewal notice to nominated email. Process: (1) monitor email for NASH certificate renewal notice (arrives 3 months before expiry), (2) approve renewal request, (3) NASH issues new certificate (digital file), (4) IT manager or software vendor installs new certificate in clinic systems (clinical software, PRODA, Medicare Online), (5) test certificate is working (submit test claim or transaction to verify digital signature accepted), (6) archive old certificate for records (may be required for audit trail). If certificate expires without renewal, clinic cannot submit claims or access secure systems (blocks revenue and operations) — set calendar reminder 6 months before expiry as failsafe.
- **My Health Record (MHR) Participation and Compliance:** My Health Record (MHR) is national digital health record available to all Australian citizens; clinic can upload patient records to MHR (e.g., consultation notes, referrals, pathology results, medications) and access patient records uploaded by other healthcare providers. Clinic participation: (1) opt-in to MHR participation (register with NEHTA or digital health vendor), (2) ensure clinical software has MHR integration (upload capability), (3) establish upload protocol: which documents are uploaded (all GP notes? referrals only? medications?); typically, GPs upload key clinical documents after significant consultation, (4) obtain patient consent for MHR participation (patient must opt-in to MHR; clinic asks patient during registration: "Can we upload your medical records to My Health Record for you to access?"), (5) educate patients on MHR benefits (can access own records, provides healthcare providers with patient history for safer care, essential for emergency situations). Compliance: (1) audit MHR uploads monthly (verify documents uploaded are correct and timely), (2) monitor for any patient privacy complaints related to MHR (patient reports unauthorized access or incorrect data), (3) ensure staff understand MHR privacy and security (not to access patient MHR for non-clinical purposes), (4) maintain MHR audit log (who accessed, when, which documents uploaded).
- **Provider Number Applications for New GPs (Location-Specific):** Each GP working at clinic location requires a Medicare provider number linked to that specific location (HPI-I — Healthcare Provider Identifier, Individual); GPs are responsible for obtaining own provider number (not clinic's role), but clinic can facilitate: (1) provide GP with information on application process (Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Agency, AHPRA), (2) confirm clinic's HPI-O and location address (required on application), (3) once GP obtains provider number, notify clinic and add to PRODA and clinical software, (4) verify provider number is linked to clinic location (wrong location link = claims may not deposit), (5) maintain register of all GPs working at clinic with their provider numbers and start/end dates; for locums, obtain provider number before first shift and confirm validity (call AHPRA or check online register).
- **Provider Number Location Link Changes and Multi-Location Clinics:** If clinic has multiple locations (e.g., head office + satellite practice), each location has separate HPI-O; if GP works across multiple locations, GP's provider number must be linked to each location; if GP moves from one location to another: (1) notify Medicare Online and clinical software of new location link, (2) ensure claims from new location go to new HPI-O (avoid claims to old clinic location which affects old clinic's reconciliation), (3) update PRODA to reflect new location access for GP. For practices with multiple locations, maintain location register (HPI-O per location, GPs allocated to each location, provider number links per GP).
- **HI Service (Healthcare Identifiers Service) Queries and Patient Lookups:** HI Service is used to verify patient eligibility for Medicare and retrieve patient's healthcare identifier number (HIN, used in some systems). HI Service queries available to authorised clinic staff (registered with HI Service); process: (1) staff member logs into HI Service portal or uses clinical software integrated HI lookup, (2) enters patient Medicare number and date of birth, (3) HI Service returns: patient's current Medicare number, HIN (if allocated), eligibility status, any restrictions (overseas visitor, temporary resident, prisoner), (4) use results to determine billing approach (bulk bill if eligible, private if not). Clinic admin: (1) ensure HI Service access is set up for clinic (registration with Services Australia), (2) add authorized staff to HI Service access list (via PRODA), (3) remove staff from access list when they leave, (4) audit HI Service queries monthly (log shows all lookups; check for inappropriate queries, e.g., staff member looking up family member or non-patient — privacy breach).
- **HPOS Access for PBS and eReferrals:** HPOS (HealthConnect PBS and Online Services) allows GPs to access Pharmaceutical Benefits Scheme (PBS) information and electronic referrals (eReferrals) to specialists; clinic must register for HPOS and add GPs and staff members to system; GPs use HPOS to: (1) check PBS status (patient eligibility, co-payment amounts, PBS restrictions on certain drugs), (2) submit electronic referrals to specialists (faster than paper, specialists receive digitally), (3) check referral status (specialist has received and responded?). Clinic administration: (1) maintain HPOS user list (GPs and staff accessing system), (2) ensure HPOS is integrated into clinical software (seamless workflow), (3) remove staff access when they leave, (4) audit electronic referrals monthly (sent, received, responded, or pending — track delays).
- **PIP and WIP Reporting via HPOS (Quality-Based Payments):** PIP (Practice Incentives Program) and WIP (Work Incentives Program) are Australian government rebates to practices meeting quality and workforce participation criteria; reported via HPOS to Department of Health: (1) PIP: practice receives rebate if meets criteria like: accreditation (RACGP/AGPAL), Aboriginal health service (if applicable), Quality Improvement activities (documented), e-Health adoption (My Health Record participation, electronic prescribing). Rebate typically AUD 5,000–20,000+ per year depending on criteria met. (2) WIP: practice receives rebate if employs allied health professionals (physiotherapist, dietitian, etc.) meeting minimum hours; rebate approximately AUD 5,000–10,000 per year. Clinic administration: (1) confirm practice meets PIP/WIP eligibility criteria annually, (2) submit annual PIP/WIP report via HPOS (by 30 June end of financial year, or as directed by Department), (3) provide documentation evidence of criteria (accreditation certificate, training records, allied health staff contracts), (4) monitor for rebate receipt (typically deposited by Department in second half of year — track for revenue forecasting).

### Who Usually Does It

**Primary:** Business Manager or IT Manager (PRODA account maintenance, staff access delegation, provider number tracking, HI Service administration, HPOS management) | **Backup:** Clinic Owner or Finance Manager (provider number applications, digital certificate renewal, PIP/WIP reporting)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Quarterly review of staff access in PRODA (ensure departed staff are removed)
- Digital certificate expiry date (set renewal reminder 6 months in advance)
- Provider number register and links (confirm all GPs' numbers linked to correct location/HPI-O)
- Annual PIP/WIP eligibility and reporting
- HI Service audit log for any suspicious queries
- My Health Record participation and upload audit

### Documents / Systems Involved

- PRODA portal (proda.humanservices.gov.au)
- AHPRA register (reg.ahpra.gov.au) for HPI-O lookup
- HI Service portal or clinical software integration
- HPOS portal for PBS and eReferrals
- NASH digital certificate vendor (e.g., Netrust)
- Clinical software provider number linking configuration
- My Health Record (myhealth.gov.au) for upload status
- Department of Health PIP/WIP portal and reporting forms
- Staff access and provider number register (spreadsheet or system)

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Expired digital certificate:** If NASH certificate expires without renewal, clinic cannot submit claims or access Medicare Online; operations blocked until certificate renewed (could take 1+ weeks); clinic loses all revenue during outage (AUD 10,000+ per week for medium clinic) and Medicare may levy late-submission penalties.
- **Incorrect HPI-O or provider number linking:** If GP's provider number linked to wrong clinic location or old HPI-O, Medicare deposits go to wrong clinic account; clinic unable to reconcile; GP claims attributed to wrong clinic in Medicare records (affects clinic's compliance profile and funding eligibility). Takes weeks to correct; lost revenue during correction.
- **Staff access not removed on departure:** If departing staff member's access left active in PRODA and clinical systems, former staff can access patient records (privacy breach); risk of data theft or sabotage; Privacy Act breach and potential patient complaints; regulatory investigation (OAIC, AHPRA); financial penalty (up to AUD 2.5M for serious breach) and reputational damage.
- **My Health Record non-participation or poor uploads:** If clinic not participating in MHR, patients cannot access own records; specialist or hospital provider accessing patient MHR unable to see GP notes (fragmented care, patient safety risk). If uploads non-compliant or delayed, benefit of MHR not realised.
- **PIP/WIP reporting missed:** If clinic meets eligibility criteria but fails to report, clinic loses rebate (AUD 5,000–20,000 annually); Department may audit and find clinic was eligible but non-compliant (clinic may still receive rebate if reported late, but incurs admin costs and delay).
- **HI Service queries audited and found unauthorized:** Unauthorized queries (staff looking up family or non-patient) = privacy breach under Privacy Act; potential complaint to OAIC; if pattern of unauthorized access, clinic may be restricted or excluded from HI Service access.

## CATEGORY 9 — CLINICAL SOFTWARE / IT / PHONES / INTERNET / BACKUPS / CYBER

### What Has to Be Done

- **Clinical Software Updates and Patch Management:** Clinic's clinical software (Best Practice, Medical Director, Genie, HotDoc) requires regular updates for: (1) security patches (fix vulnerabilities that could allow hacker access to patient data), (2) regulatory compliance (changes to MBS items, Medicare claiming rules, PBS requirements), (3) bug fixes (software errors affecting functionality), (4) feature enhancements (new tools, improved user interface). Establish update schedule: (1) monthly: check for available security patches from software vendor (via vendor portal or auto-notification), (2) review patch notes to understand what is being updated, (3) schedule update during low-clinic hours (e.g., Friday evening 5:30–6:30 pm) to minimise disruption, (4) before updating production system, test update on backup/test environment if available (verify no patient data loss, no features broken), (5) document update date, version number, and any issues encountered, (6) notify clinical staff of update and any workflow changes (e.g., new screen layout), (7) after update, confirm core functions still working (test claim submission, access to records, appointment schedule).
- **Server Maintenance (On-Premise) or Cloud Platform Monitoring:** If clinic uses on-premise clinical software server (stored in clinic): (1) server requires monthly maintenance: restart server to clear memory and logs, check hard drive space (if >80% full, risk of system failure — archive old records or add storage), verify antivirus engine is running and signatures updated daily, check network connectivity (ensure server is connected to stable internet, monitor packet loss), inspect physical server (ensure cooling fans working, no dust buildup, cables secure), (2) maintain server maintenance log (date, actions taken, any issues found and resolved). If clinic uses cloud-based software (hosted by vendor): (1) vendor responsible for server maintenance, but clinic should monitor platform status: vendor posts system status dashboard showing uptime, scheduled maintenance windows, (2) clinic receives notification of scheduled maintenance (usually low-risk and occurs outside business hours), (3) if clinic experiences access issues, check vendor status page first (is it a vendor outage or clinic network issue?), (4) contact vendor support if issue persistent (vendor logs and diagnoses), (5) cloud systems typically have better uptime (99%+ availability guaranteed) than on-premise, but clinic has no control if vendor experiences outage.
- **Daily Automated Backup Verification:** Data backup is critical — if clinic loses patient records due to hardware failure or ransomware, clinic cannot operate and patient safety at risk. Establish automated backup: (1) clinical software should have built-in backup function configured to run nightly (e.g., 11 pm) when clinic closed and no users accessing system, (2) backup should include all patient records, clinical notes, appointment history, billing data, (3) backup saved to external location (not same physical location as server — protects against fire, theft, or facility damage), (4) backup software sends confirmation email when backup completes successfully, (5) daily review of backup confirmation emails (one person assigned to check daily — if no confirmation email arrives, investigate backup failure immediately — contact IT support or software vendor), (6) backup should retain multiple versions: at least 7 daily backups (to restore from 1-week ago if needed), 4 weekly backups (1-month history), 12 monthly backups (1-year history), (7) test backup restoration monthly (see below).
- **Offsite/Cloud Backup Testing and Monthly Restore Test:** Backup is only useful if it can be restored successfully — untested backup may be corrupted or incomplete, leaving clinic unable to recover data in emergency. Establish monthly restore test: (1) select one day per month (e.g., last Friday), (2) IT manager restores backup to test/temporary system (not production — don't want to risk overwriting live data), (3) verify restored data is complete and accurate: run sample queries (can I find patient John Smith's records? Can I access appointment schedule?), check data integrity (no missing records, no duplicates), verify backup timestamp is correct (e.g., backup is from last night as expected, not weeks old), (4) document test results in restoration log (date, backup file restored, test results, any issues encountered), (5) if restoration fails or data incomplete, investigate immediately: contact backup vendor or software support, likely need new backup strategy or new backup software, (6) notify owner if restoration test fails (indicates clinic at risk if real disaster occurs).
- **Antivirus and Endpoint Security Updates:** Antivirus software and endpoint protection (blocks malware, ransomware, phishing) must be current: (1) antivirus engine auto-updates daily with latest virus signatures (definitions of known threats), (2) antivirus software itself updates periodically (new features, improved detection) — allow automatic updates or review updates monthly and apply, (3) all devices on clinic network protected: all staff computers, tablets, mobile devices, reception EFTPOS terminals, clinical server — if one device lacks protection, it's entry point for malware to spread, (4) antivirus quarantine should be reviewed weekly: if any files quarantined (detected as threats), investigate whether they're legitimate files caught by false positive, or actual malware; if actual malware, trace origin (came from email attachment? USB stick? website?) and alert staff to avoid, (5) maintain antivirus license/subscription (software often expires annually — confirm renewal dates and budget for cost, typically AUD 500–2000 per year depending on number of devices).
- **Phone System Management (PABX or VoIP) and After-Hours Routing:** Clinic phone system routes incoming calls to extensions, voicemail, and after-hours service. Two types: (1) PABX (Private Automatic Branch Exchange) — physical switchboard hardware installed at clinic, requires on-site management and maintenance, (2) VoIP (Voice over Internet Protocol) — cloud-based, phone calls over internet connection, more flexible and less hardware maintenance. Phone system administration: (1) maintain list of staff extensions (receptionist 1, receptionist 2, GP1, GP2, nurse, practice manager), (2) after-hours routing: set up call forwarding after 5:30 pm to after-hours answering service or nurse hotline (after-hours calls should not go to clinic voicemail unanswered), (3) voicemail greeting: record greeting with clinic name, hours, and after-hours contact, update greeting if hours change (seasonal hours, public holidays), (4) monitor phone lines for outages: if phones don't work, can't receive appointments or emergency calls — test dial through phone at least weekly to ensure working, (5) phone bills: review monthly bill for line rental, call costs, and long-distance charges; query any unusual usage or costs, (6) call recording: some clinics record calls for training or dispute resolution (e.g., patient calls and books appointment but later claims no booking) — ensure phone system supports call recording if needed; if recording, inform staff and patients of recording policy (legal requirement in Australia).
- **Internet Redundancy and Failover Connectivity:** Clinic absolutely depends on internet for clinical software (cloud-based or remote access), email, online banking, Medicare claiming, etc. Single internet connection is a risk: if provider outage, clinic offline and unable to operate. Establish redundancy: (1) primary internet connection: NBN (National Broadband Network) or ADSL via main provider, typical cost AUD 100–200/month, (2) secondary failover connection: mobile broadband hotspot (e.g., 4G/5G tablet or dedicated modem with SIM card) as backup if primary fails, enable automatic failover if possible (mobile hotspot activates if primary connection drops), or staff manually switch to mobile if needed (slower but better than no connection), (3) test failover connectivity monthly: physically disconnect primary connection and verify clinic can still access clinical software via failover, (4) ensure mobile hotspot plan has sufficient data (clinical software and email could use 100+ MB per day if busy clinic) — budget AUD 50–100/month for backup connection, (5) configure devices to warn if internet connection lost (some modern routers have notification feature if connection drops), (6) communicate to staff when failover activated (may experience slower internet, video calls not possible, large file uploads may timeout) so they adjust expectations.
- **Password Management and Access Control:** Clinical software and healthcare systems contain sensitive patient data — access must be restricted to authorised staff via strong passwords and access controls. Establish password policy: (1) all staff who access clinical software, email, Medicare Online, HI Service, etc. must have unique username and strong password, (2) strong password: minimum 12 characters, mix of uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols (e.g., ClinicPass#2024), (3) passwords must not be shared (if staff member forgets password, reset via system administrator, not password written on sticky note), (4) passwords must not be used across multiple systems (if one system hacked, attacker doesn't gain access to all systems), (5) password expiry: every 90 days, staff must change password (prompts staff to create new password), (6) multi-factor authentication (2FA): staff must authenticate with second factor (e.g., code from authenticator app, SMS code) to log in after password entry — adds layer of security, (7) shared system accounts (e.g., "Clinic Admin" account used by multiple staff): keep shared accounts to minimum, track who knows shared password, change shared password when staff leaves, (8) remote access: if staff access systems from home or off-site, use VPN (Virtual Private Network) to encrypt connection and prevent interception of data, (9) access removal: when staff leaves, immediately disable all access (clinical software, email, PRODA, Medicare Online) — don't leave ex-staff accounts active, (10) periodic access audit: quarterly, list all staff who have access to each system, verify they should still have access, remove any redundant access (privacy principle: least privilege — staff access only what they need for role).
- **Cyber Security Incident Response Plan:** Cyber incident could include: (1) ransomware attack (hacker encrypts patient data and demands payment for decryption key), (2) data breach (hacker steals patient data and sells it or publishes it), (3) phishing email (staff clicks malicious link or opens attachment containing malware), (4) system outage (malware or hardware failure takes systems offline), (5) website defacement (clinic website hacked and altered). Prepare response plan: (1) designate cyber incident response team (owner/manager, IT person, legal advisor), (2) plan covers immediate actions: isolate affected system (disconnect from network to prevent spread), preserve evidence (don't delete logs or files that might help investigate), notify staff and patients if personal data disclosed, contact police if criminal activity suspected, notify cyber insurance if applicable, (3) communication protocol: who notifies whom, what information is shared, legal/PR messaging if breach disclosed publicly, (4) recovery: restore from backup if ransomware, change all passwords, reinstall software if malware, verify systems are clean before resuming normal operations, (5) post-incident review: what happened, how did attacker get in, what could be prevented in future, (6) cyber insurance: consider insurance policy covering cyber attacks (typically AUD 1,000–3,000/year for clinic, covers costs of incident response, legal advice, notification, recovery).
- **Hardware Maintenance Schedule and Equipment Lifecycle:** Clinical computers, servers, printers, and EFTPOS terminals have lifespan. Establish maintenance schedule: (1) computer hardware: typical lifecycle 5–7 years; after 5 years, performance degrades and repair costs increase — plan replacement within 5-year cycle, (2) annual maintenance: clean computers (remove dust from fans and vents, prevents overheating), check hard drive health (software tests hard drive for bad sectors — if emerging issues, replace before failure), test monitor, keyboard, mouse functionality, (3) EFTPOS terminals: vendors typically replace/service annually; monitor for errors or slow processing, (4) printers: maintain toner/ink supply, perform quarterly maintenance (clean print head), (5) document all maintenance in hardware maintenance log (what was serviced, when, by whom, any issues found). Plan capital budget for equipment replacement: if clinic has 10 computers at AUD 1,500 each, replace 2 per year = AUD 3,000/year budget for hardware refresh.
- **Remote Access / VPN Security and Home Office Setup:** If clinic allows staff to work from home or access clinical systems remotely (e.g., GP reviewing records on weekend): (1) staff must connect via VPN (Virtual Private Network) — encrypted tunnel that secures data in transit over internet, (2) VPN credentials: staff must have unique VPN username/password (not shared), plus second factor (2FA) for login, (3) VPN setup: clinic IT manager provides VPN instructions and credentials to authorized staff, (4) home office security: staff must use secure home internet (not public WiFi), lock mobile device and computer when not in use, store clinical data only on encrypted devices (not on unprotected USB stick or cloud storage), (5) audit remote access: review VPN logs monthly to see who accessed when, flag unusual access (e.g., staff member accessing at 3 am when they usually work 9–5), (6) revoke remote access if staff no longer needs it (e.g., staff switches to non-clinical role, no longer needs clinical software access), (7) educate staff on security risks (phishing emails targeting home workers, family members accessing home computer and seeing patient data) — reinforce that patient data must stay confidential even at home.
- **User Account Management for New and Terminated Staff:** When new staff hired: (1) within 1 week of start, create user accounts in all systems staff needs: clinical software, email, PRODA, Medicare Online, HI Service, HPOS, phone extension, network login, etc., (2) set up with strong temporary password (staff member must change on first login), (3) provide IT induction: explain password policy, multi-factor authentication, VPN use, cybersecurity risks (phishing, ransomware), (4) document new account creation (date, systems, access level), (5) require staff to sign IT security acknowledgment (staff confirms they understand security policy). When staff terminates: (1) on last day, or immediately on notification of termination, disable all access: clinical software, email, PRODA, Medicare Online, HI Service, phone extension, network login, VPN, (2) retrieve all clinic IT equipment: laptop, phone, access cards, USB keys, (3) transfer any staff-created files/emails to clinic (clinical files must be retained as clinic property, not deleted), (4) reset any shared system passwords that staff member knew, (5) document termination in access control log, (6) do NOT re-assign email address immediately — retain for 3 months in case of messages needing to be retrieved, then delete.

### Who Usually Does It

**Primary:** IT Manager or Nominated IT Support Person (software updates, server maintenance, backups, antivirus, EFTPOS security, remote access, user account management, hardware maintenance, cyber incident response) | **Backup:** Business Manager or Clinic Manager (monitoring backup email notifications, testing phone system, internet failover, cyber incident escalation)

### What Owner Must Review Personally

- Monthly backup verification emails (ensure backups completing successfully)
- Quarterly or annual cyber security audit results
- Internet outage incidents and failover activation
- Any security breaches or suspicious activity
- Hardware and software maintenance budget and lifecycle plan
- Cyber insurance policy renewal and coverage adequacy

### Documents / Systems Involved

- Clinical software (Best Practice, Medical Director, Genie, HotDoc) — update and support portal
- Server or cloud platform management console
- Backup software and backup status monitoring
- Antivirus software and quarantine logs
- Phone system management interface (PABX or VoIP provider portal)
- Internet provider account (NBN, ADSL, mobile hotspot)
- VPN provider or VPN software
- Password manager (optional but recommended: Bitwarden, 1Password, LastPass)
- Cyber security incident response plan (written document)
- User account and access control register (spreadsheet or system)
- Hardware maintenance log and equipment replacement schedule

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Data loss from backup failure:** Unverified backup means clinic cannot recover from ransomware attack or hardware failure; clinic loses all patient records (catastrophic); operations completely halted; regulatory investigation by AHPRA/OAIC; patients cannot access care from other providers without records (patient harm); potential civil lawsuits from patients (AUD 50,000+); clinic may be unable to recover financially and forced to close.
- **Ransomware attack:** If antivirus not updated or staff falls for phishing email, malware encrypts patient data; attacker demands AUD 10,000–100,000+ for decryption key; if clinic can't pay and backup not tested, data is lost (same consequence as above); criminal investigation and potential compliance breach notice from regulator.
- **Internet outage without failover:** Primary internet connection fails (NBN outage, modem failure), clinic cannot access cloud clinical software, email, or Medicare Online; cannot book appointments, access patient records, or claim; each hour offline = lost revenue (AUD 200+ per hour for medium clinic) and patient care disruption; no secondary connection means outage lasts hours until primary connection restored.
- **Unauthorized access from weak password management:** Staff password shared or written down; non-staff member (hacker, disgruntled ex-employee) accesses clinical software using stolen password; patient data accessed or altered; privacy breach and potential OAIC complaint; if data disclosed, clinic liable to notify patients and provide credit monitoring; potential financial liability (AUD 5,000–50,000+) depending on scale of breach.
- **Expired digital certificate or software license:** NASH certificate or antivirus license expires without renewal; clinic cannot submit claims or systems unprotected; sudden loss of functionality disrupts operations (same impact as cyber attack).
- **Hardware failure without maintenance plan:** Computer or server fails without warning; if backup failed, data lost (see above); if backup working, restore takes hours to days — clinic offline during recovery; if EFTPOS terminal fails, cannot process payments (forced cash-only, patient inconvenience); no spare equipment on hand means further delay while ordering and installing replacement.

## CATEGORY 10 — COMPLIANCE / ACCREDITATION / POLICIES / AUDITS

### What Has to Be Done

- **RACGP Standards 5th Edition Compliance Maintenance:** The Royal Australian College of General Practitioners (RACGP) publishes Standards for General Practice (5th Edition, available at racgp.org.au); these are the professional standards against which Australian general practices are measured for quality and safety. Standards cover: (1) Safety and Quality Culture, (2) Continuity of Care, (3) Access and Responsiveness, (4) Professional and Ethical Practice, (5) Clinical Governance. Clinic must ensure compliance: (1) nominate one staff member as RACGP Standards coordinator (usually practice manager or quality lead), (2) obtain copy of Standards 5th Edition document (free from RACGP website), (3) review each standard and assess clinic's current compliance (green = compliant, amber = partially compliant, red = non-compliant), (4) for non-compliant areas, develop action plan (what change needed, who responsible, by when), (5) implement actions (e.g., if patient feedback process missing, establish patient survey quarterly), (6) document evidence of compliance: policies, meeting minutes, training records, audit results, patient feedback summaries, (7) maintain RACGP Standards Compliance File (physical or digital folder with all evidence), (8) annual review: at least once per year, reassess all standards and verify compliance maintained (some standards naturally drift, e.g., hand hygiene rates if audits stop), (9) before accreditation survey (see below), ensure all evidence is organised and immediately accessible to surveyors.
- **AGPAL or QPA Accreditation Cycle Management (3-Year Cycle):** AGPAL (Australian General Practice Accreditation Limited) or QPA (Quality Practice Accreditation, depending on state) conducts accreditation surveys of general practices every 3 years; accreditation confirms practice meets national standards and patient safety requirements. Accreditation cycle: (1) Year 1 of 3-year cycle: clinic receives accreditation certificate for 3 years, (2) plan for next survey: in year 2 (about 1 year before survey due), contact accreditation body to schedule survey date, (3) Year 3 (6–12 months before survey): conduct self-assessment using Standards 5th Edition, identify gaps, develop action plans to close gaps before survey, (4) 3–6 months before survey: complete mock internal audit (use accreditation organisation's audit tools to assess compliance), (5) prepare evidence file: accreditation surveys are evidence-based, so gather all documentation that demonstrates compliance with standards (policies, training records, incident reports, minutes, patient feedback, clinical audit results, equipment checks, staff files), organise evidence in accreditation folder or online system, (6) survey process: accreditation team visits clinic for 2–3 days, interviews staff and GPs, reviews clinical records (10–20 random patient files audited for clinical quality and documentation), inspects facilities (waiting room, clinical rooms, equipment, infection control), and reviews evidence file, (7) post-survey: accreditation team provides report with findings; if fully compliant, clinic receives 3-year accreditation certificate; if non-compliances identified, clinic must remediate (submit evidence of corrective action within 3 months, or accreditation may be delayed or conditional), (8) maintain accreditation certificate and display publicly (patient expectation is that accredited practice is safe and of high quality). Cost: accreditation typically costs AUD 2,500–5,000 for initial survey plus fees for maintenance/renewal.
- **Policy Review Schedule (Annually Minimum):** Clinic policies document how work is done and ensure consistency and compliance. Essential policies include: (1) Privacy and Confidentiality Policy (patient data protection, access controls), (2) Patient Safety and Quality Policy, (3) Infection Control Policy (hand hygiene, sharps safety, equipment sterilisation), (4) Immunisation Policy (vaccines, cold chain, adverse event reporting), (5) Medication Management Policy (safe prescribing, dispensing, storage), (6) Complaints and Feedback Policy (how patients lodge complaints, response timeframe, escalation), (7) Human Resources Policy (recruitment, performance management, termination, discrimination prevention), (8) IT Security and Cybersecurity Policy (passwords, remote access, data protection), (9) Health and Safety Policy (WHS compliance, injury reporting, hazard management), (10) Occupational Health and Safety and Infectious Diseases Policy (managing staff with infectious disease, post-exposure protocols). Establish policy review schedule: (1) assign one person to coordinate policy review (practice manager or compliance lead), (2) annually (or every 2 years for low-risk policies), schedule review of each policy; review against current legislation, industry best practice, and accreditation standards, (3) consult with relevant staff (e.g., nurses review Infection Control Policy, reception review Patient Access Policy) to ensure policy is practical and staff understand it, (4) update policy if changes needed (version number and date updated, list of changes at top of document), (5) communicate policy changes to all staff (email or in-service training), (6) require staff to sign acknowledgment that they've read and understand updated policy (maintain signed copies in staff files), (7) store policy document in clinic shared drive or policy management system, (8) maintain policy register (list of all clinic policies, version, date last reviewed, date next review due).
- **Internal Audit Program and Clinical Audit Schedule:** Audit is systematic review of practice against standards; audits identify gaps and opportunities for improvement. Establish internal audit program: (1) clinical audits: sample clinical records quarterly and review for quality of documentation, appropriateness of diagnosis/treatment, compliance with guidelines (e.g., is diabetes management plan reviewed annually?); involve a GP in review (not just administrative audit), (2) complaint audits: monthly review of all patient complaints received, analyse trends (e.g., are most complaints about wait times or clinical care?), identify systemic issues, implement improvements, (3) infection control audits: monthly hand hygiene observation (watch staff wash hands, score compliance against protocol), quarterly sharps safety audit (check sharps bins are not overflowing, needles recapped?), (4) financial audits: annual review of accounts (bank reconciliation, accounts payable, patient billing), verify no fraud or errors, (5) IT security audit: annual review of password compliance, user access appropriateness, backup testing results, (6) vaccination audit: monthly verify all vaccinations reported to AIR within 24 hours, (7) document all audits: maintain audit report with date, area audited, findings, recommendations, and follow-up actions. Maintain audit log (list of all audits conducted, dates, findings summary).
- **Clinical Governance Meeting and Quarterly Review:** Clinical governance is the framework for ensuring safe, high-quality patient care and continuous improvement. Establish quarterly clinical governance meeting: (1) attendees: GPs, nurses, practice manager, owner, (2) agenda items: (a) review of patient incidents/adverse events reported (e.g., wrong medication, patient fall, medication error, clinically significant delay in diagnosis), analyse root causes, implement corrective actions to prevent recurrence, (b) review of patient complaints and feedback (summary of month's complaints, trends, responses given to patients, changes to improve experience), (c) audit results (clinical audit findings, infection control audit results, financial audit, IT audit), discussion of follow-up actions, (d) compliance matters (accreditation preparation, RACGP Standards compliance status, any regulatory correspondence), (e) staff training needs (CPR renewal, new staff orientation, mandatory training), (f) quality improvement projects (current initiatives, progress, results), (3) keep meeting minutes with attendees, decisions made, action items assigned (who is responsible for each action, by when), (4) distribute minutes to all staff (transparency and awareness), (5) follow-up on prior action items (confirm completed, or if overdue, discuss obstacles and revised timeline). Meeting typically 1 hour, monthly or quarterly depending on clinic size and activity level.
- **Infection Control Monitoring and Hand Hygiene Audits:** Infection control is critical in healthcare; poor infection control can lead to healthcare-associated infections (HAI) and spread of contagious diseases. Establish infection control program: (1) hand hygiene is most important intervention: educate staff on Five Moments for Hand Hygiene (before aseptic procedure, before patient contact, after body fluid exposure, after patient contact, after touching patient surroundings), (2) audit hand hygiene monthly: staff member observes clinic staff during morning shift and scores: did staff wash hands correctly before examining patient? Did staff sanitize hands after touching patient? Record compliance percentage (e.g., 85% of observed interactions involved correct hand hygiene), (3) provide feedback to staff (in-service training if compliance <80%), (4) environmental checks: weekly, inspect clinical rooms for cleanliness, disinfectant dispensers full, soap available, (5) sharps safety audit: monthly check sharps bins are not overfilled (overfilled bins increase needle-stick risk), needles not recapped (except safety devices), disposal containers secured, (6) equipment sterilisation (if clinic performs minor surgery or uses re-usable equipment): verify autoclave is functioning (test with biological indicators monthly), sterilisation records maintained (date, equipment sterilised, autoclave temperature/time, staff member), (7) waste management: clinical waste (sharps, used dressings, contaminated items) segregated and disposed correctly (yellow bin for clinical waste, red bin for sharps), (8) vaccination status of staff: all healthcare staff should be vaccinated against: COVID-19 (minimum 1 dose, ideally booster), influenza (annually), measles/mumps/rubella (immune if vaccination records show 2 doses or serology positive), (9) post-exposure protocols: if staff member has sharps injury (needle stick) or exposure to blood/body fluid, immediate actions: wash wound with soap and water, report to manager, baseline blood tests for blood-borne viruses (HIV, Hepatitis B/C), consider post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) if high-risk exposure, (10) maintain infection control records (audit results, staff vaccination status, incidents and management).
- **Mandatory Staff Training Records and Compliance Tracking:** Staff must maintain current training in critical areas: (1) Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR): all healthcare staff must have current CPR certification (Red Cross or Australian Heart Foundation), updated every 1–2 years, (2) First Aid: staff in patient contact should have First Aid certification, updated every 3 years, (3) Privacy Act and Confidentiality: induction training for all staff on privacy law, patient consent, data security, updated annually, (4) Infection Control and WHS: induction training on hand hygiene, sharps safety, hazard identification, incident reporting, updated annually, (5) mandatory reporting: staff trained on duty to report child abuse or neglect to Department of Families, Fairness and Housing (DFFF, formerly DFFH), (6) elder abuse reporting: staff trained on identifying and reporting elder abuse (Victoria: Aged Care Quality Standards require reporting), (7) immunisation competency (for nurses administering vaccines): training and annual competency assessment, (8) use of medical devices (e.g., defibrillator, blood pressure monitor): staff must demonstrate competency before using, (9) medication safety (for staff handling medications): training on safe storage, labelling, administration, error reporting, (10) Diversity and Discrimination: staff training on respectful workplace, prevention of discrimination and harassment. Establish training register: (1) list each staff member and their training status (what training completed, date completed, expiry date if applicable), (2) identify upcoming expiries (e.g., CPR expires March 2024, need to re-enrol by January 2024), (3) schedule training well in advance (book CPR courses 2 months ahead), (4) maintain certificates in staff files, (5) send reminder emails to staff 1 month before expiry, (6) if staff fails to renew critical training (e.g., CPR), remove from clinical duties until renewed (safety issue).
- **Cold Chain Compliance Logs and Vaccine Storage Verification:** (See Category 5 Nursing Operations for detail; this category emphasises compliance and audit.) Cold chain integrity is mandatory for vaccine efficacy: (1) maintain daily cold chain log: record refrigerator temperature twice daily (morning and late afternoon) on printed or digital log, temperature must be 2–8°C, (2) document any temperature excursions: if temperature drops below 2°C or exceeds 8°C, immediately investigate (thermostat malfunction? door left open?), remove affected vaccines, assess viability, report to Vicinal, do not use vaccines without viability assessment, (3) equipment maintenance: monthly inspection of vaccine refrigerator (ensure functioning, no frost/ice buildup if auto-defrost model), annual professional service contract, (4) monthly audit of cold chain compliance: check cold chain logs for completeness and any excursions, verify temperature logger is functioning, review any incidents, (5) staff training: nurses trained on cold chain importance, correct vaccine storage, freezer vs refrigerator (vaccines damaged by freezing), (6) backup arrangements: identify backup refrigerator or cold box if main fridge fails, plan for power outage (have backup generator or portable cooler ready).
- **Medication Management Compliance and Storage Audits:** Clinic may stock medications (e.g., vaccines, adrenaline, samples): (1) storage: medications stored in cool, dry place away from sunlight (can degrade medication), locked cabinet if controlled drugs, (2) inventory and expiry: maintain medication inventory (what stocked, quantity, expiry date), monthly check for expired items (remove and dispose safely), order replacements before stock runs out, (3) documentation: if controlled drugs (adrenaline, benzodiazepines), maintain register of usage (who used, date, amount, patient, clinical reason), (4) cold chain: vaccines stored per cold chain (see above), (5) administration: only trained staff administer injections or medications, (6) error reporting: if medication error occurs (e.g., wrong dose given, wrong patient), immediately report to GP, document incident, notify patient if harm possible, file incident report, (7) audit: quarterly medication audit to verify inventory matches documentation, no expired items, storage conditions appropriate.
- **Complaints and Incidents Trend Analysis and Quality Improvement:** Patient feedback is valuable for identifying service gaps and improvement opportunities: (1) complaints process: establish clear complaints procedure: patient can lodge complaint via written form, email, phone, or in person; complaints directed to practice manager or owner; response provided within 10 business days (acknowledgement of complaint, description of investigation, outcome, and remedial action if applicable), (2) complaint log: maintain log of all complaints received (date, patient name, complaint summary, staff member involved if applicable, outcome, follow-up action), (3) monthly trend analysis: identify patterns (e.g., multiple complaints about long wait times, or concerns about particular staff member), (4) root cause analysis: for serious complaints, investigate underlying cause (e.g., long waits due to GPs running late, due to inadequate appointment buffer time — fix: increase appointment duration or reduce overbooking), (5) incidents: also track clinical incidents (medication error, wrong test ordered, delayed diagnosis), near-misses (close call that didn't result in patient harm), and accidents (staff injury, patient fall), (6) incident reporting culture: encourage staff to report incidents without blame or punishment (reporting allows learning and prevention of future incidents), (7) quality improvement projects: use complaints and incident data to identify improvement priorities (e.g., if multiple complaints about appointment access, project could be "improve same-day appointment availability"), (8) communicate improvements to patients: when changes made in response to feedback, inform patients that their feedback led to improvement (builds trust and patient satisfaction).
- **Quality Improvement Activities Documentation and Evidence Collection:** Quality improvement is ongoing process of identifying gaps in care and implementing changes that result in better patient outcomes or experience: (1) select improvement priorities based on data (complaints, incidents, audit results, patient feedback), (2) define improvement goal (SMART: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound), e.g., "Increase percentage of diabetic patients with annual review from 75% to 90% by June 2026", (3) implement changes: simple Plan-Do-Study-Act (PDSA) cycle: Plan change (e.g., send appointment reminder to diabetic patients due for review), Do it (implement for 1 month), Study results (measure improvement), Act on results (keep change if working, modify if not), (4) measure progress: track metric before and after change (e.g., percentage of diabetic patients reviewed), (5) document: keep record of improvement project (goal, changes made, measurements, results), store in quality improvement file, (6) share learnings: discuss results in clinical governance meeting, communicate to staff, (7) sustain improvement: once change is working, ensure it becomes standard practice (e.g., diabetic reminder system becomes routine, not just special project), monitor monthly to prevent backsliding (if reminders stop being sent, % reviewed may drop again).

### What Can Go Wrong If Missed

- **Accreditation failure or suspension:** If clinic is non-compliant with standards and accreditation survey finds significant gaps, clinic may not be re-accredited; loss of accreditation triggers patient concern (clinic no longer "accredited"), referral bases may reduce, and clinic reputation damaged; also may affect practice valuations (accredited practices worth more), making sale difficult.
- **Regulatory compliance breach:** If clinic fails to maintain infection control standards (poor hand hygiene, inadequate sterilisation) and patient acquires healthcare-associated infection (HAI), clinic liable for patient harm; patient may sue for medical negligence (AUD 20,000–100,000+); AHPRA or OAIC may investigate; clinic may be excluded from Medicare.
- **Patient safety incident due to non-compliance:** If staff fail to complete mandatory training (e.g., CPR) and patient collapses in clinic with no one able to resuscitate, patient may die or suffer permanent brain damage; clinic liable for negligent conduct (family can sue for wrongful death, AUD 50,000–500,000+); regulatory investigation and likely criminal charges for gross negligence against responsible managers/clinicians.
- **Infection outbreak due to non-compliance:** Poor hand hygiene or inadequate sterilisation leads to cross-infection among patients or staff; if pathogen is serious (e.g., influenza, COVID-19), outbreak could spread through clinic and community; clinic identified as source; significant media attention and public backlash; patients avoid clinic (business impact); staff illness disrupts operations; potential regulatory investigation.
- **Undetected medication error or near-miss:** If medication management not audited and staff administer wrong dose or wrong patient, patient could be harmed (AUD 10,000–100,000+ in medical/legal costs); if pattern of errors exists and not detected through audit, multiple patients harmed before problem identified (much worse outcome).
- **Complaints not managed properly:** Patient complaint not responded to in timely manner or dismissively; patient escalates to external ombudsman (Health Complaints Commissioner in Victoria); external investigation is time-consuming and costly (AUD 5,000–20,000+ in management time, legal advice); may result in adverse finding against clinic, further damaging reputation.
- **Quality improvement not implemented:** Data shows problem (e.g., high patient non-attendance at follow-up), but no action taken; problem persists year after year, leading to poor patient outcomes (missed diagnoses, uncontrolled chronic disease); potential patient safety incidents and complaints; clinic's quality metrics remain poor compared to peers.
