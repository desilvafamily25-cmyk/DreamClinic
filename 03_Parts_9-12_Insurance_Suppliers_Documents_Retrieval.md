# PART 9 — INSURANCE AND RISK MAP

## 9.1 Insurance Cover Requirements and Checklist

### 9.1.1 Public Liability Insurance

**What It Covers:**
- Third-party bodily injury or property damage occurring at the clinic
- Patient sues clinic for injury (e.g., slip on wet floor, allergic reaction to vaccine)
- Visitor injury (contractor, supplier, family member)
- Damage to building/fixtures caused by clinic operations
- Legal defence costs
- Excludes: gross negligence, intentional acts, professional negligence (covered under PI)

**Typical Cover:**
- $10–20M aggregate (most GPs carry $10M minimum, $20M preferred)
- Excess typically $500–$2,500 per claim
- Annual premium: $800–$2,500 depending on claims history and clinic size

**Who Should Hold It:**
- Clinic entity (Pty Ltd or partnership) must be named insured
- If subletting rooms, pathology may hold separate cover for their area
- Individual GPs do NOT hold clinic PL cover (clinic entity does)

**Evidence Document Needed:**
- Certificate of Currency (CofC) showing:
  - Clinic name and address
  - Policy number
  - Effective dates (from/to)
  - Coverage limits ($10M, $20M)
  - Excess amount
  - Insurer name and contact
- Original policy document (kept with insurance file)
- Claims history declaration (completed during quote process)

**Renewal Considerations:**
- Renews annually (often in calendar months: Jan, Feb, Mar)
- Insurance broker should send renewal 30–45 days before expiry
- Review claims history each renewal (impacts premium)
- If clinic has made claims, expect premium increase
- Shop around every 2–3 years; brokers often offer discounts for first-year new customers
- Document any changes to clinic (staff, procedures, seating capacity) that affect underwriting

**Operational Risk If Missing:**
- **CRITICAL**: Uninsured exposure. Single patient injury claim could bankrupt clinic.
- Unable to lease space (landlord typically requires evidence of PL)
- Unable to obtain professional indemnity (insurer will ask for PL proof)
- Professional bodies (AMA, RACGP) require PL as condition of membership
- Reputational damage if patient discovers clinic uninsured

### 9.1.2 Professional Indemnity Insurance (Clinic-Level)

**What It Covers:**
- Clinic's liability for professional negligence, misdiagnosis, incorrect treatment
- Example: patient sues because GP missed cancer diagnosis
- Patient privacy breach (confidentiality violation)
- Breach of duty of care
- Legal defence and settlement costs
- Excludes: GP's personal PI policy (see below), intentional misconduct

**Clinic-Level vs. Individual GP PI:**
- **Clinic-level PI**: covers clinic entity and all GPs working at clinic (provided they are employees or approved contractors)
- **Individual GP PI**: each GP holds personal cover (typically required by indemnity insurers)
- Both are necessary; they work together

**Typical Cover:**
- $5–10M cover per claim, $10–20M aggregate (clinic-level)
- Individual GP PI: $1–5M (employer will specify minimum)
- Excess: $500–$5,000 (clinic-level)
- Clinic premium: $3,000–$8,000 p.a. (depends on claims history, practitioner numbers, specialties)

**Who Should Hold It:**
- Clinic entity (Pty Ltd or partnership)
- Certificate of Currency must list clinic, all employed GPs, and any contractor GPs
- Each contractor GP must show their personal PI certificate on file

**Evidence Document Needed:**
- Clinic-level Certificate of Currency (as for PL)
- Individual GP PI certificates (one per GP, must be current)
- Policy document (clinic-level) showing covered practitioners and exclusions
- Underwriting questionnaire (completed at inception; must be honest about claims history)
- GP Indemnity Insurance (GPII) or similar provider documentation

**Renewal Considerations:**
- Renews annually; broker should alert 30–45 days before
- Clinic must update insurer if:
  - New GP joins clinic
  - GP leaves
  - Claims are made (must notify even if claim is small)
  - Clinic changes address
- Previous claims history will affect renewal premium
- If no claims for 3+ years, may qualify for discount ("claims-free reduction")
- Individual GPs' PI status must be verified at renewal (cannot renew clinic PI without confirmed GP coverage)

**Operational Risk If Missing:**
- **CRITICAL**: Single patient claim could destroy clinic finances and reputation
- Professional bodies require PI as condition of practising
- Unable to join accreditation schemes (RACGP Blue Book, etc.)
- Patients increasingly ask to see PI proof before treatment
- Lender will not provide finance without PI evidence
- Reputational damage; signals negligence concern

### 9.1.3 Business Insurance / Business Pack

**What It Covers (Typical Bundle):**
- Loss of business income if clinic forced to close (e.g., fire, flood)
- Reimbursement of lost revenue during shutdown period
- Covers fixed costs: rent, salaries, utilities
- Duration of cover: typically 3–12 months depending on contract

**Alternative Coverage:**
- Some policies bundle: property damage + loss of profits + public liability
- Standalone "all-risk" policies for non-traditional clinics
- Small business packages often cheaper than separate policies

**Typical Cover:**
- Income indemnity: $10,000–$30,000/month (depends on clinic turnover)
- Waiting period: 7–14 days (clinic cannot claim for first week of closure)
- Duration: 12–24 months from start of claim
- Excess: $500–$2,500
- Premium: $1,000–$4,000 p.a.

**Who Should Hold It:**
- Clinic entity (not individual GPs)
- Critical if clinic is sole trading location (loss of premises = loss of income)

**Evidence Document Needed:**
- Certificate of Currency
- Policy document showing coverage period
- Last 2 years financial statements (to establish legitimate income claim)
- Policy schedule with coverage limits and excess

**Renewal Considerations:**
- Renews annually
- If clinic revenue increases >10%, must notify insurer (may affect premium)
- Review waiting period and cover duration annually
- If clinic has not moved/expanded, renewal should be straightforward

**Operational Risk If Missing:**
- If fire/flood forces closure, clinic has no income for 3–6 months while rebuilding
- Staff must still be paid (or retrenched, creating legal risk)
- Lease rent still accrues (landlord not forgiving)
- Clinic may never reopen if unable to afford interim costs
- Less critical than PL/PI but still important

### 9.1.4 Building and Contents Insurance (If Clinic Owns or Lease Requires)

**What It Covers:**
- Building structure damage (fire, flood, storm, vandalism)
- Contents: medical equipment, computers, furniture
- Fixtures and fittings (if clinic-owned)
- Excludes: damage from faulty maintenance, wear and tear

**Ownership Scenarios:**
- **Clinic owns building**: clinic must hold full building + contents cover
- **Clinic leases**: lease usually requires clinic to hold contents cover; landlord holds building cover (verify in lease)
- **Clinic subleases room**: pathology or other tenant may hold building cover; clinic holds contents for own equipment

**Typical Cover:**
- Building: $500,000–$2,000,000 (depends on premises size and location)
- Contents: $100,000–$500,000 (medical equipment, computers, furniture)
- Excess: $500–$2,500
- Premium: $2,000–$8,000 p.a. (combined)

**Who Should Hold It:**
- **Building**: clinic (if owner) or landlord (if leased)
- **Contents**: clinic (always)
- If clinic owns building, both must be clinic's responsibility

**Evidence Document Needed:**
- Certificate of Currency (contents)
- Full policy document
- Building inventory/asset register (list of items insured, with values)
- Proof of building ownership (if clinic owns) or copy of lease (showing insurance requirement)
- Previous claims history

**Renewal Considerations:**
- Renews annually
- If clinic purchases major equipment (diagnostic machine, etc.), notify insurer immediately
- If renovations increase building value, update cover
- Post-renovation, get building revaluation (may trigger premium increase)
- Review inventory annually to ensure all items are covered

**Operational Risk If Missing:**
- If fire/flood destroys equipment and furniture, clinic cannot reopen without capital injection
- If clinic owns building and it's damaged, lender may call mortgage (if mortgaged)
- Landlord may take legal action if lease required building cover and clinic failed to obtain it
- Patient records may be destroyed (compliance risk)
- Less critical than PL/PI but serious impact on continuity

### 9.1.5 Workers Compensation / WorkSafe Victoria (Mandatory)

**What It Covers:**
- Employee injury or illness arising from work
- Medical expenses, rehabilitation costs
- Wage replacement (up to 100% of pre-injury wage)
- Death benefits (to dependents)
- Excludes: intentional self-harm, gross misconduct

**Mandatory Coverage:**
- **If clinic has ANY employees, WorkSafe coverage is MANDATORY by law** (WorkSafe Victoria Act 1958)
- Applies to: GPs (if employees), nurses, admin staff, reception
- Does NOT apply to: independent contractors (separate arrangement required)
- Failure to hold cover: fines up to $300,000, legal liability for uninsured claims

**Premium Calculation (WorkSafe Victoria):**
- Based on: payroll amount + claims history + industry classification
- Industry classification for general practice: typically "GP services" (mid-risk)
- Premium rate: approximately 0.5–2.5% of payroll (varies by history)
- Example: clinic with $500,000 payroll and 0.8% rate = $4,000 p.a. premium

**Who Should Hold It:**
- Clinic entity (cannot be held by individual GP)
- Clinic must register with WorkSafe as employer
- Can be sourced through: WorkSafe directly, private insurer (Allianz, QBE, etc.), or broker

**Evidence Document Needed:**
- WorkSafe Certificate of Currency (CofC) showing:
  - Clinic name, ABN, address
  - Policy number
  - Effective dates
  - Employer classification
  - Premium amount
- WorkSafe registration certificate (proving clinic registered as employer)
- Payroll records (to verify premium calculation)
- Claims history statement

**Renewal Considerations:**
- Renews annually (typically Sept–June financial year for WorkSafe)
- Premium recalculated based on updated payroll
- If clinic makes claim, premium may increase next year
- If clinic has claims-free record, may qualify for discount ("good history discount")
- Must update payroll estimate if hiring additional staff mid-year (may adjust premium)

**Operational Risk If Missing:**
- **LEGAL BREACH**: operating without WorkSafe cover is illegal
- WorkSafe can audit and issue compliance notice (cost $2,000–$10,000+ to rectify)
- If uninsured employee injured, clinic has unlimited personal liability
- Employee can sue clinic for full damages (medical costs + lost wages + pain/suffering)
- Cannot operate clinic if discovered without cover
- Professional bodies may cancel membership/accreditation

### 9.1.6 Cyber Insurance (Increasingly Essential for Healthcare)

**What It Covers:**
- Data breach (ransomware, hacking, malware)
- Privacy breach notification costs (legal fees, customer notification, credit monitoring)
- Loss of electronic records / data recovery
- Business interruption due to cyber attack
- Extortion / ransom demands (may include ransom payment, depending on policy)
- Third-party liability (e.g., clinic data breach affects patients)
- Does NOT cover: user error, credential compromise without breach

**Why It Matters for GP Clinics:**
- Electronic health records (EHR) contain sensitive patient data: names, DOB, medical history, contact details
- Data breaches increasingly targeted at healthcare (ransomware gangs)
- GDPR and Australian Privacy Act require notification of breaches (costs $5,000–$50,000+)
- Patient records loss could force clinic closure
- Reputation damage if breach disclosed

**Typical Cover:**
- Data breach response: $50,000–$500,000
- Privacy liability: $500,000–$2,000,000
- Business interruption: $10,000–$50,000/month
- Cyber extortion: $100,000–$1,000,000
- Excess: $500–$5,000
- Premium: $1,500–$5,000 p.a. (depends on clinic size, security practices)

**Who Should Hold It:**
- Clinic entity (not individual GPs)
- Must be in clinic's name, listing all staff with access to patient data

**Evidence Document Needed:**
- Certificate of Currency
- Policy document showing coverage limits
- Underwriting questionnaire (security practices, backup systems, staff training)
- Proof of cyber security measures (firewalls, antivirus, backup procedures)

**Renewal Considerations:**
- Renews annually
- Must disclose any security incidents or data loss events (even if not reportable)
- Insurer may ask for proof of security updates (if systems over 3 years old)
- If clinic adopts new EHR system, notify insurer (may affect premium)
- Premium may increase if security practices not maintained

**Operational Risk If Missing:**
- Ransomware attack could force clinic closure for weeks
- Patient data breach could cost $50,000–$500,000 in notification + remediation
- Reputation damage, patient loss, potential regulatory investigation
- Critical for any clinic storing patient data electronically (essentially all modern clinics)

### 9.1.7 Management Liability and Directors & Officers (D&O) Insurance (If Company Structure)

**What It Covers:**
- Directors' and officers' personal liability
- Employment practices liability (wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment claims)
- Statutory liability (Health & Safety breaches, privacy law breaches)
- Crime insurance (employee theft, fraud)
- Crisis management (reputation repair, legal advice)

**Applies If:**
- Clinic is Pty Ltd or similar company (not if sole trader or partnership)
- Directors exposed to personal liability under Corporations Act, Health & Safety Act
- Staff employed (employment practices claims possible)

**Typical Cover:**
- D&O limit: $500,000–$2,000,000
- Employment practices: $500,000–$1,000,000
- Crime/fraud: $100,000–$500,000
- Excess: $500–$5,000
- Premium: $2,000–$5,000 p.a.

**Who Should Hold It:**
- Clinic Pty Ltd (entity, not individual directors)
- Certificate should list all directors

**Evidence Document Needed:**
- Certificate of Currency
- Policy document
- Company constitution / shareholders agreement
- Details of all directors (names, roles)
- Claims history

**Renewal Considerations:**
- Renews annually
- If director changes, notify insurer
- If company structure changes (e.g., new shareholder), may need fresh underwriting
- Annual directors' meetings should address D&O risk (shows due diligence)

**Operational Risk If Missing:**
- Director personally liable for employment claims (e.g., wrongful dismissal) up to full claim amount
- No protection against statutory liability fines
- If clinic embezzles or staff theft occurs, no crime cover
- Less critical than PL/PI/WorkSafe but important for director protection

### 9.1.8 Glass, Equipment, and Business Interruption (Specialist Add-Ons)

**Glass Insurance:**
- Covers glass door/window breakage at clinic
- Excess typically $200–$500
- Often bundled with property/contents
- Not usually standalone (check main contents policy)

**Equipment Breakdown:**
- Covers medical equipment failure (ECG machine, blood pressure monitors, autoclave)
- Covers repair/replacement costs
- May include emergency repair callout costs
- Premium: $500–$2,000 p.a. (depends on equipment value)
- Excess: $500–$2,000
- Often optional add-on to contents policy

**Business Interruption (Advanced):**
- Extends loss-of-income cover beyond standard policy
- Covers additional costs during shutdown (e.g., temporary premises rental, staff retraining)
- Premium: $500–$2,000 p.a.
- Excess: $1,000–$5,000

**Who Should Hold It:**
- Clinic entity
- Glass/equipment add-ons typically part of contents policy

**Evidence Document Needed:**
- Certificate of Currency (showing add-ons listed)
- Equipment register (list of items covered with serial numbers and values)
- Maintenance records (insurers may ask for proof of regular servicing)

**Operational Risk If Missing:**
- Equipment breakdown could force temporary closure (if critical device fails)
- May be relatively minor risk if equipment is standard/replaceable quickly
- Less critical than core PL/PI/WorkSafe

### 9.1.9 Locum Insurance (If Clinic Uses Locum GPs)

**What It Covers:**
- Locum GP PI if locum is contractor (not employee)
- Clinic liability for locum's actions (if locum on clinic's insurance)
- Locum sickness benefit (if locum has own cover)

**Locum Scenarios:**
1. **Locum employed by clinic**: clinic's PI and WorkSafe covers locum
2. **Locum contractor with own PI**: locum's PI covers their work; clinic remains liable (should require copy of locum's PI certificate)
3. **Locum via agency**: agency holds PI; clinic may hold secondary liability cover

**Typical Locum PI Arrangements:**
- Medical Defence Organisation (MDO) offers locum PI (standalone)
- Medishield offers locum cover
- RACGP group schemes
- Cost: $300–$800 p.a. for locum with own PI
- Clinic should require proof of coverage

**Who Should Hold It:**
- **If locum employee**: clinic's main PI covers them (listed on CofC)
- **If locum contractor**: locum should hold own PI

**Evidence Document Needed:**
- Locum's PI certificate (if contractor)
- Locum engagement contract (stating who holds what insurance)
- Clinic's updated PI certificate (if locum is employee/listed on clinic cover)

**Renewal Considerations:**
- If using locums regularly, ensure they have active PI
- Request annual proof of locum PI (copy of CofC)
- If locum's PI lapses, clinic cannot use them (breach of professional standards)
- If clinic employs locum long-term, move to clinic payroll/WorkSafe (cheaper than managing individual PI)

**Operational Risk If Missing:**
- If locum contractor uninsured and makes error, clinic is liable
- Professional bodies may suspend clinic if locums uninsured
- Patient harm could result in uninsured claim against clinic
- Less critical if locums are rare; critical if clinic relies on locums

## 9.2 Three Top-10 Risk Registers

### 9.2.1 Top 10 Clinic Operational Risks

| # | Risk | Potential Impact | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|------|------------------|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | **Key GP departure** (principal retires, moves interstate) | Immediate loss of billing revenue, patient loss, staff morale drop, clinic may not be viable | Medium-High | Succession plan, gradual handover (6+ months), contract terms with notice period, identify/train replacement early |
| 2 | **Billing system failure** (PRODA account locked, Medicare audit, claiming errors) | Revenue drop 20–50%, cash flow crisis within 2 weeks, staff unable to process claims | Medium | Monthly audit of billing, backup claiming methods, relationship with practice manager, regular PRODA checks |
| 3 | **Reception understaffing** (staff turnover, leave, sickness) | Missed appointments, patient anger, backlogs, clinic reputation damaged, GPs idle | High | Cross-train staff, maintain 1.5× coverage (if need 1 FTE, hire 1.5), clear handover procedures, temp agency contact |
| 4 | **Cold chain breach** (vaccine storage failure, temperature logger not monitored) | Vaccines spoiled, unusable stock $2,000–$5,000, potential patient harm if used, ATAGI investigation | Medium | Daily temperature checks (documented), backup freezer, 3-month temperature logs, monthly inventory audit |
| 5 | **IT system failure** (EHR down, server crash, internet outage) | Unable to access patient records for 4–8 hours, clinic grinds to halt, patient safety risk | Medium | Cloud-based backup EHR, redundant internet (main + 4G), offline patient list, IT vendor with 1-hour SLA |
| 6 | **Key person dependency** (one admin/nurse indispensable; if leave, clinic struggles) | Staff burnout, knowledge loss, inability to cover absences, high turnover | High | Cross-train all roles, document procedures, rotate responsibilities, no single-person sign-off on critical tasks |
| 7 | **Cash flow gap** (large supplier invoice due but cash account has $2,000) | Unable to pay staff, suppliers demand payment, clinic may need emergency finance | Medium | Monthly cash flow forecast, maintain 4–8 weeks operating expense buffer, pre-negotiated line of credit |
| 8 | **Accreditation lapse** (RACGP Blue Book expires unnoticed, QIP audit overdue) | Cannot bill Medicare, lose patients, regulatory non-compliance, fines | Low-Medium | Calendar reminders (Excel file), practice manager responsibility, annual accreditation review meeting |
| 9 | **Lease issues** (landlord serves notice, lease termination dispute, rent rise) | Forced relocation, loss of clinic assets/fit-out, revenue loss during move | Low-Medium | Know lease terms (review quarterly), maintain landlord relationship, document any disputes in writing, lease legal review |
| 10 | **Complaint escalation** (patient complaint to Health Complaints Commissioner, media attention) | Regulatory investigation, reputation damage, staff morale drop, potential legal claim | Medium | Complaint handling policy, document all incidents, respond promptly to complaints, external advisor on retainer |

## 9.3 Insurance Broker and Renewal Management

### 9.3.1 Selecting an Insurance Broker

- **Specialist healthcare brokers** (e.g., Gallagher, Aon, Willis Towers Watson healthcare divisions)
- Must have experience with GP clinics (not just general SME insurance)
- Should hold professional indemnity themselves (shows credibility)
- Ask for references from 3–5 existing GP clinic clients

### 9.3.2 Broker Responsibilities (Should Be in Engagement Letter)

- Annual renewal reminders (30–45 days before expiry)
- Claims notification and support
- Annual premium review and market shopping
- Update insurer if clinic circumstances change
- Quarterly certificate of currency verification
- Annual risk review meeting

# PART 10 — PATHOLOGY, SUPPLIERS, AND EXTERNAL PARTNERS

## 10.1 Pathology Arrangement

### 10.1.1 Overview of Major Victorian Pathology Providers

**Dorevitch Pathology**
- Largest independent pathology in Victoria
- Phlebotomists on-site at most clinics (or clinic staff collect)
- Results interface: MEDI3, BEST, other EHR systems
- Room rental: typically $500–$1,500/month or commission-based
- Service areas: inner Melbourne preferred; outer suburbs slower
- Contact: Regional Manager (clinic should have mobile/email)

**Melbourne Pathology (Sonic Healthcare)**
- Large national chain, Sonic Healthcare-owned
- Results integration with various EHRs
- Room rental or commission (5–8% of collections)
- Phlebotomy staff or clinic staff supported
- Service areas: metro and regional Victoria
- Contact: Regional Account Manager

**Australian Clinical Labs (ACL)**
- Growing private provider
- Competitive pricing, modern automation
- Results integration available
- Room rental or commission
- Expanding metro presence
- Contact: Practice Manager/Sales

**Other Pathology Options**
- Darbyshire Pathology (smaller, inner metro)
- IMVS PathCentre (SA-based but some VIC presence)
- Hospital pathology services (limited to public GPs)

### 10.1.2 Typical Pathology Service Agreement Structure

**What Should Be In Writing:**

1. **Pathology Arrangement Type**
   - On-site phlebotomist (pathology provides staff)
   - Clinic staff collect (pathology provides training, supplies)
   - Off-site collection (patients go to pathology lab)

2. **Financial Terms**
   - Option A: Room rental ($500–$1,500/month, clinic gets 100% of rebates)
   - Option B: Commission-based (clinic gets 50–70% of rebate, pathology gets 30–50%)
   - Option C: Hybrid (base rental + commission for volume above threshold)
   - Payment terms: monthly invoice, due on invoice date or 14 days

3. **Service Levels**
   - Turnaround time for results (24–48 hours typical)
   - Availability of urgent/same-day results
   - Phlebotomy hours (8 am–5 pm typical, extended hours available)
   - Holiday/public holiday cover
   - Who covers pathology if phlebotomist sick (temporary staff or clinic closes)

4. **Equipment and Supplies**
   - Specimen collection containers: who provides and pays
   - Labels and request forms: who provides
   - Storage equipment (fridge, freezer): who provides, who pays power/maintenance
   - Sharps disposal bins: cost responsibility
   - Telephone/fax: who pays

5. **Results Integration**
   - EHR system connection (API, HL7 interface)
   - Who pays for interface development/support (typically clinic pays first-time, pathology pays ongoing)
   - Contingency if interface fails (fax/email backup)
   - Turnaround time for results availability in EHR

6. **Quality and Governance**
   - Pathology quality assurance (NATA accreditation, external QA)
   - Clinic responsibility for correct specimen labeling/collection
   - What happens if specimen rejected (re-collection, refund)
   - Confidentiality/privacy obligations (specimens, patient data)

7. **Liability**
   - Pathology liable for specimen transport/loss
   - Clinic liable for specimen collection errors
   - Insurance (WorkSafe if phlebotomist injured on-site)
   - Professional indemnity (pathology carries for interpretation errors)

8. **Term and Termination**
   - Contract duration (typically 12–24 months)
   - Notice period to terminate (typically 30–90 days)
   - What happens to patient specimens if terminated
   - Migration of results history

9. **Exclusions**
   - Which tests not available through this provider
   - Referrals to other labs (GPs may order from others without restriction)
   - Private/direct-to-patient testing

### 10.1.3 Pathology Service Agreement Checklist

**Before Signing:**
- [ ] Confirm pathology provider is NATA-accredited
- [ ] Confirm EHR integration available for clinic's system (ask for trial)
- [ ] Get specimen collection training (in-house staff)
- [ ] Review payment terms (rent or commission) against clinic projections
- [ ] Confirm turnaround times and emergency availability
- [ ] Review liability/insurance clauses
- [ ] Confirm cold chain/storage requirements match clinic capacity
- [ ] Get 3-year cost projection (budget planning)
- [ ] Assign one staff member as pathology liaison (order issues, interface problems, training)

**Operational Contacts:**
- Pathology phlebotomist (mobile/email)
- Pathology results support (if results not received)
- Pathology account manager (billing, service issues)
- Pathology IT support (EHR interface issues)

### 10.1.4 Monitoring Pathology Performance

**Monthly Review:**
- Results turnaround: Are 95% of results back within 24–48 hours? (Track a sample)
- Error rate: How many specimens rejected or results incorrect? (Should be <1%)
- Integration success: Are 95%+ of results auto-populating in EHR? (Check EHR logs)
- Cost per collection: Is cost per rebate in line with contract? (Audit quarterly)

**Quarterly Business Review (Recommended):**
- Meeting with pathology account manager
- Review volume trends and revenue share
- Discuss any service issues
- Plan for seasonal volume (flu season, etc.)
- Negotiate price if volume has changed significantly

**Annual Review:**
- Full contract review (terms, pricing, notice periods)
- Consider market alternatives (get quote from competitor)
- Plan for renewal or renegotiation

### 10.2.2 Vaccine Suppliers

**National Immunisation Program (NIP) Vaccines:**
- **Supplied FREE by Victoria State Government** (via Medicare/Pharmacy Guild)
- Clinic registers with Immunise Victoria to receive NIP vaccines
- Delivery: fortnightly or monthly distribution
- No cost to clinic
- Cold chain requirements: clinic must maintain storage (2–8°C)

**Private Vaccine Suppliers (For Non-NIP Vaccines):**
- **ATAGI recommendations** (RSV, pneumococcal for private patients, etc.)
- **Private vaccine suppliers:**
  - Bupa Health Pharmacy
  - Pharmacare
  - Vyperion Healthcare
  - International travel clinics (Yellow Fever, etc.)
- **Cost:** Private vaccines $20–$100 per dose depending on vaccine
- **Ordering:** Usually online portal, email, or phone
- **Delivery:** 2–7 days
- **Cold chain:** Must maintain storage at 2–8°C (same as NIP)

**Cold Chain Management:**
- **Critical for vaccine efficacy and safety**
- Temperature logger: records temperature every 15–30 minutes
- Backup freezer or ice pack system (if main fridge fails)
- Daily temperature checks (documented log)
- Monthly review of temperature logs (should never drop below 2°C or exceed 8°C)
- If breach detected: contact supplier, ATAGI, and patients potentially affected
- Incident report to AHPRA if patients given compromised vaccines

**Vaccine Inventory Management:**
- Stock rotation (FIFO: first in, first out)
- Expiry date tracking (many vaccines 12–24 month shelf life)
- Usage forecasting (especially flu season: March–Sept build-up)
- Reconciliation: monthly count vs. records (should match)
- Batch tracking (in case of recall)

**Supplier Agreements for Private Vaccines:**
- Pricing: per-dose cost
- Returns: can unused vaccines be returned for credit? (typically 30-day window)
- Payment terms: usually 30 days net
- Delivery cost: free over $500? or per-order fee?
- Emergency supply: can clinic get urgent delivery if stock runs out?

### 10.3.2 IT Vendor Service Agreement

1. **Support Services**

   - Response time: 1 hour for critical (EHR down), 4 hours for urgent, 24 hours for routine
   - Remote support: via TeamViewer or similar
   - On-site support: available? costs? travel time included?

2. **Hardware Support**
   - Workstation warranty: 3 years with next-business-day replacement

   - EHR software updates: included or separate?
   - Operating system patches/updates: included
   - Antivirus/security software: included
   - License management: tracking, renewal reminders

4. **Backup and Disaster Recovery**
   - Daily backup schedule (when, frequency)
   - Backup location: cloud provider? local? both?

5. **Security and Compliance**
   - Firewalls: configured to meet health/security standards
   - Intrusion detection: monitoring for suspicious activity
   - Encryption: data in transit (HTTPS) and at rest (if cloud)
   - Access controls: user account management, password policies

6. **Onboarding and Training**
   - Staff training: on clinic software, basic IT support
   - Onboarding: documentation of system setup, access credentials

7. **Escalation Process**

## 10.4 Telephony and Internet

### 10.4.1 Internet Requirements

**Bandwidth:**
- Minimum: 10 Mbps download, 2 Mbps upload (for small clinic, 1–3 GPs)
- Recommended: 20+ Mbps (to handle multiple concurrent calls, video calls, EHR sync)
- Upload critical: if clinic sends/receives large files (scans, images), need 5+ Mbps upload

**Reliability:**
- **99.9% uptime required** (less than 43 minutes/month downtime acceptable)
- Must have backup connection (4G modem) if primary internet fails
- NBN (National Broadband Network) if available in area (most reliable)
- ADSL2+ as backup if NBN not available

**Medical-Grade Requirements:**
- Encryption: HTTPS for all patient data transmission
- Firewall: standard, protects against external threats
- DDoS protection: recommended (cybersecurity insurance may require)

**Major Providers in Victoria:**
- **Telstra** (largest coverage, most expensive)
- **TP/Vodafone** (competitive pricing, good coverage)
- **NBN Co** (if available: most reliable, $60–$120/month)
- **4G providers:** Telstra, Optus, Vodafone (backup only, usually $30–$80/month)

### 10.4.2 Telephony System

**Clinic Phones:**
- **Dedicated clinic phone line** (landline from Telstra/TP, ~$20–$40/month)
- **VoIP system** (cloud-based, more flexible, $30–$100/month)
- **Mobile for on-call** (separate line for after-hours GP, ~$50–$100/month)

**Integration:**
- EHR integration: caller ID displays in EHR (useful for answering calls)
- Call recording: optional (if recorded, patients must be informed)
- Voicemail/auto-attendant: "Press 1 for appointments, Press 2 for results," etc.
- Call waiting: multiple calls can be held

**Service Agreement Essentials:**
- Number of lines: 1–3 typical
- Cost: per-line or bundled with internet
- Contract term: typically 12 months
- Notice period: 30 days to change provider
- Support: provider should have helpdesk for outages

### 10.4.3 Cost Structure

**Typical Monthly Communications Cost (Internet + Phones):**
- Internet (NBN or DSL): $60–$120
- Primary phone line: $20–$40
- Additional lines: $15–$25 each
- Mobile (if clinic-supplied): $40–$100
- VoIP system (if used): $30–$100
- **Total typical: $150–$400/month**

**Redundancy Setup (Recommended):**
- Primary: NBN internet + Telstra landline
- Backup: 4G modem (USB, portable)
- Cost for backup: $30–$80/month (adds to total)

## 11.4 Storage Best Practices

### 11.4.1 Cloud Storage Platform Selection

**Recommended Options for GP Clinic:**

1. **Microsoft SharePoint (part of Microsoft 365)**
   - Pros: Integration with Office 365, versions control, granular permissions
   - Cons: Higher cost ($10–$20/user/month), steeper learning curve
   - Best for: Multi-clinic operations, advanced version control needs

2. **Google Drive (part of Google Workspace)**
   - Pros: Easy sharing, lower cost ($6–$14/user/month), intuitive interface
   - Cons: Less granular permissions than SharePoint, fewer advanced features
   - Best for: Single clinic, team of 5–10, budget-conscious

3. **Dropbox Business**
   - Pros: Excellent syncing, good security, file versioning
   - Cons: Higher cost ($15–$20/user/month for Business tier)
   - Best for: Mobile/remote staff, lots of file access from multiple locations

**Recommendation for Single Clinic:** Google Drive (lower cost, adequate features)
**Recommendation for Multi-Clinic:** SharePoint (better control, scale-ability)
